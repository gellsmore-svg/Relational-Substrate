The central question is:

```text
How should light propagation, refraction, reflection, absorption, and scattering be described within AMS without pretending that AMS has proven an invisible substrate?
```

The answer is not proof.

The answer is coherence.

AMS treats light as a real transient coherent propagation through ordered runtime physical structure. It then asks whether the established behaviour of optical systems can be organised through a disciplined route grammar without replacing known optics, denying measurement, or inventing arbitrary exceptions.

## Epistemic Guardrail

AMS does not replace established optical physics.

It uses established optics as constraint.

The following are treated as ordinary physical facts that AMS must respect:

- Snell's law
- Fresnel reflection and transmission coefficients
- wavelength-dependent refractive index
- complex refractive index for metals and absorbing media
- extinction coefficient
- skin depth
- absorption bands
- scattering from disorder or roughness
- plasma-frequency behaviour in metals
- interband transitions contributing to colour
- surface reflection at refractive-index boundaries

AMS does not claim:

```text
because light can be redescribed in route language, the substrate has been proven.
```

It claims only:

```text
the same optical behaviours can be coherently reread as route continuation, route delay, route return, route capture, or route fragmentation within an ordered substrate ontology.
```

```text
the ordered non-expressive ground state of the substrate
```

So darkness is not the cause of light in an agentic or mystical sense. It is the ordered non-expressive condition that makes luminous expression possible.

## Route Grammar

AMS translates optical behaviour into route language.

The main route outcomes are:

```text
R = returned route
T = transmitted route
A = absorbed route
S = scattered route
```

For any real optical encounter:

```text
R + T + A + S = 1
```

AMS route mapping:

```text
reflection   -> route return
transmission -> route continuation
refraction   -> coherent route delay and reseating
absorption   -> route capture into loss
scattering   -> route fragmentation
```

This lets AMS compare cases without treating every optical event as the same kind of substrate response.

```text
n measures coherent route delay in the medium.
```

```text
boundary reseating plus coherent route delay across media with different optical route conditions
```

## Snell and Fresnel as Constraints

At an ideal boundary between two transparent dielectrics:

```text
n1 sin(theta_i) = n2 sin(theta_t)
```

AMS does not alter that relation.

It reads it as:

```text
the transmitted route must reseat into the second medium under that medium's delay geometry.
```

Fresnel reflection and transmission are also retained.

Conventional statement:

```text
part of the electromagnetic wave reflects,
part transmits.
```

AMS statement:

```text
the boundary divides the incident route into a returned route and a reseated transmitted route according to delay contrast, angle, polarisation, boundary quality, and defect burden.
```

## Glass: Admits and Delays

The strongest worked case is fused silica.

Fused silica is useful because it is a clean high-purity `SiO2` glass with:

- strong amorphous network closure;
- broad optical transparency;
- low metallic impurity burden when properly manufactured;
- well-characterised absorption bands;
- surface reflection governed by index contrast.

AMS core description:

```text
fused silica transmits light because its Si-O closure field supports coherent optical handoff without converting most of the route into absorption or scattering.
```

The route sequence is:

```text
incoming optical disturbance
-> admitted at entry surface
-> reseated into silica route field
-> delayed by polarisation-coupled network response
-> propagated through bulk
-> released at exit surface
```

Transmission is therefore not absence of interaction.

It is successful coherent handoff.

## Glass Boundary Reflection

Even clean glass reflects some light.

At normal incidence from air into fused silica:

```text
air n ~= 1.00
fused silica n ~= 1.46
R ~= ((1.00 - 1.46) / (1.00 + 1.46))^2
R ~= 0.035
```

So about 3.5 percent is reflected per clean uncoated surface.

AMS reading:

```text
even a clean glass surface has route return because delay contrast is real.
```

The boundary is not invisible to the optical route. It is just compatible enough that most of the route continues.

## Bulk Propagation Through Glass

Inside fused silica, the main AMS profile is:

```text
primary route: R1 through-route
secondary route: R2 polarisation / refractive delay
loss routes: R5 absorption, R6 scattering
obstruction: O1 ordered delay, O3 absorption, O6 scatter
dominance: D1 when clean and wavelength-compatible
grade: G_A in good optical windows
```

Fused silica is amorphous, but not optically random.

Its local `Si-O` closure is connected enough to preserve optical coherence across the bulk.

AMS formula:

```text
local Si-O closure
+ amorphous isotropy
+ low defect density
=> coherent through-route
```

This matters because AMS should not say:

```text
glass is transparent where its internal route field preserves coherence better than it captures or fragments the optical route.
```

## Metal: Rejects and Returns

The paired contrast case is metallic reflection.

Core contrast:

```text
fused silica
-> dielectric through-route with coherent delay

metal
-> conductive surface response with strong return route and shallow absorption
```

Metals reflect because the incident optical route is not admitted as a deep coherent dielectric through-route.

Instead:

```text
incident optical field
-> drives collective surface / conduction-electron response
-> induced response creates strong return route
-> small fraction enters as shallow lossy route
```

AMS reading:

```text
metallic reflection
= high boundary return capacity
+ low deep propagation admission
+ strong conductive surface reseating
+ shallow absorption / damping
```

## Complex Refractive Index and Skin Depth

For metals, conventional optics uses:

```text
N = n + i k
```

where:

```text
n = real refractive contribution
k = extinction / attenuation coefficient
```

Normal-incidence reflectance from air:

```text
R = ((n - 1)^2 + k^2) / ((n + 1)^2 + k^2)
```

When `k` is large:

```text
R -> high
```

Skin depth:

```text
delta = lambda / (4 pi k)
```

AMS reading:

```text
large k
-> strong route attenuation
-> shallow penetration
-> strong return dominance
```

The optical route does not enter deeply and continue. It mostly returns, while the admitted fraction is quickly captured or dissipated.

Important modifiers:

So the question is not only:

```text
what terminal layer is the optical route actually encountering?
```

## Copper, Silver, and Gold

Metal colour and reflectance are not treated as arbitrary exceptions.

AMS follows established physics:

- silver has very high visible reflectance across much of the visible range;
- copper and gold have wavelength-dependent absorption and reflectance because of interband transitions;
- surface oxidation or tarnish changes optical response.

AMS reading:

```text
metal identity
+ free-electron response
+ interband transition structure
+ surface condition
=> specific return / absorption / colour profile
```

This lets the framework distinguish metals without inventing a separate optical story for each one.

## Absorption and Scattering

Absorption and scattering must not be collapsed.

Absorption:

```text
route capture into internal modes, defect states, vibrational modes, electron excitation, or heat-producing dissipation
```

Scattering:

```text
route fragmentation caused by disorder, roughness, inclusions, density fluctuations, or incompatible local geometry
```

AMS shorthand:

```text
absorption -> R5/O3
scattering -> R6/O6
```

This difference matters because a material may fail optical transmission by:

- capturing the route;
- fragmenting the route;
- returning the route;
- failing to admit it;
- failing to release it cleanly.

These are not the same failure.

From the vorton perspective, optical behaviour depends on how stable material regimes interact with transient propagation.

In glass:


In metal:

```text
conductive material organisation

```

This preserves the distinction between:

- primary topology;
- transient aether structures;
- secondary material regimes;
- measurement descriptions.
