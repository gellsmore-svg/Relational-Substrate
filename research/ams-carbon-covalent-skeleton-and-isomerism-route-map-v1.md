# AMS Carbon Covalent Skeleton and Isomerism Route Map v1

## Purpose

This note maps carbon's `C1` and `C2` roles:

```text
C1: covalent molecular skeleton carrier
C2: molecular geometry differentiator
```

These are the foundation roles for carbon.

The central claim is:

> Carbon turns arrangement into molecular identity. It does this by forming stable extensible skeletons, then differentiating those skeletons through branching, rings, bond order, stereochemistry, and chirality.

## Source Anchors

The source base supports these facts:

- Carbon commonly forms four covalent bonds.
- Carbon can bond to itself, forming chains, branches, rings, and extended frameworks.
- Carbon skeletons are central to organic molecules.
- Catenation is bonding of atoms of the same element into chains or rings, and carbon is especially important for this.
- Structural isomers have the same molecular formula but different atom connectivity.
- Chain isomerism arises from branching in carbon chains.
- Stereoisomers have the same connectivity but different spatial arrangement.
- Chirality occurs when a molecule is not superimposable on its mirror image.
- Enantiomers can have identical behaviour in achiral contexts but different behaviour in chiral biological contexts.
- Amino acids, except glycine, are chiral, and biological systems strongly use one handedness.
- Carbon atoms bonded to four different substituents are common stereogenic centres.

## Mechanism 1: Catenated Skeleton Formation

Carbon can bond to itself repeatedly to form chains and rings.

AMS route:

```text
carbon atom
-> C-C covalent bond
-> extended chain/ring/branch
-> persistent molecular skeleton
```

Classification:

```text
R1/O1/D1/G_A
```

Why:

This is a strongly real route. Carbon-carbon skeletons persist well enough to support organic molecular identity.

AMS variables:

```text
B_in    = available carbon bonding sites
Q_coh   = skeleton coherence: bond order, valence satisfaction, strain tolerance
J_bound = atoms retained in covalent framework
J_trans = route extension through further bonding/functionalisation
B_out   = stable molecular skeleton
I_comp  = excessive strain, wrong valence, radical cleavage, oxidation
D       = D1
```

AMS reading:

```text
carbon skeletons are molecular route frames.
```

## Mechanism 2: Branching and Chain Isomerism

The same formula can yield straight-chain or branched molecules.

AMS route:

```text
same atom inventory
-> different C-C connectivity
-> different molecular skeleton
-> different properties
```

Classification:

```text
R1/R3/O1/O2/D1/D4/G_A/G_C depending context
```

Why:

The route is valid when the branch produces a stable identity. It becomes `R3` only when an apparent structural route is mistaken for the intended one.

AMS interpretation:

```text
branching shows that carbon identity is not composition alone.
```

Example logic:

```text
same formula
different carbon connectivity
different molecular identity
```

This is one of the strongest AMS supports for "configuration is prior to assembly." The atoms are the same; the order is different; the identity changes.

## Mechanism 3: Ring Closure

Carbon chains can close into rings, creating cyclic route constraints.

AMS route:

```text
open carbon chain
-> terminal or internal closure
-> cyclic skeleton
-> constrained geometry and altered reactivity
```

Classification:

```text
R1/R4/O1/O4/D1/D4/G_A
```

Why:

Ring closure creates a stable route but also introduces constraint. The molecule now has a different spatial and energetic field.

AMS reading:

```text
carbon rings turn linear extension into bounded molecular form.
```

Failure:

```text
excess strain -> unstable ring -> cleavage or rearrangement
```

Classification:

```text
R3/R6/O2/O6/D4/G_C
```

## Mechanism 4: Bond-Order Geometry

Carbon can form single, double, and triple bonds.

AMS route:

```text
bond order change
-> geometry change
-> rotation constraint / electron distribution change
-> molecular identity change
```

Classification:

```text
R1/R4/O1/O4/D1/D4/G_A
```

Why:

Bond order changes both structure and route potential. Single bonds allow rotation; double bonds constrain geometry and support unsaturation; triple bonds produce strong linear constraints.

AMS interpretation:

```text
bond order is a route-geometry switch.
```

This becomes important later for:

- lipid saturation/unsaturation;
- conjugated systems;
- aromaticity;
- metabolic oxidation/reduction;
- polymer geometry.

AMS route:

```text
-> distinct molecule
```

Classification:

```text
R1/O1/D1/G_A
```

This principle is fundamental to AMS.

The carbon skeleton is a clear T1B demonstration that form cannot be reduced to parts alone.

## Mechanism 6: Stereoisomerism and Chirality

Stereoisomers have the same connectivity but different spatial arrangement.

Chiral molecules are not superimposable on their mirror images. Enantiomers can behave similarly in achiral contexts but differently in chiral biological contexts.

AMS route:

```text
same atom count
same connectivity
different spatial arrangement
-> different molecular route identity in chiral fields
```

Classification:

```text
R1/R4/O1/O4/D1/D4/G_A
```

Why:

The difference is not in composition or even simple connectivity. It is in spatial configuration. Biological systems often recognise one configuration differently from another.

AMS interpretation:

```text
chirality proves that spatial order is identity-bearing.
```

Biological stress-test:

```text
amino acid handedness
enzyme active-site recognition
drug enantiomer differences
```

AMS principle:

```text
same material, same connectivity, different handedness, different route fit.
```
