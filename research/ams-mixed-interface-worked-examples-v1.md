# AMS Mixed Interface Worked Examples v1

## Purpose

This document runs the full mixed-interface diagnostic framework step by step on the strongest current benchmark cases:

- `glass -> air`
- `glass -> crystal`
- `air -> oxidised metal`

The goal is to show that the framework is now operational rather than merely well-specified.

Each example is worked through in the same order:

1. identify local zones present
2. judge which zone dominates
3. test route reality
4. identify obstruction character
5. estimate likely transition flow
6. assign whole-interface outcome

## Active Framework

Primitive variables:

- `B_in`

- `D`

- `I_comp`

Main local zones:

- Zone A: route-dominant zone
- Zone B: pocket-dominant zone
- Zone C: diffusing fringe

Main dominance modes:

- `D1` route-dominant
- `D2` pocket-dominant
- `D3` diffusion-dominant
- `D4` mixed competitive

## Worked Example 1: Glass -> Air

## Step 1: Identify Local Zones Present

Best current reading:

- Zone A is present
- Zone B is present
- Zone C is present

Why:

- some terminal release paths are genuinely real for a time
- some local regions support pocketed pseudo-release
- some outer fringe regions broaden into diffuse release

This is exactly why `glass -> air` remains the strongest release-side frontier case.

## Step 2: Judge Which Zone Dominates

Best current reading:

- no single zone dominates cleanly across the whole interface
- pocket capture is often locally strongest
- route logic is still real enough that the whole case is not simply pocket-only

Provisional dominance mode:

- `D4` mixed competitive

with frequent drift toward:

- `D2` pocket-dominant

Why:

- Zone B often captures the event before route completion wins
- but Zone A still matters enough to prevent the whole case from being reduced to pure pseudo-route logic

## Step 3: Test Route Reality

Three route forms matter here.

### Clean Release Route

Route-reality grade:

- `B`

Why:

- forward continuity is real
- directional commitment is real
- completion viability exists
- but terminal fragility keeps all of those weaker than in the stronger entry or ordered-release anchors

### Blocked Release Route

Route-reality grade:

- `C`

Why:

- the route is genuinely present
- but fails under progression

This is the clearest `R3` route form in the case.

### Pseudo-Release Loop

Route-reality grade:

- `D`

Why:

- local activity is strong
- apparent progress is high
- but completion-side viability is mostly only apparent

This is the clearest `R5` route form in the case.

## Step 4: Identify Obstruction Character

Best current reading:

- `O2`
- `O3`
- `O5`

Role of each:

- `O2` late-gate obstruction:
  - produces blocked release routes
- `O3` pocketing obstruction:
  - produces pseudo-release loops and trapped release
- `O5` diffusing obstruction:
  - produces broad degraded release and weak distributed continuation

This is the richest single obstruction mix in the whole programme.

## Step 5: Estimate Likely Transition Flow

Most likely flows:

- `A -> B`
- `B -> B`
- `B -> C`
- `D4 -> B`

Interpretation:

- a genuinely real route often begins
- pocket capture often overtakes it
- local pocket persistence is strong once capture occurs
- if coherence weakens further, pocket logic often bleeds into diffuse fringe behaviour

This makes `glass -> air` the clearest dynamic benchmark for:

- `R3 -> R5`
- and for pocket-to-diffuse degradation

## Step 6: Assign Whole-Interface Outcome

Best current whole-interface diagnosis:

- primarily `D4` mixed competitive
- with frequent local drift toward `D2` pocket-dominant

### Final Reading

`glass -> air` is best understood as a mixed release interface in which:

- route reality is real but fragile
- pocket capture is often the strongest local winner
- diffuse fringe behaviour appears when bounded local coherence weakens

### Why This Example Matters

It shows the framework handling:

- route reality
- pseudo-route logic
- obstruction mix
- dynamic transition
- mixed local dominance

all in one case.

## Worked Example 2: Glass -> Crystal

## Step 1: Identify Local Zones Present

Best current reading:

- Zone A is present strongly
- Zone B is present
- Zone C may be present weakly in poorer compatibility regions

Why:

- a genuine narrow viable handoff route can exist
- some local transition regions behave pseudo-progressively
- some weaker fringes broaden rather than sharpen compatibility

## Step 2: Judge Which Zone Dominates

Best current reading:

- cleaner compatibility regions: `D1`
- frontier compatibility regions: `D4`

So this case is not fixed to one dominance mode.

In stronger local conditions:

- route dominance wins

In frontier or mixed conditions:

- route and pocket logics compete

This is exactly why `glass -> crystal` remains the strongest compatibility frontier.

## Step 3: Test Route Reality

Three route forms matter here too.

### Viable Compatibility Route

Route-reality grade:

- `B/A` edge

Why:

- a narrow handoff route can be genuinely real
- directional commitment toward admissible crystal-style completion is present
- completion remains a live outcome
- but viability is more conditional than in simpler anchor cases

### Blocked Compatibility Route

Route-reality grade:

- `C`

Why:

- the route really exists in a meaningful sense
- narrowing and handoff progression begin
- final admissibility is not achieved

### Pseudo-Compatibility Loop

Route-reality grade:

- `D`

Why:

- narrowing-like activity appears
- but no robust admissible route stabilises

This is the clearest compatibility-side `R5` form.

## Step 4: Identify Obstruction Character

Best current reading:

- `O1`
- `O2`
- `O3`

Role of each:

- `O1` funnelling:
  - preserves one narrow viable route
- `O2` late-gate:
  - blocks a real route near admissible completion
- `O3` pocketing:
  - creates pseudo-progressive local transition zones

This is the strongest benchmark for mixed selective and pseudo-selective compatibility.

## Step 5: Estimate Likely Transition Flow

Most likely flows:

- `A -> completion`
- `A -> B`
- `D4 -> A`
- `D4 -> B`

Interpretation:

- route-led compatibility can succeed
- route-led compatibility can also fall into pocket capture
- mixed compatibility zones can resolve either back toward a real narrow route or toward pseudo-route dominance

This is the strongest benchmark for `L3` as a true cross-whole stall family.

## Step 6: Assign Whole-Interface Outcome

Best current whole-interface diagnosis:

- `D1` in cleaner selective regions
- `D4` in the strongest frontier regions

### Final Reading

`glass -> crystal` is best understood as a selective mixed interface in which:

- real narrow handoff routes can exist
- blocked near-completion is common
- pseudo-compatibility pockets can coexist with real admissible pathways

### Why This Example Matters

It proves the framework can handle:

- genuine handoff possibility
- blocked route failure
- pseudo-route competition
- mixed local dominance

without reducing the case to one flat label.

## Worked Example 3: Air -> Oxidised Metal

## Step 1: Identify Local Zones Present

Best current reading:

- Zone A is weakly present
- Zone C is strongly present
- Zone B is not usually dominant

Why:

- some route logic can survive temporarily
- but broader route degradation and fragmentation pressures dominate more than stable pocket capture

## Step 2: Judge Which Zone Dominates

Best current reading:

- mostly `D3` diffusion-dominant

Why:

- route leverage weakens broadly
- the interface does not usually preserve one strong bounded pocket strongly enough to dominate
- degraded coherence pushes the event toward scattered or fragmented continuation

## Step 3: Test Route Reality

Dominant route form:

- degraded surface route field

Route-reality grade:

- `C/D` edge

Why:

- some route appearance remains
- but forward continuity is weak
- completion-side viability is very weak
- fragmentation pressure is high

This case is one of the clearest proofs that apparent route activity is not enough for robust route reality.

## Step 4: Identify Obstruction Character

Best current reading:

- `O2`
- `O6`

Role of each:

- `O2` late-gate:
  - preserves some apparent route logic for a time
- `O6` fragmenting:
  - breaks route coherence into degraded or scattered behaviour

Weak secondary role:

- `O4` if remnants of ordered reflective bias survive locally

## Step 5: Estimate Likely Transition Flow

Most likely flows:

- `A -> C`
- `D4 -> C`

Interpretation:

- if any route logic survives, it often broadens into degraded diffusing behaviour rather than becoming a stable pocket or recovering route dominance

This makes the case very different from the glass frontiers.

## Step 6: Assign Whole-Interface Outcome

Best current whole-interface diagnosis:

- `D3` diffusion-dominant

### Final Reading

`air -> oxidised metal` is best understood as a degradation case in which:

- some route logic remains temporarily
- but fragmentation and diffusion pressures dominate the whole-interface outcome

### Why This Example Matters

It provides the strongest contrast case against the glass frontiers.

It shows the framework can diagnose:

- mixed but still route-relevant cases

and distinguish them from:

- diffusion-dominant degradation regimes

## Strongest Practical Shortcuts

If:

- route reality is strong
- pocketing is weak
- diffusion weak

then think:

- `D1`

If:

- local pocket capture is strongest
- route reality weakens into pseudo-progression

then think:

- `D2`

If:

- route and pocket logics both weaken
- fringe broadening dominates

then think:

- `D3`

If:

- route, pocket, and/or fringe remain live together
- with unstable competition

then think:

- `D4`
