# AMS Carbon Delocalised Electronic and Aromatic Route Map v1

## Purpose

This note maps carbon's `C7` role:

```text
C7: delocalised electronic architecture carrier
```

The previous carbon maps established:

```text
Carbon turns arrangement into molecular identity.
Carbon carries identity across scale.
Carbon hydrocarbon architecture makes biological boundaries possible.
Carbon fixation admits environmental carbon into living construction.
Carbon routes skeletons through metabolism and redox extraction.
```

This file maps how carbon carries delocalised electronic order through aromatic rings, conjugated chains, quinones, pigments, nucleobases, graphite, and graphene.

The central claim is:

> Carbon does not only build local skeletons. In sp2 and conjugated forms it can distribute electronic behaviour across rings, chains, and sheets, creating stable aromatic identity, colour, redox mediation, molecular recognition, and electrical conduction.

## Source Anchors

The source base supports these facts:

- Aromaticity is associated with cyclic electron delocalisation and enhanced thermodynamic stability relative to suitable non-aromatic analogues.
- Benzene is planar, cyclic, sp2-hybridised, and has delocalised pi electrons extending around the ring.
- Benzene's carbon-carbon bonds are equivalent rather than alternating ordinary single and double bonds.
- Aromatic stabilisation helps explain why benzene is less reactive than ordinary alkenes and tends to preserve aromatic character.
- Conjugation allows electrons to be delocalised over multiple connected p orbitals rather than confined to a single bond.
- Heteroaromatic rings extend the same principle into molecules containing nitrogen, oxygen, sulfur, and other heteroatoms.
- Nucleobases contain aromatic heterocyclic structures whose electronic distribution contributes to pairing, stacking, and recognition.
- Quinones are redox-active compounds that can undergo reversible oxidation and reduction; physiological quinones participate in electron and proton transfer.
- Carotenoids contain extended conjugated double-bond systems that affect colour and light absorption.
- Graphene is a two-dimensional sp2 carbon lattice with delocalised pi electrons contributing to electronic conduction.
- Graphite consists of stacked graphene-like layers and conducts much better within layers than between layers.
- Defects, edges, interruptions, and substitutions can localise or distort delocalised electron fields.

## AMS Delocalisation Principle

The delocalisation principle:

```text
delocalised carbon order = retained electronic behaviour spread across a relational carbon field
```

This is not vague "electron cloud" language. A delocalised route means that an electronic property cannot be assigned cleanly to one local bond or atom. The route is carried by the whole relational field.

In AMS terms:

```text
local bond route = identity concentrated between specific atoms.
delocalised route = identity distributed across a constrained electronic topology.
```

This is a direct vorton-relevant case. A delocalised ring, chain, or sheet behaves as a closure field. The electronic route returns through the structure rather than terminating at a single site.

## Route Map 1: Benzene and Aromatic Closure

Benzene is the standard aromatic reference point.

AMS route:

```text
six sp2 carbons -> planar cyclic p-orbital overlap -> delocalised pi field -> aromatic stability
```

Benzene is not best understood as three isolated double bonds alternating with three single bonds. Its ring structure permits cyclic electronic delocalisation. The result is a stable closure field with equivalent carbon-carbon bonds.

Vorton interpretation:

```text
benzene vorton = planar cyclic pi-closure field
```

The vorton is not a little object inside the ring. It is the stable electronic closure condition of the ring itself.

Route profile:

```text
route class: R1/R5
obstruction class: O1
dominance: D1
grade: G_A
```

Why `R5`?

Because aromaticity is loop-dominated. The route is not just a line from atom to atom. It is a retained loop field around the ring. This is a legitimate `R5`, not a pseudo-route, because the loop is chemically stabilising and empirically powerful.

Mechanism-facing statement:

```text
Aromatic carbon closure distributes pi-electron behaviour across a planar cyclic topology, making ring identity more stable than an equivalent localised-bond picture would predict.
```

## Route Map 2: Conjugated Chains and Spectral Filtering

Conjugation extends electronic distribution through alternating single and double bonds.

AMS route:

```text
connected p orbitals -> extended pi system -> lower energy transitions -> light absorption / colour / redox tuning
```

In carotenoids, extended conjugated carbon chains produce strong coloration and light-absorption behaviour. The length and configuration of the conjugated system affect absorption maxima and spectral behaviour.

Vorton interpretation:

```text
conjugated-chain vorton = elongated delocalised route field
chromophore vorton = light-coupled electronic route
```

Route profile:

```text
route class: R2/R4
obstruction class: O1/O4
dominance: D1/D4
grade: G_A
```

Conjugated chains are not as closed as aromatic rings, but they carry distributed electronic behaviour over a defined path.

AMS implication:

```text
colour is route-selective electronic response.
```

This connects the carbon family to the optical work already done on glass and metals. In glass, light route delay is governed by dielectric structure. In pigments, light response is governed by molecular electronic transition fields.

## Route Map 4: Quinones and Reversible Redox Delocalisation

Quinones are redox-active carbonyl-conjugated systems.

AMS route:

```text
quinone <-> semiquinone <-> hydroquinone
```

This makes quinones electron and proton carriers. In biology, ubiquinone and related systems participate in electron transfer routes. Quinones can also produce or modulate reactive oxygen species depending on route context.

Vorton interpretation:

```text
quinone vorton = reversible redox-delocalisation field
semiquinone vorton = radical intermediate route state
hydroquinone vorton = reduced proton/electron-retained state
```

Route profile for governed quinone cycling:

```text
route class: R4
obstruction class: O4
dominance: D4
grade: G_A
```

Route profile for destructive redox cycling:

```text
route class: R6
obstruction class: O6
dominance: D4
grade: G_C/G_D
```

AMS statement:

```text
quinones show that delocalised carbon fields can carry reversible redox states, not merely stable aromatic identity.
```

This connects `C7` back to `C6`: carbon can route metabolism through skeleton changes, but it can also route redox state through delocalised electronic fields.

## Route Map 5: Graphite and Graphene Sheet Conduction

Graphene is a two-dimensional sp2 carbon lattice. Graphite consists of stacked graphene-like layers.

AMS route:

```text
sp2 carbon sheet -> delocalised pi network -> in-plane electronic mobility -> conductive material behaviour
```

The same basic carbon capacity that supports aromatic rings expands into a sheet. The delocalised field is no longer a small molecular ring. It becomes an extended material topology.

Vorton interpretation:

```text
graphene sheet vorton = extended two-dimensional delocalised carbon field
graphite layer vorton = stacked anisotropic electronic route field
```

Route profile:

```text
route class: R2/R5
obstruction class: O1/O5
dominance: D1/D4
grade: G_A
```

Why `R2/R5`?

Graphene/graphite conduction is distributed across a wide field rather than a narrow molecular route. It is also loop/mesh-dominated because hexagonal carbon topology repeats across the sheet.

Graphite's anisotropy matters. Electron movement is much easier within layers than between layers. AMS interpretation:

```text
in-plane route = strong distributed delocalised field.
interlayer route = weaker transfer across van der Waals separation.
```

This gives a direct material-level example of `B_in` and `B_out` asymmetry:

```text
B_in within sheet >> B_out between sheets
```

## Route Map 6: Edge, Defect, and Substitution Effects

Delocalised carbon fields are powerful but vulnerable to interruption.

AMS failure routes:

```text
defect -> localised electron state
edge -> altered electron density
heteroatom substitution -> tuned or distorted route field
oxidation -> broken conjugation
twist/nonplanarity -> reduced p-orbital overlap
```

In graphene, pristine interior carbon differs from edge carbon. Edges, holes, vacancies, dopants, and structural disorder can localise electrons and increase reactivity. In organic molecules, breaking conjugation or forcing nonplanarity can weaken the delocalised route.

Vorton interpretation:

```text
defect vorton = local interruption or redirection of a delocalised field
edge vorton = boundary-biased electronic route
dopant vorton = tuned field asymmetry
```

Route profile:

```text
route class: R3/R6
obstruction class: O2/O6
dominance: D4
grade: G_C
```

This is not always bad. A defect can damage conduction, but it can also create useful reactivity, binding, sensing, catalysis, or functionalisation. The same interruption can be failure in one context and designed route access in another.

AMS principle:

```text
defects are not inherently disorder; they are local topology changes whose meaning depends on route function.
```

```text
sulfur can tune delocalised fields and protect carbon architecture from redox failure.
```

### Carbon and Metals

Metal centres can couple to delocalised carbon systems in pigments, catalysts, electrodes, and materials.

```text
carbon supplies electronic field geometry.
metals supply redox depth, coordination, or catalytic boundary conditions.
```

This is why graphite, quinones, chlorophyll-related systems, heme environments, and metal-organic interfaces are not isolated cases but part of a wider carbon-electronic grammar.

## Link to Optical and Electrical Material Questions

Glass routes light through polarisation and delay in an extended dielectric network.

Carbon materials occupy a distinctive middle territory:

```text
diamond = localised sp3 skeleton, insulating.
graphite/graphene = delocalised sp2 field, conductive.

```

AMS implication:

```text

```

This is one of the clearest cases where topology, not elemental identity alone, determines function.
