# Audience Profile: GCSE Physics Readers (UK, age ~16)

Date: 2026-03-11
Status: research baseline for AMS v2.0 audience review

## Purpose

This document defines the likely reading, pacing, and explanation needs of a UK GCSE-level physics reader for the future AMS v2.0 GCSE adaptation.

It is not a simplification plan for the whole framework.

It is a reading-profile document for later review and adaptation work.

## Research Basis

Online reference points used:
- BBC Bitesize GCSE Physics/Energy revision episode format: short lesson blocks, explicit learning goals, single-topic focus, recap structure.
- Pearson Revise/BBC Bitesize revision-guide positioning: bite-sized chunks, retrieval prompts, worked exam-style preparation.
- GCSE-facing revision-note ecosystems such as Save My Exams and Physics & Maths Tutor: short sections, immediate definitions, worked examples, and strong topic segmentation.

Research takeaway:
- this readership expects short, high-clarity units
- concepts are usually introduced one at a time
- abstraction is tolerated only after concrete anchoring
- unfamiliar words need immediate explanation
- recap and retrieval matter

## Reader Baseline

Likely safe assumptions:
- basic school physics vocabulary: force, energy, current, voltage, wave, field
- familiarity with diagrams, equations, short definitions, and revision questions
- limited or no prior exposure to ontology, metaphysics, epistemology, or formal theology

Unsafe assumptions:
- comfort with nested premises
- comfort with category distinctions such as ontology vs model vs mechanism
- patience for long unbroken prose
- ability to infer theological guardrails from compressed wording

## Sentence Structure

Recommended style:
- short to medium sentences
- one main claim per sentence where possible
- low clause density
- avoid stacked subordinate clauses
- avoid long parenthetical qualifiers

Tolerance profile:
- nested reasoning: low
- digressions: low
- abstract compression: low

## Vocabulary Profile

Best practice:
- prefer common English before specialist language
- define AMS terms immediately on first use
- repeat definitions in later chapters without embarrassment
- keep Greek/Latin-derived abstraction rare

Technical vocabulary that can survive if carefully taught:
- ontology
- substrate
- configuration
- boundary
- admissibility

But each of those needs:
- plain-English unpacking
- an example
- a reminder later

## Explanatory Method

Best fit:
- concrete before abstract
- question first, answer second
- example before definition when possible
- frequent restatement in different words

Recommended chapter moves:
1. start with a familiar phenomenon
2. state the puzzle
3. introduce the AMS term
4. contrast it with the usual classroom picture
5. recap what changed

## Assumption Load

This audience can handle:
- one new major AMS distinction every few pages
- two or three linked premises if they are narrated gently

This audience will likely struggle with:
- five or more unstated premises in sequence
- theology and physics being cross-referenced without transition
- phrases that assume philosophical literacy

## Axiom Consumption Capacity

Recommended ceiling:
- 2 major axioms introduced freshly in a chapter
- 1 additional recalled axiom may be active without overload

Recommended handling:
- axioms should usually be narrated rather than listed formally
- each axiom should be tied to a concrete example
- chapter openings should restate the needed prior assumptions
- chapter endings should restate the active conceptual chain

Loss points:
- when two or more AMS distinctions are introduced before the reader sees why they matter
- when theological boundaries are compressed into shorthand

## Conceptual Pacing

Needs:
- slower pacing than every other target audience
- regular recap boxes or chapter-end summaries
- preview sentences before difficult transitions
- explicit “what this chapter is doing” framing

Recommended recap frequency:
- every subsection or every 2-3 pages in prose terms

## Tone

Target tone:
- patient
- confidence-building
- respectful
- non-patronising
- direct without sounding severe

Avoid:
- adversarial posturing
- compressed polemic
- academic self-importance
- irony or sarcasm

## Structural Expectations

Best fit:
- short chapters or heavily subsectioned chapters
- definition boxes
- worked examples
- diagrams
- end-of-chapter recap
- mini-glossary support

High-value teaching supports:
- “in ordinary words” restatements
- “what AMS is not saying” boxes
- comparison tables

## Theological Handling

Requirement:
- theological boundaries must remain intact
- but doctrinal guardrails need direct plain-language explanation

Best method:
- explain the boundary first in simple faithful terms
- only then connect it to ontology or physics implications

Risk:
- if theology is compressed, readers may either miss it or read it as arbitrary assertion

## Reader Fatigue Risk

Likely overload points:
- ontology-heavy openings
- multiple new abstractions without example
- long paragraphs defending terms before showing their use
- chapter sequences with no recap

## Common Failure Modes

1. Using “simple” words but leaving the logic implicit.
2. Keeping chapter order adult-facing and only shortening sentences.
3. Assuming the reader can translate AMS terms into ordinary language alone.
4. Collapsing doctrinal precision to avoid difficulty.
5. Overloading the reader with negations: “not this, not that, not that either.”

## Writing Guidance For This Audience

The future GCSE version should:
- explain more
- restate more
- slow down transitions
- keep chapter scope narrow
- use frequent examples from ordinary school physics

It should not:
- flatten AMS-specific meanings into mainstream approximations
- remove theological guardrails
- pretend that difficult concepts are easy when they are not

## Recommended Master-Manuscript Implications

The master book should preserve enough structure for later GCSE adaptation by including:
- chapter purpose statements
- strong internal definitions
- explicit bridge paragraphs
- recap-ready section endings
- example opportunities in every major chapter
