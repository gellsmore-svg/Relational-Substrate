This note closes one of the clearest remaining iron-family gaps:


## Central Claim

The central claim is:

> Non-heme iron enzymes show that iron's family identity is not exhausted by heme pockets, oxygen transport, or Fe-S relays. Outside heme, iron still functions as governed redox depth: it activates O2, supports substrate radical chemistry, enables hydroxylation and ring cleavage, and mediates difficult oxidative transformations only when embedded in carefully tuned coordination environments that prevent uncontrolled oxygen damage.

## Source Anchors

The source base supports these facts:

- Non-heme iron enzymes catalyse a wide range of biologically important reactions including hydroxylation, halogenation, epimerisation, cyclisation, ring cleavage, and oxidative bond scission.
- Many mononuclear non-heme iron enzymes use the common `2-His-1-carboxylate` facial triad to bind Fe and activate O2.
- α-ketoglutarate/iron-dependent oxygenases generate high-valent ferryl-type intermediates that drive substrate oxidation.
- Rieske and other non-heme dioxygenases use iron centres to activate O2 for incorporation into aromatic substrates and degradation pathways.
- A central theme in these enzymes is regulated oxygen activation: the iron centre, surrounding ligands, and protein environment cooperate to avoid unproductive ROS chemistry.

## AMS Non-Heme Iron Principle

The non-heme iron principle is:

```text
iron can carry catalytic redox depth outside heme if the surrounding coordination geometry disciplines oxygen activation tightly enough.
```

That means the iron identity survives, but its route expression changes:

- heme uses porphyrin embedding and pocket control;
- non-heme systems use ligand geometry, substrate positioning, cosubstrates, and active-site residues.

AMS interpretation:

```text
non-heme iron is iron depth with less intrinsic enclosure and therefore stronger dependence on local route governance.
```
