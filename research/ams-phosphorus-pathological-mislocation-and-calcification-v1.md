## Purpose


- DNA/RNA backbone continuity;

The pressure question is:


## Central Claim

The central claim is:

> Pathological calcification does not break the phosphorus family identity. It reveals its boundary. Phosphorus remains the carrier of transferable and closure-capable order, but when phosphate balance, location, or inhibitory governance fail, that same closure-capacity is misdeployed into ectopic mineralization. Pathological calcification is therefore phosphorus order outside proper domain, not evidence that the family identity is wrong.

## Source Anchors

The source base supports these facts:

- Pathological or ectopic calcification is inappropriate biomineralization in soft tissues.
- These deposits are often composed mainly of calcium phosphate salts, especially hydroxyapatite.
- Elevated extracellular phosphate is a major risk factor for vascular calcification.
- Pyrophosphate is a major endogenous inhibitor of calcium-phosphate crystal formation and growth.
- The balance between phosphate and pyrophosphate is a key regulator of whether mineralization is promoted or restrained.
- Soft tissues remain unmineralized under normal conditions not because phosphate is absent, but because inhibitory and regulatory systems restrict calcium-phosphate crystal growth to proper contexts.

Sources consulted:

- PMC, "Vascular Calcification: Key Roles of Phosphate and Pyrophosphate";
- PMC, "Ectopic Calcification: Gathering Hard Facts about Soft Tissue Mineralization";
- PMC, "Ectopic mineralization disorders of the extracellular matrix of connective tissue";
- PMC, "Pyrophosphate metabolism and calcification".

This preserves the family and clarifies its boundary.
