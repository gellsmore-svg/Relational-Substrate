# AMS Vorton Invariant Profile and Formation Mechanism v1

## Purpose

This note defines the first controlled version of `I_profile`, the invariant profile required before a candidate closure-pattern may be called a stable vorton.

It also locks an important governing rule:

```text
AMS ontology may be revised, but it must always preserve a candidate mechanism
by which substrate organisation can form into perceived matter.
```

Without that mechanism, AMS becomes only a vocabulary for mystery. With it, the framework remains answerable: it must say how non-expressive substrate order can become stable primary topology, and how stable primary topology can support secondary material regimes.

## Mechanism Requirement

Every future vorton model must preserve this minimum formation chain:

```text
continuous substrate
-> local organised disturbance
-> torsion/tension/alignment expression
-> candidate closure-pattern
-> stable primary topology/vorton
-> coupled vorton regimes
-> perceived matter
```

The details may change. The chain may be refined. But the model may not skip from substrate to perceived matter without a formation account.

AMS must therefore always be able to answer:

```text
What changes in the substrate?
How is the change retained?
How does it return through itself?
How does it persist under perturbation?
How does it couple to other stable closures?
How do coupled closures become material regimes?
Why does this regime become perceivable as matter?
```

## What `I_profile` Does

`I_profile` is the discriminating identity profile of a vorton candidate.

It answers:

```text
what remains the same enough for this to count as the same primary topology?
```

It must not be treated as a single number. It is a structured profile containing the invariants and tolerances that distinguish stable vorton identity from transient propagation, failed closure, or secondary material patterning.

## Minimal `I_profile`

For a candidate vorton `V`, define:

```text
I_profile(V) =
[
  C_class,
  W_sig,
  H_sig,
  R_map,
  B_env,
  Q_band,
  L_band,
  S_env,
  K_couple,
  M_reseat,
  F_modes
]
```

Where:

- `C_class` = closure class
- `W_sig` = winding or return signature
- `H_sig` = handedness or orientation signature
- `R_map` = return-path map
- `B_env` = boundedness envelope
- `Q_band` = coherence tolerance band
- `L_band` = leakage tolerance band
- `S_env` = stability envelope
- `K_couple` = coupling behaviour
- `M_reseat` = permitted reseating modes
- `F_modes` = characteristic failure modes

This profile is semi-formal. Its purpose is to discipline the next modelling pass.

## 1. Closure Class `C_class`

`C_class` describes the kind of self-returning organisation present in the candidate.

Questions:

- Does the pattern close?
- Is closure local, distributed, nested, braided, or composite?
- Is closure full, quasi-closed, or externally supported?
- Does closure survive ordinary perturbation?

A candidate without closure may be a transient structure, but it is not a vorton.

## 2. Winding or Return Signature `W_sig`

`W_sig` describes the return-count or recurrence structure of the candidate.

It does not require a final mathematical winding number yet. It requires that the candidate exhibit some stable return signature:

```text
after traversing the candidate's organising relation,
the pattern returns to itself within tolerance
```

If the pattern never returns to itself, it fails as underreturned topology.

## 3. Handedness or Orientation Signature `H_sig`

`H_sig` records whether the candidate has a stable orientation, twist sense, handedness, or related directional signature.

This matters because two candidates may share broad closure behaviour while differing in orientation compatibility, coupling behaviour, or reseating pathway.

`H_sig` may be:

- strong and discriminating
- weak but detectable
- absent for some candidate classes

Absence of handedness does not automatically disqualify a candidate, but the profile must state whether it is present.

## 4. Return-Path Map `R_map`

`R_map` describes how strain, perturbation, or local mismatch routes through the candidate and returns without destroying identity.

Questions:

- Is return single-path or distributed?
- Are there backup return routes?
- Does return require a supporting neighbouring regime?
- Does return degrade under repeated perturbation?

This is one of the key bridges from substrate organisation to perceived matter. A perceived material object is not merely a static presence; it is a stable regime that can receive, route, and return perturbation.

## 5. Boundedness Envelope `B_env`

`B_env` describes the range across which the candidate remains bounded.

It should include:

- minimum inward boundedness
- maximum tolerable outward boundary pressure
- leakage threshold
- collapse threshold from overconcentration

This prevents a candidate from being called stable simply because it briefly concentrates.

## 6. Coherence Tolerance Band `Q_band`

`Q_band` describes how much internal mismatch the candidate can absorb while preserving identity.

Questions:

- How much internal conflict can the candidate reseat?
- Does perturbation produce lawful adjustment or fragmentation?
- Does coherence recover after stress?

This is the difference between stable topology and brittle arrangement.

## 7. Leakage Tolerance Band `L_band`

`L_band` describes how much organised constraint can leak before identity is lost.

Low leakage alone is not enough. Overconstrained topology may leak little and still fail by brittleness. `L_band` must therefore be read with `Q_band`, `R_map`, and `B_env`.

## 8. Stability Envelope `S_env`

`S_env` describes the conditions under which the vorton candidate persists.

It should include:

- perturbation tolerance
- route-load tolerance
- neighbouring support requirements
- temperature-like agitation tolerance where applicable
- transition thresholds

This makes vorton identity conditional without making it arbitrary.

## 9. Coupling Behaviour `K_couple`

`K_couple` describes how the candidate interacts with other stable closures and transient route structures.

Questions:

- Does it attract, repel, align, block, scatter, store, or transmit?
- Does it support bonding-like secondary topology?
- Does it bias magnetic alignment?
- Does it support electrical route-bearing regimes?
- Does it absorb, scatter, or transmit light-like propagation?

This is the bridge from stable vorton identity to material regimes.

## 10. Permitted Reseating Modes `M_reseat`

`M_reseat` describes how the candidate can adjust without ceasing to be itself.

Examples:

- local slip
- orientation adjustment
- boundary reseating
- coupling change
- reversible deformation
- transition between compatible route states

If a candidate cannot reseat at all, it may be overconstrained. If it reseats without identity preservation, it is not a stable vorton.

## 11. Characteristic Failure Modes `F_modes`

`F_modes` lists how the candidate fails.

Every vorton type should have expected failure modes. This is not a weakness; it is part of making the model constrained.

Relevant failure classes:

- diffuse topology
- collapse-prone topology
- overconstrained topology
- underreturned topology
- unstable oscillatory topology
- incompatible adjacency topology
- threshold-fragile topology
- fragmentation
- leakage decay
- coupling overload

If the candidate has no defined failure modes, the profile is incomplete.

## Identity Preservation Rule

A candidate vorton must preserve `I_profile` across a minimum perturbation window:

```text
I_profile(V, t0) ~= I_profile(V, t0 + N_min cycles)
```

This does not mean every variable is numerically unchanged. It means the profile remains inside its identity-preserving tolerance bands.

Allowed change:

```text
reseating, reversible deformation, tolerable leakage, compatible coupling shift
```

Identity-breaking change:

```text
loss of closure class, loss of return signature, fragmentation,
collapse, irreversible leakage, incompatible coupling takeover
```

## Formation Into Perceived Matter

The candidate mechanism from substrate to perceived matter is:

```text
1. Continuous substrate has ordered but non-expressive capacity.
2. Local disturbance creates torsion/tension/alignment expression.
3. Some disturbances begin to close through returnable organisation.
4. Most candidates fail by diffusion, collapse, underreturn, oscillation, or incompatibility.
5. A narrow class preserves closure, boundedness, coherence, and invariant profile.
6. These stable closures are vortons: primary topology.
7. Vortons couple, reseat, constrain, and form secondary regimes.
8. Secondary regimes produce persistent material behaviours.
9. Perceived matter is the stable, interaction-bearing presentation of those regimes.
```

This is not proof of AMS. It is the required candidate mechanism.

## Perceived Matter Boundary

Perceived matter begins when stable primary topology and secondary regimes can provide:

- persistence
- boundary behaviour
- resistance to perturbation
- route-bearing interaction
- coupling to light-like propagation
- coupling to electrical and magnetic response
- repeatable measurement presentation

In AMS terms, matter is not perceived because substrate becomes visible directly. Matter is perceived because stable topological regimes interact lawfully and repeatedly with other route structures, including those involved in light, instrumentation, bodies, and observers.

## Example: Non-Vorton Transient

A light-like transient may have:

- high coherence
- route continuity
- phase structure
- measurable propagation

But if it lacks boundedness, returnability, and invariant persistence, then:

```text
I_profile incomplete -> not a vorton
```

## Example: Magnetic Domain

A magnetic domain may have:

- stable alignment
- boundary behaviour
- hysteresis
- material-scale persistence

But if it is an aggregate secondary regime rather than a primary closure-pattern, then:

```text
T2 regime -> not automatically a vorton
```

The domain may depend on vortons. It is not therefore itself a vorton.

## Example: Proto-Vorton

A proto-vorton may have:

- partial closure
- above-threshold boundedness
- emerging returnability
- temporary coherence

But if it fails to preserve `I_profile` across the required perturbation window, then:

```text
proto-vorton decays -> no stable vorton identity
```

## Current Status

This note defines `I_profile` as a controlled semi-formal identity profile.

It does not yet solve:

- exact substrate metric
- exact closure mathematics
- exact energy accounting
- exact scale of stable vorton formation
- exact relation to conventional particle descriptions

It does establish the gate future vorton work must pass before claiming that substrate organisation has formed stable primary topology capable of supporting perceived matter.
