# AMS Sulfur Mineral-Redox and Prebiotic Bridge Map v1

## Purpose

This note maps sulfur's `S7` role:

```text
S7: mineral-redox bridge
```

The previous sulfur maps established:

- `S3`: disulfide protein fold locks;
- `S4`: iron-sulfur electron route nodes;
- `S5/S8`: thiol redox buffering, signalling, and toxicity boundaries;
- `S6`: sulfated extracellular matrix charge fields.

This note connects mineral sulfur chemistry to biological sulfur chemistry. It asks how sulfur-bearing minerals, sulfides, sulfates, hydrothermal gradients, Fe-S clusters, and microbial sulfur metabolism form a coherent route bridge between geological order and living order.

The central claim is:

> Sulfur bridges mineral redox structure and biological metabolism by providing geochemical gradients, metal-sulfide surfaces, Fe-S cluster continuity, and cycling between sulfide, elemental sulfur, sulfate, and assimilated organic sulfur.

This is a coherence model, not a proof of life's origin.

## Source Anchors

The source base is strong for mineral-biological continuity and plausible for origin-of-life modelling:

- Fe-S proteins are ancient and fundamental to electron transfer and CO2 fixation.
- Biological Fe-S clusters have structural similarities to Fe-S minerals such as greigite found in hydrothermal settings.
- Redox-active Fe-S clusters can form spontaneously from inorganic iron/sulfide and low concentrations of cysteine in alkaline aqueous conditions.
- In laboratory work, cysteine-Fe-S clusters formed `[4Fe4S]`, `[2Fe2S]`, and mononuclear iron species under anaerobic alkaline conditions and showed redox activity.
- Alkaline hydrothermal vent models propose redox, pH, and temperature gradients across iron-sulfide precipitates as plausible settings for prebiotic chemistry.
- Metal sulfide minerals in hydrothermal vent environments can provide catalytic, electrochemical, and concentrating surfaces.
- Hydrothermal systems can involve serpentinization, H2 production, alkaline fluids, sulfide-rich precipitates, and microporous mineral compartments.
- Microbial sulfur metabolism includes sulfide oxidation, sulfate reduction, sulfur disproportionation, sulfur assimilation, and links to carbon, nitrogen, iron, and redox cycles.
- Sulfide produced by assimilatory sulfate reduction supports cysteine, methionine, and Fe-S cluster biosynthesis.

## Mechanism 1: Metal Sulfide Surface Route

Metal sulfide minerals provide surfaces where redox chemistry, adsorption, electron flow, and concentration can occur.

AMS route:

```text
hydrothermal reductant / environmental oxidant
-> metal sulfide surface
-> electron/ion/adsorbate route
-> local chemical transformation
```

Classification:

```text
R2/R4/O4/D4/G_B
```

Why:

The route is real but distributed and context-dependent. Mineral surfaces can guide chemistry, but the specificity is lower than enzyme-governed biology.

AMS variables:

```text
B_in    = geochemical reductant/oxidant supply
Q_coh   = mineral surface coherence, crystal face, conductivity, porosity
J_bound = adsorbed reactants/intermediates
J_trans = surface-mediated electron or chemical transfer
B_out   = transformed products or released intermediates
I_comp  = diffusion, side reactions, poisoning, precipitation, thermal destruction
D       = D4
```

Failure modes:

```text
too little binding -> diffusion loss
too much binding -> surface trapping
wrong mineral face -> low selectivity
thermal/chemical excess -> product destruction
```

Classifications:

```text
diffusion loss: R2/O5/D3/G_C
surface trapping: R5/O3/D2/G_C
low selectivity: R3/O2/D4/G_C
fragmented chemistry: R6/O6/D4/G_C
```

## Mechanism 3: Fe-S Cluster Continuity

The strongest `S7` anchor is the continuity between mineral Fe-S chemistry and protein-bound Fe-S clusters.

Source-supported facts:

- Fe-S proteins are ancient and fundamental.
- Fe-S clusters are used for electron transfer and CO2 fixation.
- Fe-S cluster structures resemble units found in Fe-S minerals.
- Cysteine-Fe-S clusters can form spontaneously under alkaline aqueous anaerobic conditions.
- These clusters can be redox-active.

AMS route:

```text
inorganic Fe/S chemistry
-> cysteine or organic ligand capture
-> Fe-S cluster closure
-> redox-active node
-> protein-bound Fe-S cofactor
```

Classification:

```text
R1/R4/O1/O4/D1/D4/G_A
```

Why:

This is the highest-confidence bridge in the `S7` map. It gives a direct route from mineral-like redox geometry to biological electron-transfer machinery.

Vorton interpretation:

```text
mineral Fe-S closure -> ligand-stabilised Fe-S vorton -> protein-embedded Fe-S route node
```

The protein does not invent the redox geometry from nothing. It captures, stabilises, tunes, and internalises a mineral-compatible route closure.

## Mechanism 4: Sulfur Oxidation-State Cycle

Sulfur spans multiple oxidation states:

```text
sulfide -> elemental sulfur -> sulfite -> sulfate
```

and in biology:

```text
sulfate -> assimilatory reduction -> sulfide -> cysteine -> methionine / Fe-S / thiols
```

Microbial sulfur metabolism cycles sulfur through oxidation, reduction, disproportionation, and assimilation.

AMS route:

```text
environmental sulfur species
-> microbial oxidation/reduction route
-> energy generation or assimilation
-> organic sulfur incorporation
-> protein/cofactor/redox function
```

Classification:

```text
R1/R4/O1/O4/D1/D4/G_A
```

Why:

In living systems this is not random sulfur chemistry. It is enzymatically governed cycling. Sulfur becomes a metabolic route substrate, redox participant, and biosynthetic precursor.

Key bridge:

```text
sulfate reduction supplies sulfide for cysteine
cysteine supplies methionine, thiols, disulfides, and Fe-S clusters
```

This links environmental sulfur state to core biological architecture.

R2/R4/O4/D4/G_B

## Testable Expectations

If the AMS `S7` model is coherent, the following should hold:

1. Metal sulfide minerals should show environment-sensitive catalytic or electrochemical behaviour under relevant gradients.
2. Fe-S-like clusters should be easier to obtain under plausible prebiotic conditions than highly elaborate enzyme-like structures.
3. Organic ligands such as cysteine should stabilise or redirect inorganic Fe/S chemistry into more portable cluster forms.
4. Mineral surfaces should both help and hinder prebiotic chemistry depending on binding/release balance.
5. Hydrothermal gradient systems should outperform unbounded homogeneous solution for route persistence.
6. Biological Fe-S cluster systems should retain recognisable mineral-redox logic while adding protein tuning and assembly control.
7. Sulfur metabolism should remain tightly connected to iron, carbon, nitrogen, and redox cycling.
8. Sulfur route failure should frequently appear as oxidation collapse, sulfide toxicity, precipitation, or unselective side branching.

These expectations match the source literature direction.
