# AMS Boundary and Interface Corpus v1

## Purpose

This corpus isolates one of the strongest themes emerging from the current AMS work:

- many of the most informative higher-order wholes are defined not only by bulk persistence, but by boundary behaviour and interface logic

The earlier corpus work already showed this in:

- membranes
- catalytic surfaces
- grain structures
- oxide boundaries
- hydration and hydrogen-bond networks

This document gathers those pressures into one dedicated corpus so the ontology can be tested where:

- inside and outside matter
- compatibility and incompatibility matter
- local stability and boundary failure diverge

## Governing Question

The key question in this corpus is:

- when is a boundary merely the edge of a thing, and when is it part of the thing’s identity?

AMS should answer that some wholes are only weakly boundary-dependent, while others are strongly boundary-defined.

## Case 1: Membranes As Boundary-Defined Wholes

### Why This Case Matters

Membranes are among the clearest cases where the boundary is not accidental. The whole exists as the kind of thing it is partly because it separates and selectively relates.

### Observed

Membranes show:

- inside / outside distinction
- selective permeability
- dynamic repair
- whole-level persistence despite local replacement

### AMS interpretation

The membrane boundary is constitutive rather than decorative.

- `Boundary role`: constitutive
- `Interface type`: selective, dynamic, relational
- `Failure mode`: rupture, incoherence, permeability loss
- `Higher-order significance`: very high

### Confidence

- `Observed`: strong
- `Strong AMS inference`: strong
- `Confidence score`: `8/10`

## Case 2: Catalytic Surfaces As Functional Interfaces

### Why This Case Matters

Catalytic surfaces concentrate function at the boundary. The bulk whole persists, but much of the explanatory action sits at the interface.

### Observed

Catalytic surfaces show:

- boundary-dependent activity
- site sensitivity
- poisoning and conditioning
- defect dependence

### AMS interpretation

This is one of the best cases for showing that:

- some wholes are materially real at bulk scale
- but explanatorily decisive at the interface scale

- `Boundary role`: function-bearing
- `Interface type`: selective, reactive, site-dependent
- `Failure mode`: poisoning, site loss, boundary disorder
- `Higher-order significance`: high

### Confidence

- `Observed`: strong
- `Conjectural AMS extension`: significant
- `Confidence score`: `6/10`

## Case 3: Grain Boundaries In Metals

### Why This Case Matters

Grain boundaries show that a material whole may remain real while still containing internal interfaces that strongly affect behaviour.

### Observed

Grain boundaries influence:

- fracture
- fatigue
- deformation
- conductivity
- magnetic behaviour

### AMS interpretation

The grain boundary is not mere imperfection added after the fact. It is an interface condition within a real whole that shapes how the whole redistributes strain and failure.

- `Boundary role`: internal interface
- `Interface type`: structural discontinuity within a higher-order whole
- `Failure mode`: crack propagation, boundary weakness, incoherent redistribution
- `Higher-order significance`: high

### Confidence

- `Observed`: strong
- `Strong AMS inference`: moderate to strong
- `Confidence score`: `7/10`

## Case 4: Metal-Oxide Boundaries

### Why This Case Matters

Metal-oxide boundaries are strong tests of whether AMS can explain regime transitions within one ontology:

- metallic interior
- oxidised exterior
- interface-dependent stress and persistence

### Observed

These boundaries matter in:

- corrosion
- oxide-layer protection
- mechanical weakening or strengthening
- transport changes

### AMS interpretation

These interfaces matter because local grouped chemistry and higher-order whole identity diverge across the boundary while remaining part of one system.

- `Boundary role`: regime-transition interface
- `Interface type`: mixed persistence boundary
- `Failure mode`: flaking, stress concentration, progressive corrosion
- `Higher-order significance`: high

### Confidence

- `Observed`: strong
- `Strong AMS inference`: strong
- `Confidence score`: `7/10`

## Case 5: Crystal-Defect Interfaces

### Why This Case Matters

Crystals help define strong whole identity. Defect interfaces test how far that identity survives local breakdown.

### Observed

Crystal defects can alter:

- fracture behaviour
- local charge or transport behaviour
- growth behaviour
- alignment

### AMS interpretation

A crystal defect is a boundary event inside an otherwise ordered whole. It tests whether AMS can keep both claims true:

- the whole is real
- the local interface matters decisively

- `Boundary role`: defect-defined interface
- `Interface type`: local incompatibility within ordered whole
- `Failure mode`: fracture nucleation, local incoherence, altered propagation
- `Higher-order significance`: high

### Confidence

- `Observed`: strong
- `Strong AMS inference`: strong
- `Confidence score`: `8/10`

## Cross-Case Results

### 1. Boundaries are not all the same

The corpus now distinguishes:

- constitutive boundaries
- functional interfaces
- internal grain boundaries
- regime-transition boundaries
- defect-defined interfaces

This is important because AMS should not use one vague boundary concept for all cases.

### 2. Boundary behaviour often carries higher-order identity

Many wholes remain what they are partly because their interface behaviour is stable and meaningful.

### 3. Boundary analysis strengthens T1C realism

This corpus supports the claim that higher-order whole identity is not weakened by boundary dependence. In many cases it is clarified by it.

## Pressure On Existing Scores

This corpus likely applies upward pressure to:

- catalytic surfaces
- metallic iron
- `FeCl3`
- membranes
- quartz / defect-sensitive crystal cases

No immediate score changes are applied here. They should wait until the defect and memory corpus is also in place.
