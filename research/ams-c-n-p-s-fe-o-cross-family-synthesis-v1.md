## Core Non-Overlap

The family set only works if each identity does distinct work.

### Carbon versus Oxygen

```text
carbon = architecture
oxygen = polarity and oxidative pull
```

Carbon answers:

- what is the scaffold;
- how far can structure extend;
- how can topology vary while identity remains carbonic.

Oxygen answers:

- where is the field asymmetric;
- where is a site hydrated, charged, attacked, linked, or terminally discharged.

If carbon is the frame, oxygen is the field and oxidative vector laid across the frame.

### Carbon versus Nitrogen

```text
carbon = buildable architecture
nitrogen = biological addressability and recognition
```

Carbon makes chains, rings, polymers, membranes, and allotropes. Nitrogen makes parts of those structures biologically legible, protonatable, sequence-readable, and ecologically gated.

### Nitrogen versus Phosphorus

```text
nitrogen = availability and recognition
phosphorus = transferable biological order
```

Nitrogen says whether the system can read, bind, fix, signal, or recognise.

Phosphorus says whether the system can:

- string information into a backbone;
- move energy in transferable packets;
- establish membrane headgroup order;
- switch route permissions by phosphorylation.

### Sulfur versus Oxygen

```text
sulfur = controllable reactivity
oxygen = polarity and oxidative power
```

Sulfur occupies the governed danger zone inside life. Oxygen often supplies the surrounding polarity field or the oxidising pressure against which sulfur buffering, repair, and signalling must operate.

### Iron versus Oxygen

```text
iron = redox depth
oxygen = terminal or fragmented redox closure
```

Iron stages electron-state capacity. Oxygen either:

- closes that depth productively into water;
- or fragments it into ROS and oxidative collapse.

### Iron versus Sulfur

```text
iron = redox depth
sulfur = route-closing redox governance
```

Iron can change state deeply. Sulfur helps contain, relay, lock, or buffer that dangerous depth, especially in Fe-S clusters and thiol systems.

### Phosphorus versus Oxygen

```text
phosphorus = transfer-centred identity
oxygen = exposed charged perimeter
```

Phosphate is a phosphorus family, but much of its outward usability is oxygen-mediated. This is a genuine division of labour, not overlap confusion.

## Runtime Grammar

The six-family set now supports a coherent runtime chain.

### 1. Structure

```text
Carbon carries molecular architecture.
```

This is the base build layer:

- small molecules;
- polymers;
- membranes;
- aromatic fields;
- carbon materials.

### 2. Readability and Entry

```text
Nitrogen carries biological availability and recognition.
Oxygen carries polarity and oxidative power.
```

Nitrogen and oxygen make carbon architecture addressable:

- nitrogen through amino addressability, amide restraint, nucleobase readability, fixation gates;
- oxygen through hydration, carbonyl polarity, oxoanion perimeters, and solvent order.

### 3. Transfer and Regulation

```text
Phosphorus carries transferable biological order.
```

Once structure is built and readable, phosphorus provides:

- stringable informational backbone;
- portable energy relay;
- boundary headgroups;
- regulatory switch tokens.

### 4. Controlled Reactivity and Depth

```text
Sulfur carries controllable chemical reactivity.
Iron carries redox depth.
```

Sulfur and iron let the system do dangerous chemistry without immediate collapse:

- sulfur through fold locks, Fe-S closure, thiol buffering, sulfation fields;
- iron through heme, Fe-S nodes, oxygen handling, catalysis, storage, mineralisation.

### 5. Terminal Closure and Failure

```text
Oxygen carries polarity and oxidative power.
```

At the deepest redox end, oxygen:

- closes respiration;
- powers gradients;
- fragments into ROS if closure fails;
- scales into atmosphere, oxides, combustion, and planetary regime.

## Functional Stack

The six families can be rendered as a compact AMS stack:

```text
Carbon -> architecture
Nitrogen -> availability and recognition
Oxygen -> polarity and oxidative vector
Phosphorus -> transferable order
```

The repeat appearance of oxygen is not duplication. It reflects oxygen's unique span:

- early as polarity field;
- late as terminal oxidative power.

This is not a random list of useful biological elements.

The set is coherent because each family answers a different high-level systems question:

| What carries deep electron-state capacity? | Iron |

This is strong evidence that the AMS element programme is not just descriptive chemistry. It is converging on a runtime ontology.

## Remaining Gaps

The core set is strong, but not finished mathematically or comparatively.

Main remaining technical gaps:

- Carbon still lacks formal vorton geometry for major topology classes.
- Phosphorus still lacks ATP synthase and deeper multisite phosphorylation modelling.
- Sulfur still has some context-heavy sulfate and mineral-history cases that cap the score below `9.5/10`.
- Iron still lacks a dedicated non-heme catalytic iron map.
- Oxygen's planetary integration is broader and less tightly constrained than its core biochemical maps.
- The six-family comparative system still needs one pass linking the element identities directly into the molecule-level vorton catalogue and the glass/material route programme.
