# Audience Profile: A-level Physics Readers (UK, age ~18)

Date: 2026-03-11
Status: research baseline for AMS v2.0 audience review

## Purpose

This document defines the likely reading and abstraction profile of a UK A-level reader for the future AMS v2.0 A-level adaptation.

## Research Basis

Online reference points used:
- AQA A-level Physics specification and subject-content framing.
- Save My Exams A-level Physics revision notes and exam-prep structure.
- Physics & Maths Tutor A-level notes/problem sets.

Research takeaway:
- this readership can tolerate more abstraction than GCSE readers
- definitions and derivations can come earlier
- equations, models, and conceptual comparison are expected
- but transitions still need explicit guidance

## Reader Baseline

Likely safe assumptions:
- comfort with formal definitions
- confidence with equations and model-based explanation
- ability to track multi-step explanations in mechanics, electricity, waves, and fields
- some tolerance for abstract unseen entities if clearly motivated

Unsafe assumptions:
- philosophy vocabulary
- theology vocabulary
- comfort with unresolved mechanism language
- effortless handling of cross-domain axioms

## Sentence Structure

Recommended style:
- medium-length sentences
- moderate clause density
- reasoning chains of 2-3 linked moves are acceptable
- longer sentences are acceptable if syntactically clean

Tolerance profile:
- nested reasoning: moderate
- abstraction: moderate
- qualification: moderate

## Vocabulary Profile

This audience can handle:
- model
- ontology
- mechanism
- constraint
- coherence
- admissibility
- configuration

But still needs:
- first-use definitions
- stable term discipline
- occasional ordinary-language restatement

## Explanatory Method

Best fit:
- definition plus example
- puzzle to model to reinterpretation
- compare standard account and AMS account directly

This audience benefits from:
- sharper contrast framing
- more explicit argument steps
- moderate use of diagrams and worked thought-examples

## Assumption Load

This audience can usually hold:
- 3 fresh premises if they are strongly linked
- 4-5 active premises if some are recalled from earlier chapters

Main risk:
- stacking ontology, theology, and physics claims without signalling which layer is being used

## Axiom Consumption Capacity

Recommended ceiling:
- 3 new axioms or distinctions per chapter
- 2 recalled axioms can remain active if they are briefly named again

Recommended handling:
- formal premise lists are acceptable in moderation
- but they should be followed by worked explanation
- abstract claims should still be grounded in physical scenes

Likely break point:
- when a chapter assumes philosophical method, scriptural boundary logic, and physics reinterpretation all at once without scaffolding

## Conceptual Pacing

Recommended pace:
- noticeably faster than GCSE
- still slower than bachelor’s level
- each chapter should have clear internal stages

Useful supports:
- chapter aims
- summary of active assumptions
- end-of-chapter “what has been established”

## Tone

Target tone:
- intellectually respectful
- crisp
- encouraging without overexplaining
- willing to introduce real difficulty

Avoid:
- faux-child-friendly language
- compressed doctoral prose
- sweeping rhetoric replacing argument

## Structural Expectations

Best fit:
- medium chapter length
- clean subheadings
- summary tables
- diagrams where conceptually helpful
- chapter-end consolidation

High-value additions:
- “why this matters” transitions
- “common misunderstanding” notes
- glossary support for recurring AMS terms

## Theological Handling

This audience can accept:
- explicit doctrinal boundaries
- direct statement of scriptural authority in the framework

But needs:
- clear explanation of why theological claims are not being smuggled in as physics equations
- level-tagged language distinguishing doctrine, ontology, mechanism, and empirical discussion

## Reader Fatigue Risk

Likely overload points:
- dense early ontology chapters
- too many AMS-specific terms introduced before physical payoff
- abstract guardrail chapters with no worked example

## Common Failure Modes

1. Speaking as though A-level readers are already philosophically trained.
2. Overusing compression because the readers are “smart enough.”
3. Forgetting to mark layer changes between theology and mechanism.
4. Repeating GCSE-style recap so heavily that the prose loses momentum.

## Writing Guidance For This Audience

The future A-level version should:
- remain explicit
- move more briskly than GCSE
- use definitions confidently
- teach layered distinctions without hiding them

It should not:
- assume university-level tolerance for abstraction
- depend on implied philosophical background

## Recommended Master-Manuscript Implications

The master book should preserve:
- precise terminology
- clean layer tagging
- chapter-end establishments
- example hooks for electricity, light, and time
