The central claim is:

> Nitrogenous bases are heteroaromatic recognition fields. Their nitrogen atoms help define hydrogen-bond geometry, protonation state, tautomeric possibility, and molecular shape, allowing genetic sequence to be copied, read, repaired, and sometimes miscopied.

## Source Anchors

The source base supports these facts:

- Nucleotides contain a nitrogen-containing base, pentose sugar, and phosphate group.
- DNA and RNA are polymers of nucleotides.
- The canonical DNA bases are adenine, guanine, cytosine, and thymine; RNA uses uracil instead of thymine.
- Adenine and guanine are purines; cytosine, thymine, and uracil are pyrimidines.
- Canonical Watson-Crick base pairs are A-T in DNA, A-U in RNA, and G-C.
- A-T/A-U base pairs use two hydrogen bonds; G-C base pairs use three hydrogen bonds.
- Canonical base pairing depends on complementary hydrogen-bond donors and acceptors and on steric/geometric compatibility.
- Noncanonical base pairs, including Hoogsteen and wobble/mispair forms, can occur in certain contexts.
- Rare tautomeric or ionised states of nucleobases can permit Watson-Crick-like mispairing and contribute to mutation mechanisms.
- Base stacking and aromatic interactions contribute to nucleic-acid structure and stability alongside hydrogen bonding.
- Polymerases, repair enzymes, and ribosomes exploit base-pair geometry to maintain fidelity in replication, transcription, and translation.

## AMS Nucleobase Principle

The nucleobase principle:

```text
genetic information requires stable backbone continuity plus readable base recognition.
```

Backbone continuity alone would make a polymer but not a readable code. Base recognition alone would make small molecules but not a transferable genome. Genetic order requires both:

```text
phosphate-sugar backbone = ordered carrier route.
nitrogenous base sequence = readable recognition route.
```

AMS interpretation:

```text
N3 is the route by which sequence becomes molecularly readable.
```

## Route Map 1: Purine-Pyrimidine Geometry

DNA pairs one purine with one pyrimidine in canonical Watson-Crick base pairs.

AMS route:

```text
purine width + pyrimidine width + hydrogen-bond pattern -> stable duplex geometry
```

The purine/pyrimidine distinction matters because two purines would be too wide and two pyrimidines too narrow for canonical duplex geometry. Pairing is not simply "two bases sticking together." It is recognition within a spatial constraint.

## Route Map 4: Tautomeric and Ionised Mispairing

## Route Map 5: Hoogsteen and Noncanonical Pairing

AMS route:

```text
base rotation / protonation / altered hydrogen-bond face -> noncanonical recognition field
```

These pairings are not automatically errors. They can contribute to structural diversity, RNA function, protein recognition, stress response, or transient conformational states. But in replication and translation, noncanonical geometry can threaten fidelity unless governed.

Vorton interpretation:

In structural RNA, noncanonical routes may be function. In DNA replication, the same class of route may be error risk.

## Confidence Assessment

The evidence base is very strong:

- nucleobase structures are well known;

### AMS Model Strength

The AMS mapping is strong because:

- base recognition maps directly to nitrogen's family identity;
- tautomerism gives a precise recognition-failure mechanism;
- phosphorus/carbon/nitrogen division of labour is clean;
- noncanonical pairing fits route grammar without forcing all deviations to be errors;
- polymerase fidelity maps recognition chemistry into higher-order biological governance.

### Remaining Uncertainty

The uncertainty is mostly fine-grained:

- tautomer lifetimes and mutation relevance vary by base pair, environment, and enzyme context;
- noncanonical pairing can be functional or erroneous depending on biological layer;
- quantum modelling of base-pair proton transfer is still complex;
- RNA structural grammar deserves a separate detailed map.
