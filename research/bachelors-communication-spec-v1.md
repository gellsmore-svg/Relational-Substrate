## Purpose

This document defines the communication goals and editorial rules for the first derived `Bachelor’s edition` of `Coherent Biblical Ontology`.

It exists to answer a simple question:

If the current master is denser than a normal bachelor’s-level reader can track comfortably, what exactly must change in the delivery while the ontology stays the same?

The answer is:
- keep the structure
- keep the ontology
- lower the compression
- increase navigability
- make inferential hierarchy visible

## What The Bachelor’s Edition Is

The Bachelor’s edition is:
- a serious undergraduate-facing version
- analytically direct
- conceptually demanding
- readable without graduate-level compression habits
- still fully recognisable as the same book

It is not:
- a simplified paraphrase
- a popular-level retelling
- a jargon-free version
- a doctrinal softening
- a mechanism-free version

## Communication Goal

The communication goal is:

To make the existing ontology legible at sustained bachelor’s level without flattening it, sentimentalizing it, or breaking the argument.

That means the reader should be able to:
- follow the dependency order of the argument
- identify which claims are foundational and which are derived
- see where theology grounds the frame and where ontology develops within it
- understand mechanism proposals without confusing them with settled first principles
- finish each chapter knowing what was established and why it matters next

## Structural Goal

The Bachelor’s edition should preserve:
- the 15-chapter main sequence
- the appendix structure
- the current main narrative arc
- the current chapter dependencies

It may change:
- internal subsectioning
- paragraph length
- transition clarity
- recap visibility
- placement of some technical stress-test density

## Primary Reader Problem To Solve

The current master is not too difficult in principle for bachelor’s readers.

The main problem is compression.

More specifically:
- difficult distinctions arrive too densely in some chapters
- claim status is sometimes present but not visible enough
- hidden dependency chains make later chapters harder than they need to be
- some chapters assume the reader can carry more prior structure than is realistic

The Bachelor’s edition must therefore solve for:
- density
- navigability
- hierarchy
- retention

## Tone Rules

Target tone:
- mature
- calm
- precise
- unshowy
- serious without sounding institutional

Avoid:
- classroom over-scaffolding
- motivational padding
- rhetorical overclaim
- “as we already saw” overuse
- throat-clearing before difficult claims

## Chapter Opening Rules

Every chapter should begin by doing three things within the first one or two paragraphs:

1. state the chapter’s question or pressure point
2. state why it matters inside the book’s larger argument
3. indicate what the chapter will establish, not just what it discusses

Preferred opening pattern:
- what problem is now in view
- why the previous chapter makes it unavoidable
- what this chapter must now show

Avoid:
- beginning with abstract density before the reader knows the stakes
- beginning with caveat stacks
- beginning with detail before chapter purpose is visible

## Transition Rules

Transitions must do real inferential work.

Good transitions should signal:
- what has already been established
- what remains unresolved
- why the next move follows

Minimum transition functions:
- chapter-to-chapter continuity
- section-to-section dependency marking
- re-entry after scripture blocks

Useful transition phrases:
- “The next question is…”
- “What this now requires is…”
- “That distinction matters because…”
- “This becomes decisive when…”

Avoid repeated default pivots:
- “That is why…” used too often
- “This also means…” where dependency is still unclear
- “Put differently…” unless genuine clarification follows

## Paragraph Load Rules

The Bachelor’s edition should reduce local cognitive overload without infantilizing the prose.

Working rule:
- one paragraph should usually carry one main move and at most one subordinate move

Split paragraphs when they:
- introduce more than one new distinction
- mix ontology, mechanism, scripture, and implication all at once
- hide the main sentence inside too much support material

Keep paragraphs longer when:
- the reasoning is cumulative and linear
- the sentence architecture remains controlled
- the conceptual thread is single-track

## Sentence Rules

Allowed:
- medium to long sentences
- moderate clause density
- layered reasoning

Required:
- controlled syntax
- visible sentence head
- no burying of the main claim

Avoid:
- multiple abstract nouns arriving before the predicate clarifies the point
- serial qualification that delays understanding
- stacking two or three distinctions into one sentence if they could be staged

## Terminology Rules

The Bachelor’s edition must retain core AMS terminology.

Stable terms should remain:
- ontology
- continuity
- configuration
- substrate
- runtime order
- coherence
- corruption
- redemption
- Five-Axis description
- `T0`, `T1A-T1D2`, `T2`
- `bara`, `asah`, `yatsar`

Rules for term use:
- define clearly on first use
- avoid unnecessary synonym drift
- do not replace precise AMS terms with weaker mainstream approximations
- if a term recurs after a long gap, lightly re-anchor it in context

Glossary rule:
- glossary supports the edition
- glossary does not replace clear prose

## Claim-Status Rules

Bachelor’s readers can handle complexity if the status of claims is visible.

Each chapter should make it easier to tell the difference between:
- scriptural frame
- baseline ontological claim
- explanatory extension
- mechanism proposal
- stress-test or application

This does not require labeling every paragraph mechanically.

It does require explicit signals in pressure chapters, especially:
- Chapter 4
- Chapter 5
- Chapter 8
- Appendix C
- Appendix D

Useful status markers:
- “The baseline claim is…”
- “What follows is an explanatory extension…”
- “This is proposed as a mechanism-facing account…”
- “This does not mean Scripture directly teaches…”

## Scripture Handling Rules

Bachelor’s readers can handle dense scripture use if the rhetorical function is clear.

Each scripture block should be doing one of three things:
- framing the chapter
- anchoring a distinction
- testing a claim

Avoid:
- stacked quotations with unclear interpretive function
- assuming theological agreement instead of showing why the passage matters

After scripture, the prose should answer:
- what did this passage establish here?

## Chapter Ending Rules

Every chapter should end with enough orientation to make the next chapter land cleanly.

Preferred ending functions:
- what this chapter has now secured
- what question it opens next

The ending should not become:
- a sermon recap
- a redundant summary of everything already said

It should function as retention architecture.

## Example Rules

Bachelor’s readers still need examples, but not constant introductory examples.

Use examples to:
- test explanatory gain
- expose failure of rival models
- clarify a difficult distinction

Do not use examples merely to decorate the prose.

Best example timing:
- after a claim is made
- after a distinction is introduced
- when mechanism or application needs grounding

## Appendix Transfer Rules

The Bachelor’s edition may keep the full appendix system.

But main-line chapters should offload density when:
- a technical case is not needed for the chapter’s main inferential move
- an edge case becomes a distraction from the argument
- the reader only needs to know that the ontology survives the test, not all the details immediately

Strong candidates for appendix emphasis:
- the densest technical stress tests
- edge-case mechanism detail
- some physics-case overflow from Chapters 5 and 8

What must remain in the main line:
- the explanatory payoff
- the reason the technical material matters

## Communication Success Criteria

The Bachelor’s edition succeeds if a serious undergraduate reader can:
- explain the ontology stack in their own words
- distinguish continuity from atomism
- follow the Five-Axis description
- track the difference between ontology and mechanism
- understand why corruption is parasitic
- understand why redemption is restoration of real creaturely identity
- finish the book feeling stretched, but not structurally lost

## Failure Criteria

The Bachelor’s edition fails if it:
- sounds easier but becomes vaguer
- retains all the difficulty while hiding the hierarchy
- overexplains simple points and underexplains difficult ones
- blurs mechanism and ontology
- cuts technical difficulty by cutting ontological precision

## Operational Editing Checklist

Before accepting a rewritten chapter, check:
- Is the chapter question visible early?
- Is the dependency on the previous chapter explicit?
- Are the main claims typed clearly enough?
- Are difficult terms defined when first needed?
- Is there any hidden paragraph overload?
- Does the chapter ending hand off cleanly?
- Has any precision been lost in the name of readability?

- the Chapter 4 pilot adaptation

## Bottom Line

The Bachelor’s edition should communicate the same ontology with:
- more visible hierarchy
- lower compression
- better transitions
- stronger chapter architecture

It should feel clearer, not smaller.
