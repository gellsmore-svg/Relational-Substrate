# AMS Nitrogen Redox and Oxidation-State Diversity Route Map v1

## Purpose

This note maps nitrogen's `N5` role:

```text
N5: oxidation-state and redox diversity carrier
```

The previous nitrogen maps established:

```text
nitrogen makes carbon architecture biologically addressable and foldable.
nitrogen makes genetic sequence readable.
nitrogen must be unlocked before it can be recognised.
```

This file maps nitrogen's state-switching power: ammonia/ammonium, hydroxylamine, nitrite, nitric oxide, nitrous oxide, nitrate, dinitrogen, and related microbial and biological routes.

The central claim is:

> Nitrogen carries one of biology's widest state ladders. It can move between reduced nutrient form, inert atmospheric form, oxidised electron-acceptor form, gaseous return form, mobile signalling form, and reactive stress form. This state diversity makes nitrogen ecologically powerful and biologically dangerous.

## Source Anchors

The source base supports these facts:

- The nitrogen cycle involves interconversion of nitrogen species across oxidation states from reduced ammonia/ammonium through nitrite, nitric oxide, nitrous oxide, dinitrogen, and nitrate.
- Important nitrogen species include `NH3/NH4+`, `NH2OH`, `NO2-`, `NO`, `N2O`, `N2`, and `NO3-`.
- Microbial nitrogen transformations include nitrogen fixation, nitrification, denitrification, anammox, DNRA, assimilatory nitrate reduction, and dissimilatory nitrate reduction.
- Nitrification oxidises ammonia/ammonium toward nitrite and nitrate, involving ammonia-oxidising bacteria/archaea, nitrite-oxidising bacteria, and related enzymes.
- Denitrification reduces nitrate/nitrite through nitric oxide and nitrous oxide toward dinitrogen.
- Nitrite and nitrate participate in interconnected chemical biology, including reduction toward nitric oxide.
- Nitric oxide and related reactive nitrogen species can support signalling or cause nitrosative/nitrative stress depending on concentration, context, oxygen state, reactive oxygen species, metals, thiols, and timing.
- Nitrous oxide is a greenhouse gas and can arise from microbial nitrogen transformations, especially under certain soil oxygen and substrate conditions.
- Anthropogenic reactive nitrogen has greatly increased global nitrogen cycling and contributes to eutrophication, atmospheric pollution, nitrate leaching, and nitrous oxide emissions.

## Route Map 1: Ammonia/Ammonium as Reduced Assimilable Nitrogen

Ammonia/ammonium is reduced nitrogen that can enter biological construction.

AMS route:

```text
NH3/NH4+ -> glutamine/glutamate route -> amino acids / nucleotides / biomass
```

This is nitrogen in its biologically accessible reduced form. It is useful, but must be governed. Excess ammonia/ammonium can become toxic or ecologically disruptive.

## T1B-to-T1C/T1D Implication

At T1B, nitrogen can occupy many redox states. At T1C, life uses those states for nutrients, respiration, signalling, detoxification, and ecological circulation. At T1D2, human agriculture and industry amplify reactive nitrogen flows, causing productivity gains and systemic leakage.

## Confidence Assessment

### Evidence Strength

The evidence base is very strong:

- nitrogen oxidation states and species are well defined;
- nitrification, denitrification, DNRA, anammox, and fixation are established microbial routes;
- nitrate/nitrite/NO chemistry is well studied;
- NO signalling and RNS damage are strongly documented;
- N2O leakage is a major recognised environmental issue;
- anthropogenic reactive nitrogen expansion is well documented.

### AMS Model Strength

The AMS mapping is strong because:

- oxidation state gives a concrete state ladder;
- microbial pathways map naturally to route grammar;
- signalling versus stress fits governance-dependent state use;
- environmental leakage maps cleanly to partial route completion;
- cross-element dependencies align with oxygen, iron, sulfur, carbon, and phosphorus.

### Remaining Uncertainty

The uncertainty lies in scope and subdivision:

- `N5` overlaps strongly with `N6` signalling and `N8` ecology;
- each microbial route deserves a separate detailed map;
- quantitative scoring should eventually include redox potentials, enzyme rates, oxygen gradients, substrate availability, and ecosystem fluxes;
- nitrogen oxide chemistry is highly context-dependent.
