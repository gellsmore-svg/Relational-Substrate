# AMS T1C Stress-Test Corpus v1

## Purpose

This document is the first higher-order scaling test for the AMS substrate coherence programme.

The earlier pilot corpus focused on `T1B` grouped micro-organisation. That was necessary, but not sufficient.

The real pressure now is:

- when does grouped structure become a genuine higher-order whole?
- when does it fail to?
- can AMS scale from local grouped persistence to bounded higher-order identity without changing ontology?

This corpus is designed to answer that question provisionally and comparatively.

## Included T1C Cases In v1

The first stress-test cases are:

- water networks
- quartz
- glass
- metallic iron crystal / grain structures
- magnetite with domain relevance

These were selected because together they pressure:

- distributed rather than merely local order
- whole-dependent persistence
- defect formation
- threshold behaviour
- memory and hysteresis
- the boundary between T1B aggregation and true T1C whole formation

## Confidence Labels

Each case uses:

- `Observed`
- `Strong AMS inference`
- `Conjectural AMS extension`
- `Open`

## Confidence Score Rule

Each case also includes a `1-10 confidence score`.

That score means:

- how well constrained and comparatively defensible the AMS interpretation currently is within the pilot corpus

These scores are provisional and should later force recalibration of the `T1B` corpus.

## T1C Decision Rule

A case should only be treated as a real `T1C` whole if it shows most of the following:

- disturbance redistributes through the whole
- boundaries matter to identity
- the whole exhibits retention-properties not reducible to one constituent alone
- threshold response exists at the whole level
- the whole remains meaningful across limited part replacement
- the whole carries some degree of memory, preferred behaviour, or structured persistence through change

If those conditions remain weak, the case may still be better described as:

- advanced T1B aggregation
- or marginal whole formation

## Case 1: Water Networks

### Formation type

- hydrogen-bonded water networks

### Constituent basis

- stable `H2O` grouped micro-organisations

### Why this is a T1C test

Water is not important only because `H2O` is a stable molecule. It is important because many major behaviours depend on networked relation between molecules rather than on isolated molecules alone.

### Observed

Water exhibits large-scale behaviours that depend on relational structure:

- fluid coherence
- phase transition
- ice formation
- unusual thermal and structural behaviour
- network dependence

### AMS interpretation

Water networks are one of the clearest early candidates for real but dynamic T1C whole formation.

The key point is that the whole is not a simple pile of `H2O` molecules. The networked relation-patterns now do explanatory work.

- `Boundary significance`: moderate and context-dependent
- `Persistence profile`: real but dynamic rather than rigid
- `Memory behaviour`: limited and regime-dependent
- `Threshold behaviour`: strong
- `Failure modes`: incoherence, phase change, local network breakdown
- `Redistribution behaviour`: strong
- `Part-replacement tolerance`: high at local scale while whole behaviour persists
- `Higher-order identity assessment`: stable higher-order whole in many regimes, though often dynamically maintained rather than rigidly bounded

### Non-persistent topology pressure

Relevant classes:

- `Locally viable but globally incoherent`
- `Threshold-fragile`
- `Metastable-only`

These matter because local hydrogen-bond patterns do not always scale into the same whole-structure behaviour.

### Confidence

- `Observed`: strong
- `Strong AMS inference`: strong
- `Confidence score`: `8/10`

### Effect on earlier T1B scores

This strengthens the earlier `H2O` T1B interpretation because it shows the local grouped case does seem to scale coherently into a higher-order whole.

## Case 2: Quartz

### Formation type

- quartz as ordered silicon dioxide whole

### Constituent basis

- repeated `SiO2` network-supporting grouped closures

### Why this is a T1C test

Quartz is one of the strongest tests of whether repeated local grouped closure can become a stable, bounded, kind-bearing whole.

### Observed

Quartz shows:

- durable crystal structure
- clear whole-level persistence
- strong boundary significance
- defect-sensitive but robust ordered identity
- piezoelectric relevance under certain conditions

### AMS interpretation

Quartz is a very strong candidate for stable T1C whole formation.

The whole carries organised behaviour that is not reducible to one local `SiO2` grouping alone.

- `Boundary significance`: high
- `Persistence profile`: strong
- `Memory behaviour`: moderate through ordered structural retention
- `Threshold behaviour`: real, especially under fracture or defect conditions
- `Failure modes`: fracture, defect propagation, disordering
- `Redistribution behaviour`: strong at the whole-structure level
- `Part-replacement tolerance`: limited
- `Higher-order identity assessment`: stable higher-order whole, likely kind-bearing whole

### Non-persistent topology pressure

Relevant classes:

- `Locally viable but globally incoherent`
- `nucleates defect`
- `Threshold-fragile`

These matter because quartz shows how local viability can scale into strong whole coherence, but defects remain decisive.

### Confidence

- `Observed`: strong
- `Strong AMS inference`: very strong
- `Confidence score`: `9/10`

### Effect on earlier T1B scores

This raises the earlier `SiO2` T1B interpretation. The network-supporting grouped closure now has stronger evidence of meaningful T1C escalation.

## Case 3: Glass

### Formation type

- glass as higher-order disordered whole

### Constituent basis

- silicon-oxygen network-related grouped structures without the same ordered crystal coherence as quartz

### Why this is a T1C test

Glass is crucial because it pressures the ontology with a whole that is real and persistent, but not ordered in the same way as a crystal.

### Observed

Glass shows:

- stable whole identity
- amorphous or non-crystalline structure
- fracture behaviour
- whole-level persistence without crystal regularity

### AMS interpretation

Glass is a strong test of whether T1C identity requires perfect ordered repetition. AMS should say no.

Glass is a real whole, but one whose higher-order identity is carried by persistent distributed relation without the same regular lattice coherence as quartz.

- `Boundary significance`: high
- `Persistence profile`: strong
- `Memory behaviour`: moderate in strain and defect history
- `Threshold behaviour`: real, especially under fracture and thermal transition
- `Failure modes`: fracture, brittleness, local defect propagation
- `Redistribution behaviour`: weaker and less orderly than quartz
- `Part-replacement tolerance`: low
- `Higher-order identity assessment`: stable higher-order whole, but not a crystal-ordered kind in the same way as quartz

### Non-persistent topology pressure

Relevant classes:

- `Locally viable but globally incoherent`
- `Overconstrained`
- `Threshold-fragile`

Glass is a major test of whether AMS can distinguish:

- real whole identity
- from globally incoherent failure

without falsely forcing all wholes into crystal-like order.

### Confidence

- `Observed`: strong
- `Strong AMS inference`: strong
- `Confidence score`: `8/10`

### Effect on earlier T1B scores

This reinforces the importance of `SiO2`, but also warns against over-identifying whole formation with only one kind of order.

## Case 4: Metallic Iron Crystal / Grain Structures

### Formation type

- bulk metallic iron with grain structure

### Constituent basis

- metallic iron local relation cases

### Why this is a T1C test

Earlier corpus work already showed that metallic iron immediately pressures the boundary between T1B and T1C. Grain structure makes that pressure explicit.

### Observed

Bulk iron shows:

- whole-level persistence
- grain structure
- defect relevance
- deformation behaviour
- magnetic relevance

### AMS interpretation

Iron grain structure is a strong candidate for stable T1C whole formation, but it is also one of the clearest cases where:

- boundaries matter
- defects matter
- memory-like material behaviour matters

- `Boundary significance`: high
- `Persistence profile`: strong
- `Memory behaviour`: meaningful under strain and magnetic history
- `Threshold behaviour`: real under deformation, fracture, and phase-related change
- `Failure modes`: defect propagation, fracture, oxidation-driven transition
- `Redistribution behaviour`: strong across the material whole
- `Part-replacement tolerance`: low to moderate depending on scale
- `Higher-order identity assessment`: stable higher-order whole with strong defect- and grain-dependent behaviour

### Non-persistent topology pressure

Relevant classes:

- `nucleates defect`
- `Locally viable but globally incoherent`
- `Resonantly destabilised`

This case is important because it shows that a real higher-order whole can still carry strong local discontinuities of grain, defect, and strain.

### Confidence

- `Observed`: strong
- `Strong AMS inference`: moderate to strong
- `Confidence score`: `7/10`

### Effect on earlier T1B scores

This raises the score of the metallic iron local case slightly, but not dramatically. The T1C success is real, yet the T1B-to-T1C boundary remains conceptually difficult.

## Cross-Case Results

### 1. T1C identity is real and varied

The corpus now supports at least three different whole-formation profiles:

- dynamic network whole (`water networks`)
- ordered crystal whole (`quartz`)
- non-crystalline but real whole (`glass`)

This is important because AMS should not force all higher-order wholes into one model.

### 2. Defect and threshold behaviour are central

Every strong T1C case is shaped by:

- boundary significance
- defect significance
- threshold response

This means the ontology cannot stop at stable form. It must include:

- strain
- memory
- defect
- threshold fragility

as core higher-order realities.

### 3. Some earlier T1B scores should already move

The new corpus suggests provisional score adjustments:

- `H2O`: from `7` to `8`
- `SiO2`: from `7` to `8`
- metallic `Fe`: from `5` to `6`
- `Fe3O4`: from `6` to `7`

These are not final, but they show the comparative system working.

### 4. Quartz is currently the strongest T1C support case

Quartz currently appears to be the cleanest early case of:

- stable higher-order whole formation
- strong boundary significance
- durable kind-bearing persistence
- clear scaling from grouped basis to whole identity

That is why it currently receives the highest score in this corpus.

## Extended Cross-Case Results

### 5. T1C identity is not one thing

The extended corpus now supports several distinct whole-formation profiles:

- dynamic network whole
- ordered crystal whole
- disordered but persistent whole
- boundary-defined whole
- mixed metallic whole
- function-bearing biological whole

This is one of the strongest positive results so far. AMS does not need to force all wholes into a single shape to keep them ontologically real.

### 6. Boundary significance is more important than first expected

Membranes and catalytic surfaces especially show that some wholes are defined as much by boundary behaviour as by bulk interior order.

This should later pressure the framework language itself.

### 7. Functional wholeness is now unavoidable

Biological assemblies and catalytic surfaces show that some wholes must be described not only by persistence, but by:

- selective interaction
- organised response
- maintained role within a larger system

That means the bridge from structure to teleology now needs sharper handling in later corpus layers.

### 8. The highest current T1C support cases

Current strongest cases in the corpus appear to be:

- quartz
- water networks
- membranes
- glass

These currently seem to give the clearest support for real higher-order wholeness under AMS without demanding too much speculative patching.
