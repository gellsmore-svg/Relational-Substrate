# AMS Iron Magnetic Biomineralization Route Map v1

## Purpose

This note maps iron's `I7` role:

```text
I7: mineral-magnetic identity carrier
```

The primary high-confidence case is magnetotactic bacteria.

The central claim is:

> Iron can be biologically converted into governed magnetic mineral identity: a cell can concentrate iron, tune redox and pH, nucleate magnetite or greigite, control crystal size and shape, align crystals into chains, and use the resulting magnetic moment for orientation in Earth's geomagnetic field.

This is a major AMS case because it shows a direct T1B mineral property becoming T1C navigational function.

## Source Anchors

The source base supports these facts:

- Magnetotactic bacteria biomineralize iron into magnetic nanoparticles.
- The magnetic minerals are commonly magnetite (`Fe3O4`) or greigite (`Fe3S4`).
- These nanoparticles are formed inside specialised membrane-bounded organelles called magnetosomes.
- Magnetosomes contain magnetosome-associated proteins that regulate iron concentration, redox state, pH, nucleation, crystal size, crystal shape, and chain arrangement.
- Magnetosome formation is genetically controlled and associated with magnetosome gene islands, including `mam` and `mms` genes.
- Magnetosome formation can be described in major steps: membrane invagination/protein sorting, chain alignment, ion transport/local environment control, and crystal nucleation/growth/shape control.
- Magnetosome crystals often fall within stable single-domain magnetic size ranges and have narrow size distributions and high crystalline order.
- Chains of magnetosomes provide a permanent magnetic dipole, allowing magnetotactic bacteria to align along geomagnetic field lines and navigate toward favourable oxic-anoxic zones.
- Magnetosome crystal morphology and chain arrangement vary across species.
- Local iron accumulation can precede nucleation and growth.
- Magnetosome chemical conditions involve controlled pH and redox windows.
- Biogenic magnetite differs chemically and structurally from many abiotic magnetites, though identifying ancient magnetofossils can be challenging.

## Testable Expectations

If the AMS `I7` model is coherent, the following should hold:

1. Magnetosome formation should depend on bounded compartmental control, not simple iron precipitation.
2. Iron concentration, redox state, and pH should be tightly linked to successful crystal formation.
3. Crystal size and shape should affect magnetic function.
4. Chain alignment should produce cell-level behaviour beyond isolated particle magnetism.
5. Genetic disruption of magnetosome proteins should produce predictable failures in vesicle formation, crystal morphology, or chain organisation.
6. Biogenic magnetite should differ from abiotic magnetite in physical, chemical, or organisational signatures.
7. Magnetosome function should be coupled with motility and chemical-gradient sensing rather than acting alone.

These expectations align strongly with the source literature for magnetotactic bacteria.
