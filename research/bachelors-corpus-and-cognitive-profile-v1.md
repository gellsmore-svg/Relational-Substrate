Status: sourced adaptation reference

## Purpose

This document converts external undergraduate writing and textbook guidance into a practical editorial corpus for the Bachelor’s edition of `Coherent Biblical Ontology`.

It is not a list of random “simpler words.” It is a communication profile for:
- vocabulary level
- sentence behavior
- transition style
- paragraph architecture
- chapter cognition load
- acceptable abstraction density

## High-Level Synthesis

Inference from the sources:

- Bachelor’s readers can track abstraction if the argument path is visible.
- They do not need constant simplification, but they do need overt structure.
- They tolerate technical vocabulary better than hidden dependency chains.
- They benefit from paragraphs that carry one main move and clearly connect to the chapter thesis.
- They expect learning objectives, definitions, examples, and summaries to perform navigational work.
- They are more comfortable with a term when it appears in a stable form, with a clear function, than when it is repeatedly paraphrased.

## Bachelor’s Cognitive Load Model

### Safe Processing Load

Per chapter, a bachelor’s reader can usually track:
- 1 primary claim
- 2 to 4 major distinctions
- 1 mechanism proposal cluster
- 1 theological frame cluster
- 1 forward implication cluster

### Unsafe Processing Load

Comprehension begins to degrade when a chapter:
- introduces more than 4 fresh major distinctions without visible hierarchy
- mixes ontology, scripture, mechanism, and implication in the same paragraph without signposting
- assumes the reader will remember a technical distinction from 3 chapters earlier without reactivation
- uses multiple near-synonyms for the same core concept

### Best Cognitive Sequence

Best sequence for this audience:
1. chapter question
2. chapter stakes
3. main distinction
4. argument development
5. example or stress test
6. what the chapter has now established
7. handoff to the next chapter

Rule:

- Band C must be introduced with clear local function and not merely named.

## Preferred Bachelor’s Expressions

These expressions fit the external undergraduate guidance well because they are explicit, orderly, and argument-carrying.

### Chapter-Stakes Expressions

- `The next question is...`
- `What this chapter must show is...`
- `This matters because...`
- `The pressure point here is...`
- `At this point the argument requires...`

### Dependency Expressions

- `Once this is in place...`
- `If that distinction holds...`
- `What follows from this is...`
- `This becomes decisive when...`
- `That claim matters later because...`

### Status-Marking Expressions

- `The baseline claim is...`
- `What follows is a proposed mechanism-facing account...`
- `This does not mean Scripture directly teaches...`
- `The ontological point is...`
- `The mechanism question is separate...`

### Contrast Expressions

- `This is not the same as saying...`
- `The point is not that... but that...`
- `That explanation can still stand, but it is not enough...`
- `The issue is not whether... but what kind of...`

### Chapter-Ending Expressions

- `What this chapter establishes is...`
- `That leaves the next question...`
- `The argument can now move from... to...`
- `The next chapter must therefore ask...`

## Expressions To Reduce

These are not forbidden, but should not become reflexive:
- `That is why...`
- `This also means...`
- `Put differently...`
- `In this sense...`
- `It should be clear that...`
- `Obviously...`

Reason:
- Harvard and Purdue both emphasize helping the reader see relationships rather than repeatedly flagging them with generic transition markers.
- Repeated generic pivots create rhythm fatigue and hide actual dependency logic.

## Paragraph Architecture Rules From External Guidance

Derived from Harvard’s paragraph and organization guidance:

- each paragraph should have one main point
- the opening sentence should do double duty:
  - make the local claim
  - show its relevance to the larger argument
- if a paragraph contains two major moves, it should probably be split
- topic-sentence quality matters more than paragraph length alone

Editorial implication for AMS:
- every dense paragraph in Chapters 2, 4, 5, 8, 12, 14, and 15 should be checked for multiple hidden moves

## Transition Rule From External Guidance

Derived from Harvard’s transition guidance:

Readers follow arguments more easily when new sentences begin from known information before introducing new information.

Editorial implication for AMS:
- chapter transitions should begin from the prior chapter’s settled gain
- section transitions should begin from the established distinction, not from a fresh abstraction
- avoid transitions that begin with a conclusion word but no relational logic

## OpenStax-Derived Communication Behaviors

OpenStax undergraduate physics materials consistently rely on:
- learning objectives
- key terms
- worked examples
- summaries
- explicit sectional outcomes

Inference for AMS:

The Bachelor’s edition should borrow the communication logic, not the textbook tone.

This means:
- chapter-purpose framing
- stable term introduction
- occasional “what this establishes” endings
- examples that demonstrate explanatory gain
- appendix use for overflow detail

## Editorial Rules This Corpus Implies

1. Prefer stable technical terms over rotating paraphrase.
2. Prefer visible hierarchy over rhetorical flow alone.
3. Prefer one main move per paragraph.
4. Prefer chapter-purpose framing at the front.
5. Prefer chapter-end retention cues.
6. Prefer examples that test claims, not decorate prose.
7. Prefer explicit status markers in high-density chapters.
8. Prefer appendix transfer for overload, not concept deletion.

## How This Should Be Used

This corpus should guide:
- the Chapter 4 pilot adaptation
- line-edit decisions in the Bachelor’s edition
- glossary selection
- whether a phrase is too compressed for this audience
- whether a paragraph needs splitting

It should not be used to:
- flatten the ontology
- remove difficult terms merely because they are technical
- replace rigorous language with vagueness

## Bottom Line

The Bachelor’s edition should sound like:
- a serious undergraduate-facing argument
- structurally explicit
- technically literate
- theologically disciplined
- cognitively trackable

It should not sound like:
- a graduate seminar transcript
- a popularized paraphrase
- a textbook stripped of metaphysical force

The right target is controlled intellectual accessibility.
