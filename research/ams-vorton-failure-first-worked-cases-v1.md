# AMS Vorton Failure-First Worked Cases v1

## Purpose

This note applies the corrected vorton ontology and `I_profile` gate to worked cases.

The method is failure-first. A candidate is not allowed to become a vorton merely because it is interesting, coherent, energetic, topological, or persistent at a secondary scale.

The governing chain remains:

```text
continuous substrate
-> local organised disturbance
-> torsion/tension/alignment expression
-> candidate closure-pattern
-> stable primary topology/vorton
-> coupled vorton regimes
-> perceived matter
```

Every case below asks:

```text
where does the chain stop?
```

## Case 1: Light-Like Transient Propagation

### Description

A coherent light-like transient travels through an ordered substrate route and through a permissive medium.

It may show:

- phase coherence
- route continuity
- refraction-like delay
- reflection or transmission at boundaries
- absorption or scattering under poor route compatibility

### `I_profile` Check

```text
C_class   weak or absent stable closure
W_sig     no stable self-returning signature
H_sig     may have polarisation-like orientation, but not stable closure identity
R_map     route handoff, not self-return
B_env     not bounded as a persistent identity
Q_band    high coherence during propagation
L_band    route-dependent attenuation
S_env     transient
K_couple  interacts with matter routes
M_reseat  not identity-preserving reseating
F_modes   absorption, scattering, decoherence, transmission loss
```

### Verdict

```text
not a vorton
```

Reason:

The transient may be coherent, lawful, measurable, and route-sensitive, but it does not possess stable primary closure. It is propagation, not persistent topological identity.

### Formation Chain Stop

```text
torsion/tension/alignment expression
-> transient propagation
-> no stable closure-pattern
```

## Case 2: Dielectric Lock-Up

### Description

An electrical route encounters a non-transmissive or weakly transmissive regime. Slip is blocked, so mismatch stores as polarisation-like lock-up.

It may show:

- stored mismatch
- recoverable response
- interface charge behaviour
- delayed relaxation
- breakdown if thresholds are exceeded

### `I_profile` Check

```text
C_class   stored constraint, not necessarily self-returning closure
W_sig     absent unless a genuine return loop forms
H_sig     may have orientation bias
R_map     mostly blocked or stored, not self-returning
B_env     boundary-dependent
Q_band    medium-dependent
L_band    relaxation and leakage significant
S_env     can persist as material state, often T2
K_couple  strong at interfaces
M_reseat  reversible polarisation or breakdown
F_modes   leakage, relaxation, breakdown, interface failure
```

### Verdict

```text
not automatically a vorton
```

Reason:

Dielectric lock-up may be persistent and route-bearing, but persistence at a secondary material scale is not the same as primary vorton identity. It becomes vorton-relevant only if a local closure-pattern forms and preserves invariant identity.

### Formation Chain Stop

```text
local organised disturbance
-> stored mismatch
-> T2 material response
-> no stable primary closure demonstrated
```

## Case 3: Magnetic Alignment Domain

### Description

A magnetic material contains a domain: a region of coordinated alignment within a secondary material regime.

It may show:

- remanence
- hysteresis
- domain-wall movement
- Barkhausen-like event structure
- alignment persistence

### `I_profile` Check

```text
C_class   secondary alignment regime
W_sig     domain-scale relation, not necessarily primary return signature
H_sig     strong orientation signature
R_map     material-scale alignment reseating
B_env     domain boundary dependent
Q_band    depends on defects and pinning
L_band    not the main identity criterion
S_env     stable under some material conditions
K_couple  strong magnetic coupling
M_reseat  wall motion, rotation, depinning
F_modes   domain-wall collapse, reversal, fragmentation, thermal agitation
```

### Verdict

```text
T2 regime, not automatically a vorton
```

Reason:

A magnetic domain may depend on vorton-bearing matter, but the domain itself is an aggregate secondary topology unless primary closure identity is demonstrated.

### Formation Chain Stop

```text
coupled vorton regimes
-> secondary material alignment
-> magnetic domain
```

This is after vorton-level identity, not identical with it.

### `I_profile` Check

```text

H_sig     weak
R_map     fails to return
B_env     below threshold
Q_band    weak or temporary
L_band    leakage above threshold

M_reseat  no identity-preserving reseating
```

### Verdict

```text
failed candidate, not a proto-vorton
```

The candidate lacks inward boundedness. It does not earn proto-vorton language.

### Formation Chain Stop

```text
candidate closure-pattern
-> leakage
-> diffusion
```

## Case 5: Collapse-Prone Candidate Closure

### Description

A local disturbance concentrates strongly and appears highly bounded, but the retained constraint is too narrow or overloaded. It collapses rather than distributing returnable closure.

### `I_profile` Check

```text
C_class   overconcentrated
W_sig     may briefly appear
H_sig     may be strong but unstable
R_map     overloaded
B_env     high inward boundedness, low distributedness
Q_band    brittle
L_band    low leakage but high collapse risk
S_env     unstable
K_couple  potentially disruptive
M_reseat  fails under load
F_modes   collapse-prone topology
```

### Verdict

```text
failed candidate, not a stable vorton
```

Reason:

Strong concentration is not stable closure. Without distributedness and returnability, the candidate fails by collapse.

### Formation Chain Stop

```text
candidate closure-pattern
-> overconcentration
-> collapse
```

### `I_profile` Check

```text
C_class   partial closure
W_sig     incomplete
H_sig     possible
R_map     below threshold
B_env     may be moderate
Q_band    partial
L_band    leakage through failed return
S_env     unstable

```

### Verdict

```text
failed candidate, possible pre-proto pattern only
```

```text
candidate closure-pattern

```

## Case 7: Proto-Vorton That Decays

### Description

A candidate crosses several thresholds. It has partial closure, some boundedness, some returnability, and temporary coherence. For a time, it behaves like a near-stable closure-pattern.

Then leakage, fragmentation, incompatible adjacency, or repeated perturbation breaks the invariant profile.

### `I_profile` Check

```text
C_class   emerging
W_sig     present but not durable
H_sig     may be discriminating
R_map     present but fragile
B_env     above threshold briefly
Q_band    narrow
L_band    eventually exceeded
S_env     metastable
K_couple  weak or unstable
M_reseat  limited
F_modes   threshold-fragile, oscillatory, leakage decay
```

### Verdict

```text
proto-vorton, not stable vorton
```

Reason:

The candidate deserves proto-vorton language because it crosses meaningful formation thresholds. It does not deserve stable vorton identity because `I_profile` is not preserved across the required perturbation window.

### Formation Chain Stop

```text
proto-vorton
-> invariant profile loss
-> decay or reconfiguration
```

## Case 8: Stable Vorton Candidate

### Description

A narrow class of candidate closure-pattern reaches bounded, distributed, returnable, coherent organisation. It preserves an invariant profile across perturbation and can couple with other stable closures without losing identity.

### `I_profile` Check

```text
C_class   stable closure class
W_sig     preserved return signature
H_sig     defined or explicitly absent
R_map     returnable and resilient
B_env     bounded across tolerance range
Q_band    recovers after tolerable perturbation
L_band    leakage below identity-loss threshold
S_env     defined stability envelope
K_couple  repeatable coupling behaviour
M_reseat  identity-preserving reseating allowed
F_modes   defined and bounded
```

Minimum gate:

```text
B_in(V) > B_min
D_spread(V) > D_min
R_return(V) > R_min
Q_coh(V) > Q_min
L_leak(V) < L_max
X_frag(V) < X_max
I_profile(V,t) preserved across N_min perturbation cycles
```

### Verdict

```text
stable vorton candidate
```

Reason:

This is the first case in the sequence that can carry stable primary topological identity. It still remains a candidate until the model supplies a stronger substrate metric and invariant mathematics, but it passes the conceptual vorton gate.

### Formation Chain Continued

```text
stable primary topology/vorton
-> coupling with other vortons
-> secondary material regimes
-> perceived matter
```

## Matter Formation Implication

Perceived matter requires more than one stable vorton candidate in isolation.

The next formation step is:

```text
stable vorton identities
-> repeatable coupling behaviour
-> persistent secondary topology
-> material-scale boundary, resistance, route-bearing, and measurement behaviour
```

Matter is perceived when secondary regimes interact lawfully and repeatedly with route structures, including light-like propagation, electrical response, magnetic response, instruments, bodies, and observers.

## Current Result

The failure-first pass gives the vorton programme a sharper discipline:

- light-like propagation is not a vorton
- dielectric lock-up is not automatically a vorton
- magnetic domains are secondary regimes, not automatically vortons
- weak closure attempts usually fail
- concentrated closure attempts may collapse
- partial return attempts are underreturned
- proto-vortons can decay
- only invariant-preserving closure earns stable vorton-candidate status

The next required file is a matter-formation bridge showing how stable vorton identities couple into secondary regimes without turning `T0` into a hidden lattice.
