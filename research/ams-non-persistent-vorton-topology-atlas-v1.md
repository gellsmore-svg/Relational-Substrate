# AMS Non-Persistent Vorton Topology Atlas v1

## Purpose

This atlas defines the first negative-space taxonomy for AMS.

Its job is simple:

- identify classes of closure-pattern that do not become stably persistent
- explain why they fail
- provide a common language for analysing how unstable or transient topology may still interact with stable `T1B` formations and higher-order `T1C` wholes

AMS should not explain only what survives. It should also explain why many candidate closure-patterns:

- dissipate
- collapse
- oscillate without settling
- remain metastable only
- destabilise more stable formations

This atlas is therefore a necessary counterpart to the stable molecular corpus.

## Governing Principle

The taxonomy below is ontological rather than directly observational.

That means:

- the classes are AMS interpretive tools
- they are not claimed as direct textbook objects
- they must still be constrained by known behaviour in chemistry and materials science

Every class therefore needs four things:

1. a clear AMS definition
2. a failure logic
3. a runtime signature
4. an interaction profile

## Core Taxonomy

## 1. Diffuse Topology

### Definition

A candidate closure-pattern whose local organisation is too weakly bounded to retain itself against surrounding redistribution.

### Failure logic

The pattern does not achieve enough local containment. Strain and organisation leak away faster than stable closure can reseat itself.

### Runtime signature

- rapid dissipation
- low retention
- no durable identity
- transient local effect only

### Likely interaction profile

- weak scattering
- local disturbance
- little or no stable binding
- possible contribution to background instability

### T1B relevance

Diffuse regimes are likely to fail to produce stable grouped micro-organisation unless supported by stronger neighbouring structures.

### Confidence

- `Strong AMS inference`
- `Confidence score`: `7/10`

## 2. Collapse-Prone Topology

### Definition

A candidate closure-pattern whose organisation is bounded enough to form locally but too concentrated to distribute strain safely.

### Failure logic

The regime overfocuses constraint. Return-paths are too narrow or too overloaded, so the topology implodes, snaps, or deforms into a different regime.

### Runtime signature

- abrupt collapse
- short-lived local intensity
- threshold-triggered failure
- unstable local overconcentration

### Likely interaction profile

- puncturing or destabilising adjacent closures
- triggering local reconfiguration
- contributing to defect nucleation

### T1B relevance

Collapse-prone regimes may appear near highly reactive, highly strained, or threshold-fragile local structures.

### Confidence

- `Strong AMS inference`
- `Confidence score`: `7/10`

## 3. Overconstrained Topology

### Definition

A candidate closure-pattern whose internal conditions are too rigidly specified to permit workable reseating, slip, or adaptation under runtime perturbation.

### Failure logic

The regime can form locally, but it lacks enough admissible flexibility to remain viable across ordinary disturbance.

### Runtime signature

- brittleness
- threshold-fragility
- low adaptive tolerance
- sudden failure when strain is redistributed

### Likely interaction profile

- fracture trigger
- low slip compatibility
- threshold break under small perturbation once load accumulates

### T1B relevance

Overconstrained regimes may map well onto cases where local ordering is strong but brittle, especially where stable form depends on narrow admissibility windows.

### Confidence

- `Conjectural AMS extension`
- `Confidence score`: `5/10`

## 4. Underreturned Topology

### Definition

A candidate closure-pattern that can build local strain but cannot return that strain through a sufficiently coherent closed path.

### Failure logic

The closure begins to form, but the loop does not close strongly enough. Strain escapes, fragments, or fails to re-enter the regime in a sustaining way.

### Runtime signature

- incomplete loop behaviour
- low persistence
- partial local coherence
- repeated failed reseating

### Likely interaction profile

- transient attachment
- weak catalytic behaviour
- degraded closure support

### T1B relevance

This class may be especially important wherever transient intermediates appear to form but do not stably persist.

### Confidence

- `Strong AMS inference`
- `Confidence score`: `7/10`

## 5. Unstable Oscillatory Topology

### Definition

A candidate closure-pattern that does not dissipate immediately but remains trapped in self-intensifying or non-settling oscillation.

### Failure logic

The regime carries energy and pattern, but it cannot enter stable lock-up. It cycles through near-viable states without reaching persistent closure.

### Runtime signature

- recurring unstable oscillation
- repeated near-lock states
- threshold-sensitive escalation
- possible resonant amplification

### Likely interaction profile

- resonance with neighbouring regimes
- destabilisation through amplification
- threshold transfer into collapse or reseating

### T1B relevance

This class matters where oscillatory activity appears before stable form, or where resonance destabilises otherwise stable groupings.

### Confidence

- `Conjectural AMS extension`
- `Confidence score`: `5/10`

## 6. Incompatible Adjacency Topology

### Definition

A candidate closure-pattern that may be locally viable in itself but fails because its neighbourhood relations are not admissible.

### Failure logic

The problem is not only internal. The surrounding topology does not permit coherent coupling, slip, or return-path continuity.

### Runtime signature

- local instability near boundaries
- failure to bind or align
- repeated rejection at interfaces

### Likely interaction profile

- non-binding collisions
- boundary instability
- defect formation
- local scattering

### T1B relevance

This class is important for interface behaviour, heterogeneous environments, crystal defects, phase boundaries, and selective interaction.

### Confidence

- `Strong AMS inference`
- `Confidence score`: `7/10`

## 7. Threshold-Fragile Topology

### Definition

A candidate closure-pattern that is stable across a narrow band of conditions but fails sharply once a threshold is crossed.

### Failure logic

The regime is viable, but only within tight limits of strain, temperature, alignment, neighbouring support, or other runtime conditions.

### Runtime signature

- apparently stable until sudden change
- high threshold sensitivity
- cascade potential once failure begins

### Likely interaction profile

- rapid phase-like change
- local-to-global transition
- threshold-triggered redistribution

### T1B relevance

Threshold-fragile regimes are likely to matter wherever persistence exists, but only under narrow supporting conditions.

### Confidence

- `Strong AMS inference`
- `Confidence score`: `8/10`

## 8. Metastable-Only Topology

### Definition

A candidate closure-pattern that is genuinely persistent for a time but remains burdened by unresolved strain and does not represent the lowest viable stable regime.

### Failure logic

The regime is real and can endure, but it carries hidden pressure toward later transition, decay, reconfiguration, or release.

### Runtime signature

- persistent but strained
- delayed transformation
- sensitivity to perturbation
- non-final stability

### Likely interaction profile

- temporary binding
- delayed destabilisation
- potential threshold release
- pathway to reconfiguration

### T1B relevance

This class is central for the pilot because many interesting chemical and material cases are not simply stable or unstable. They are metastable.

### Confidence

- `Strong AMS inference`
- `Confidence score`: `8/10`

## 9. Locally Viable But Globally Incoherent Topology

### Definition

A candidate closure-pattern in which local sub-regimes are viable, but the larger grouped formation fails to cohere into a meaningful whole.

### Failure logic

Neighbouring local structures can each persist, but their combined relation-pattern does not support stable higher-order organisation.

### Runtime signature

- patchy local order
- low whole-system coherence
- weak redistribution across the larger pattern
- no robust whole identity

### Likely interaction profile

- granular clustering
- frustrated aggregation
- defect-rich grouped behaviour

### T1B relevance

This class is especially important at the T1B to T1C boundary, where many grouped units may fail to become a real whole.

### Confidence

- `Strong AMS inference`
- `Confidence score`: `7/10`

## 10. Resonantly Destabilised Topology

### Definition

A candidate closure-pattern that would otherwise remain viable, but is driven into instability by resonant interaction with other regimes or repeated forcing.

### Failure logic

The pattern is not intrinsically impossible. It fails because external periodic or patterned forcing aligns too well with vulnerable modes.

### Runtime signature

- amplified strain
- mode locking into instability
- repeated forcing leading to structural failure

### Likely interaction profile

- resonance-triggered breakdown
- sympathetic destabilisation
- oscillatory transfer into threshold failure

### T1B relevance

This class matters wherever resonance, repeated forcing, or environmental periodicity changes persistence behaviour.

### Confidence

- `Conjectural AMS extension`
- `Confidence score`: `5/10`

## Cross-Class Comparison

The atlas should be used comparatively rather than as a list of disconnected labels.

### Main contrasts

- `Diffuse` versus `Collapse-prone`
  - too little boundedness versus too much destructive concentration
- `Underreturned` versus `Overconstrained`
  - failure to close versus closure too rigid to survive runtime disturbance
- `Threshold-fragile` versus `Metastable-only`
  - narrow stability band versus real but strained temporary persistence
- `Locally viable but globally incoherent` versus `T1C whole`
  - local success without whole identity versus real higher-order persistence

## Interaction Modes To Track

Every later molecular or material case should be tested against these interaction possibilities:

- `destabilises`
- `temporarily binds`
- `scatters`
- `catalyses reconfiguration`
- `nucleates defect`
- `triggers threshold change`
- `amplifies by resonance`
- `dissipates harmlessly`

These are the minimum interaction tags for the pilot corpus.

## Priority Links To Pilot Elements

The atlas should be applied early to the pilot set like this:

### High priority

- `H`
  - transient proton-related regimes
  - hydrogen-bond support and failure
- `O`
  - reactive transition and threshold behaviour
- `Si`
  - local stability versus larger structural coherence
- `Fe`
  - strain, memory, defect, and magnetic-order relevance

### Medium priority

- `C`
  - closure diversity and ring / chain viability
- `N`
  - high local stability versus reactive regime change
- `S`
  - ring persistence and oxidation diversity

### Supporting priority

- `Na`
- `Mg`
- `P`

These should still be covered, but the first explanatory pressure will likely come from the higher-priority set above.

## What This Atlas Is For

This atlas should allow later research to ask better questions:

- Why does this formation not persist?
- Why is this one only metastable?
- Why does this grouped system fail to become a whole?
- Why does resonance stabilise here but destabilise there?
- Why does local viability fail to scale?

Those questions are necessary if AMS is to become a real coherence-testing framework rather than a vocabulary set for successful cases only.
