# AMS Vorton Model Family Catalogue v1

## Purpose

This catalogue is the first concrete model-space document for the AMS vorton experimental modelling programme.

Its task is to define a bounded set of candidate mechanism families that can be:

- compared
- parameterised
- geometrically explored
- mathematically formalised later

The catalogue does not pretend that one final vorton model has already been discovered.

It exists to support experimentation.

## Governing Rule

Each family must be:

- clear enough to distinguish from the others
- flexible enough to support parameter variation
- concrete enough to generate different predictions under boundary, transmission, reflection, defect, and whole-formation tests

## Shared Parameters

Each family should eventually be describable using some or all of these variables:

- `T` = torsion magnitude
- `C` = closure tightness
- `R` = return-path strength
- `K` = knotting or linking complexity
- `J` = coupling strength to neighbours
- `S` = slip tolerance
- `Q` = resonance compatibility
- `B` = boundary sensitivity
- `D` = defect sensitivity
- `M` = memory retention

These are placeholders, not final mathematical definitions.

## Family A: Simple Closed Torsion Loop

### Core Idea

A local torsional regime closes on itself and remains persistent because bounded local circulation plus return-path viability preserve identity.

### Main strengths

- simplest baseline identity case
- strong candidate for minimal persistent grouped micro-organisation
- useful as a control family

### Main weaknesses

- may be too simple for distributed or higher-order behaviours
- may underperform in boundary-heavy or optical interaction cases

### Likely best-use cases

- baseline local persistence
- simple stable grouped identities
- comparison against richer models

### Likely parameter sensitivities

- high sensitivity to `T`, `C`, and `R`
- lower richness in `K` and `M`

## Family B: Distributed Ring Closure

### Core Idea

Persistence depends not on one narrow local loop alone, but on distributed closure across a ring-like or recurrent relation pattern.

### Main strengths

- better candidate for ring persistence
- better distributed strain handling than Family A
- likely relevant for aromatic or repeated closure analogies

### Main weaknesses

- may still be too limited for larger nested whole formation
- may become ambiguous at defect-rich boundaries

### Likely best-use cases

- ring-like grouped persistence
- recurrent distributed closure
- local-to-whole relation experiments

### Likely parameter sensitivities

- strong sensitivity to `K`, `R`, and distributed `J`

## Family C: Nested Closure

### Core Idea

Smaller closures participate inside a larger enclosing closure, producing higher-order persistence not reducible to local members alone.

### Main strengths

- strong candidate for T1B to T1C scaling
- supports higher-order whole formation
- promising for crystal, membrane, and layered whole cases

### Main weaknesses

- can become too permissive if nesting conditions are not tightly controlled
- risks verbal inflation without good whole-formation criteria

### Likely best-use cases

- crystal wholes
- membrane-like wholes
- repeated higher-order material organisation

### Likely parameter sensitivities

- strong sensitivity to `J`, `R`, `B`, and `D`

## Family D: Resonant Dynamic Closure

### Core Idea

Persistence depends on recurring dynamic coherence rather than static lock-up alone.

### Main strengths

- strong candidate for dynamic network wholes
- likely relevant for water networks, oscillatory systems, and some living wholes
- naturally suited to threshold and resonance questions

### Main weaknesses

- may collapse into instability if resonance conditions are too weakly constrained
- may blur with unstable oscillatory cases unless stabilising criteria are defined clearly

### Likely best-use cases

- dynamic wholes
- networked media
- regulated systems
- propagation experiments

### Likely parameter sensitivities

- strong sensitivity to `Q`, `S`, `R`, and `M`

## Family E: Boundary-Concentrated Functional Closure

### Core Idea

The most important organised behaviour is concentrated at the boundary or interface rather than distributed uniformly through bulk structure.

### Main strengths

- strong candidate for catalytic surfaces
- strong candidate for membrane function
- promising for optical boundary phenomena

### Main weaknesses

- can be overused if every boundary-active case is forced into it
- needs careful distinction between constitutive and secondary boundary behaviour

### Likely best-use cases

- catalytic surfaces
- membrane transport
- metal reflection
- interface transitions

### Likely parameter sensitivities

- strong sensitivity to `B`, `Q`, `D`, and interface-specific `J`

## Family F: Defect-Responsive Closure

### Core Idea

The model includes defect, discontinuity, and retained history as structurally real rather than as later perturbations added onto an ideal system.

### Main strengths

- strong candidate for metals, crystals, and hysteresis-bearing wholes
- supports memory and path dependence
- naturally suited to fracture, fatigue, and domain-like history effects

### Main weaknesses

- can become too broad if defect logic is not tightly specified
- may require more mathematical formalisation sooner than the other families

### Likely best-use cases

- metals
- crystals
- hysteresis
- boundary / defect optical effects

### Likely parameter sensitivities

- strong sensitivity to `D`, `M`, `B`, and strain-linked `R`

## Family G: Pathway-Selective Propagation Closure

### Core Idea

Propagation is governed less by uniform medium behaviour and more by the availability of admissible pathways through a structured relation field.

### Main strengths

- very promising for optical transmission questions
- useful for glass vs crystal differences
- naturally connects propagation, scattering, and defect-sensitive transmission

### Main weaknesses

- may overlap with Family D or E unless propagation rules are carefully distinguished
- could become too descriptive if pathway criteria stay vague

### Likely best-use cases

- light transmission through glass
- crystal propagation
- defect-driven scattering
- interface-selective propagation

### Likely parameter sensitivities

- strong sensitivity to `S`, `B`, `D`, `Q`, and distributed `J`

## Family H: Collective Surface-Response Closure

### Core Idea

A whole responds to incoming disturbance collectively at or near the surface rather than merely through independent local interactions.

### Main strengths

- strong candidate for metal reflection
- useful for explaining whole-level surface response without reducing it to isolated local events
- may bridge metallic order, boundary activity, and optical reflection

### Main weaknesses

- may be difficult to distinguish from boundary-concentrated functional closure unless collective response is formalised clearly
- could become hand-wavy without stronger whole-response criteria

### Likely best-use cases

- reflection from metals
- surface-driven optical response
- boundary-wide coherent response experiments

### Likely parameter sensitivities

- strong sensitivity to `B`, `J`, `Q`, and whole-level `R`

## Family I: Gradient-Coupled Functional Closure

### Core Idea

Whole behaviour depends on maintained gradients or asymmetries that are not accidental side conditions, but part of the whole’s persistent organisation.

### Main strengths

- strong candidate for ATP synthase-like systems
- useful for membrane-linked energy conversion
- may illuminate some regulated optical or transport asymmetries

### Main weaknesses

- more biologically or functionally loaded than the earlier material families
- may be too advanced for early material-only experiments

### Likely best-use cases

- biological energy conversion
- regulated transport systems
- strong boundary-asymmetry wholes

### Likely parameter sensitivities

- strong sensitivity to `B`, `Q`, `R`, `M`, and directional `J`

## Family J: Multi-Scale Hybrid Closure

### Core Idea

A whole is sustained by different closure logics acting together at different scales.

### Main strengths

- likely needed for the most complex cases
- can model systems where local closure, distributed boundary behaviour, and retained history all matter together

### Main weaknesses

- highest risk of becoming a catch-all
- should only be used after simpler family combinations fail

### Likely best-use cases

- the most complex living wholes
- mixed material-biological cases
- difficult optical-material interfaces

### Likely parameter sensitivities

- broad sensitivity across all variables

## Family Comparison Axes

The model families should be compared along these axes:

- local vs distributed closure
- static vs dynamic persistence
- bulk vs boundary concentration
- defect-insensitive vs defect-responsive
- memory-poor vs memory-rich
- weakly functional vs strongly function-bearing
- poor vs rich propagation behaviour

- `Family E: Boundary-Concentrated Functional Closure`

### Highest priority for material whole benchmarks

- `Family C: Nested Closure`
- `Family F: Defect-Responsive Closure`
- `Family B: Distributed Ring Closure`

### Highest priority for living / organism-linked benchmarks

- `Family D: Resonant Dynamic Closure`
- `Family I: Gradient-Coupled Functional Closure`
- `Family J: Multi-Scale Hybrid Closure`

## Warning Against Premature Elegance

The existence of ten families does not mean all ten are equally good or equally real.

The programme should expect:

- some families to collapse into others
- some to prove redundant
- some to survive only as limiting cases
- some to become much richer than they first appear

That is normal for an experimental model-space.
