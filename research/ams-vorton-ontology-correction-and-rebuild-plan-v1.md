# AMS Vorton Ontology Correction and Rebuild Plan v1

## Purpose

This note corrects a drift in the recent supporting research on vortons, magnetism, electricity, light, and ordered darkness.

The useful part of that research is its insistence on topology, interfaces, thresholds, route behaviour, and local admissibility. The wrong part is its tendency to let modelling terms such as `cells`, `edges`, `faces`, `cell complexes`, and `lattices` slide from formal notation into ontology.

That slide must be stopped.

AMS does not need a discretised material substrate between `T0` and vortons. If it introduces one, the framework loses one of its central claims: that stable matter arises as primary topological closure in a continuous substrate, not as arrangements of smaller material pellets.

## Correction Summary

The corrected stack is:

```text
T0: continuous substrate
T1: stable primary topology, including vortons
T2: secondary material regimes built from stable primary topology
```

The modelling stack is separate:

```text
formal mesh / graph / cell complex / coordinate scaffold
= descriptive apparatus for reasoning and simulation
```

The modelling stack must not be inserted into the ontological stack.

Do not write:

```text
substrate -> cells -> vortons -> matter
```

Write:

```text
continuous substrate -> stable closure-patterns/vortons -> material regimes
```

When a discrete model is needed, write:

```text
continuous substrate, represented for modelling by local regions and adjacency relations
```

## What The Supporting Research Got Wrong

The recent research used language such as:

- substrate cells
- neighbouring substrate cells
- cell complexes
- edges carrying local state
- faces carrying loop mismatch
- material cells
- lattice-like substrate

Some of this is acceptable in a simulation paper. It is not acceptable as AMS ontology.

The main errors are:

1. It risks making the substrate granular.
2. It risks making cells more basic than vortons.
3. It risks treating local modelling regions as if they are physical units.
4. It risks replacing continuous substrate ontology with a hidden lattice ontology.
5. It risks making vortons seem like objects placed inside cells rather than stable closure-patterns of the substrate itself.

The correction is not to discard all formal language. The correction is to classify it properly.

## What Should Be Preserved

The supporting research did contribute useful pressure in five areas.

### 1. Local Admissibility

Propagation should be described as local admissibility updating, not as a field-object spreading through empty space.

This remains useful if `local` means:

```text
local region of continuous substrate or local relation in a formal representation
```

not:

```text
one tiny substrate cell
```

### 2. Route Partition

At boundaries, an incoming disturbance can be:

- transmitted
- returned
- stored
- dissipated
- fragmented
- converted into a different route mode

This remains useful for light, electricity, magnetism, and vorton generation.

### 3. Threshold Behaviour

AMS needs thresholds. A perturbation should not automatically become a vorton.

The useful threshold grammar includes:

- boundedness
- distributedness
- returnability
- coherence
- leakage
- fragmentation
- invariant persistence

### 4. Failure-First Discipline

The research usefully exposed the need to explain failure modes. Vorton theory must explain why most candidate closure-patterns fail.

### 5. Topology Over Particle Imagery

The best parts of the research pushed away from little-object imagery and towards stable topology. That should be retained, but it must be expressed without making the substrate a graph of material pieces.

## Non-Negotiable Vorton Definition

A vorton is:

```text
a stable primary topological closure-pattern in the continuous substrate
```

Expanded:

```text
a bounded, distributed, returnable, coherent pattern of substrate organisation
whose invariant closure profile persists through perturbation
strongly enough to function as primary topological identity
```

A vorton is not:

- a particle in the ordinary material sense
- a cell of substrate
- a piece of substrate-stuff
- a bead on a hidden lattice
- a field excitation by itself
- every twist in the substrate
- every transient loop
- every light-like propagation
- every local alignment mismatch
- every magnetic domain
- every electrical route event

This definition must govern all future vorton research.

## Correct Layer Relations

### `T0`: Continuous Substrate

`T0` is the continuous substrate domain. It is not made of material parts in the AMS account.

Permitted language:

- continuous substrate
- substrate region
- local substrate state
- local substrate relation
- route through the substrate
- constraint in the substrate

Avoid unless explicitly marked as modelling notation:

- substrate cell
- substrate lattice
- substrate node
- substrate edge
- substrate mesh

### `T1`: Stable Primary Topology

`T1` is stable primary topology.

Vortons belong here.

This means vortons are not intermediary material units between substrate and matter. They are the first stable topological identities in the substrate.

Permitted language:

- stable closure-pattern
- primary topological identity
- bounded persistence pattern
- invariant closure profile
- torsional knot-state
- returnable constraint organisation

### `T2`: Secondary Material Regimes

`T2` is secondary topology: lattices, interfaces, materials, domains, phase boundaries, conductive paths, dielectric barriers, magnetic domains, and engineered systems.

These regimes are real, but they are not more fundamental than vortons.

They arise from:

- stable primary topology
- persistent boundary conditions
- coupled route structures
- aggregate constraints
- transient structures interacting with stable closures

## Formal Modelling Rule

Discrete formalisms may be used only under this rule:

```text
Discrete variables describe a representation of local relations.
They do not define what the substrate is made of.
```

Acceptable:

```text
For simulation, represent the continuous substrate by regions R_i with adjacency relations A_ij.
```

Risky:

```text
The substrate is a graph of cells.
```

Acceptable:

```text
A loop in the representation tests whether a local closure relation returns coherently.
```

Risky:

```text
A face in the substrate carries the closure.
```

Acceptable:

```text
The vorton support can be approximated by a bounded region in the modelling mesh.
```

Risky:

```text
The vorton sits inside a cell complex.
```

The corrected notation should prefer:

```text
S       continuous substrate domain
R_i     local modelling region within S
A_ij    adjacency relation between modelling regions
X(x,t)  local substrate state
J(x,t)  local constraint routing
V       candidate vorton region
dV      operational boundary of candidate region
```

Use graph or mesh notation only after stating that it is representational.

## Failure-First Vorton Rebuild

Future vorton work should begin with non-vortons.

A candidate closure-pattern does not count as a vorton if it is:

- transient only
- too diffuse
- too concentrated
- internally incoherent
- unable to return strain
- unable to preserve invariant identity
- dependent on a narrow unsupported threshold
- only a route event
- only a measurement projection
- only a secondary material domain

The failure-first ladder is:

```text
local disturbance
-> transient torsion/tension expression
-> candidate return path
-> failed closure or proto-closure
-> proto-vorton only if thresholds are partly crossed
-> stable vorton only if invariant identity persists
```

This protects the theory from overgeneration.

## Minimal Vorton Criteria

A stable vorton requires at least:

```text
B_in(V) > B_min
D_spread(V) > D_min
R_return(V) > R_min
Q_coh(V) > Q_min
L_leak(V) < L_max
X_frag(V) < X_max
I_profile(V,t) preserved across N_min perturbation cycles
```

Where:

- `B_in` = inward boundedness
- `D_spread` = distributedness of retained constraint
- `R_return` = returnability
- `Q_coh` = coherence
- `L_leak` = leakage tendency
- `X_frag` = fragmentation tendency
- `I_profile` = discriminating invariant profile

This remains semi-formal. It is intentionally a gate, not a final equation.

## Proto-Vorton Rule

A proto-vorton is not a small vorton.

It is:

```text
a candidate closure-pattern that has crossed some formation thresholds
but has not yet demonstrated stable primary identity
```

Proto-vorton language should be used sparingly. It is useful for threshold formation and decay, but dangerous if it becomes a way to label any interesting transient.

## Darkness Rule

Ordered darkness is not a vorton source-agent.

Do not write:

```text
darkness generates vortons
```

Write:

```text
ordered darkness is the coherent non-expressive substrate ground
within which stable closure can form under suitable torsion, tension,
alignment, closure, and threshold conditions
```

Darkness is therefore a ground condition, not an actor.

## Magnetism Rule

Magnetic propagation should not be collapsed into vorton generation.

Magnetism can be described as alignment-closure mismatch and its propagation through matter and substrate routes.

Vortons may:

- anchor material identity
- bias alignment behaviour
- resist or permit reseating
- participate in domain-scale behaviour

But a magnetic effect is not automatically a vorton, and a magnetic domain is not automatically a vorton.

## Electricity Rule

Electrical propagation should not be collapsed into vorton motion.

Electricity can be described as ordered route slip, occupancy reseating, and stored mismatch along admissible paths.

Vortons may:

- provide stable route-bearing identity
- constrain conductive paths
- participate in lock-up or breakdown thresholds

But ordinary current is not vorton generation.

## Light Rule

Light should not be collapsed into vorton formation.

Light is transient coherent propagation.

A vorton is stable primary closure.

Only under special threshold conditions should a light-like transient be discussed as contributing to defect formation, capture, or possible proto-vorton formation.

## Research Rebuild Sequence

The vorton programme should now proceed in this order.

### Phase 1: Ontology Lock

Produce a short canonical definition sheet:

- what `T0` is
- what `T1` is
- what a vorton is
- what a vorton is not
- what modelling notation may and may not mean

### Phase 2: Failure Atlas Integration

Use the non-persistent vorton topology atlas as a required companion to every formation claim.

Every vorton-generation proposal must state:

```text
why this candidate does not merely diffuse, collapse, oscillate, fragment, or fail at adjacency
```

### Phase 3: Invariant Profile

Define `I_profile`.

The profile should include at least:

- closure class
- return path structure
- handedness or orientation signature where applicable
- stability envelope
- coupling behaviour
- permitted reseating modes
- failure thresholds

### Phase 4: Worked Formation Cases

Build worked cases in this sequence:

1. transient optical propagation that does not form a vorton
2. dielectric lock-up that stores mismatch but does not form a vorton
3. magnetic alignment domain that is secondary topology, not a vorton
4. collapse-prone candidate closure that fails
5. underreturned candidate closure that fails
6. proto-vorton that decays
7. stable vorton candidate that meets the gate conditions

### Phase 5: Book Integration

After the correction is stable, update the technical companion in three places:

1. Chapter 10, darkness: clarify darkness as ordered ground, not source-agent.
2. Chapter 16, vorton: sharpen the definition and reject discretised-substrate readings.
3. Appendix A, vorton discrimination: add the modelling-notation guardrail.

## Rules For Future Research Prompts

Future research prompts should include these constraints:

```text
Do not treat the substrate as made of cells, nodes, pellets, grains, or lattice units.
If discrete notation is used, explicitly mark it as modelling representation only.
Preserve the T0/T1/T2 distinction.
Define vorton as stable primary topology, not as particle or substrate unit.
Start with failure modes before assigning vorton identity.
Do not collapse light, magnetism, electricity, or darkness into vorton generation.
Frame all claims as coherence-building, not direct proof of invisible substrate.
```

## Current Verdict

The vorton work is salvageable, but the research must be disciplined.

The useful direction is:

```text
topology, thresholds, routes, closure, invariants, failure modes
```

The dangerous direction is:

```text
hidden lattice, substrate cells, graph ontology, materialised T0
```

AMS should keep the first and reject the second.
