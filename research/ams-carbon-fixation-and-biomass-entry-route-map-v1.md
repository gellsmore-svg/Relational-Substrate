## Source Anchors

The source base supports these facts:

- Autotrophic organisms fix inorganic carbon into organic matter and are primary producers in terrestrial and marine ecosystems.
- The Calvin-Benson-Bassham cycle is the dominant global carbon-fixation pathway and is used by plants, algae, cyanobacteria, and many proteobacteria.
- Other natural carbon-fixation pathways include the reverse tricarboxylic acid cycle, the Wood-Ljungdahl pathway, the 3-hydroxypropionate bicycle, the 3-hydroxypropionate/4-hydroxybutyrate cycle, the dicarboxylate/4-hydroxybutyrate cycle, and the reductive glycine pathway.
- RuBisCO catalyses CO2 fixation into ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate in the Calvin cycle, but it also reacts with O2, producing the oxygenation route that drives photorespiration.
- The Wood-Ljungdahl pathway fixes two one-carbon units into acetyl-CoA through enzyme-bound one-carbon chemistry involving metal centres, including nickel, iron-sulfur, and corrinoid chemistry.
- The Wood-Ljungdahl pathway is noncyclic and is associated with anaerobic bacteria and archaea.
- Reverse TCA and hydroxypropionate/hydroxybutyrate routes show that carbon entry is not a single universal route but a family of constrained biochemical gates.
- Carbon fixation requires energy and reducing power; fixed carbon becomes useful only when coupled into metabolic and biosynthetic networks.

## AMS Fixation Principle

The carbon fixation principle:

```text
fixed carbon = environmental carbon admitted into retained biological form
```

In ordinary chemistry, carbon dioxide is a small, oxidised, stable molecule. It does not automatically become sugar, lipid, amino acid, nucleotide, or biomass. It must be captured, activated, reduced, and routed.

AMS interpretation:

```text
carbon fixation is not simple capture.
it is route conversion.
```

The carbon atom moves from environmental availability into metabolic addressability. Once fixed, it can be passed into central metabolism, polymer construction, boundary formation, storage, repair, and growth.

The route is therefore not:

```text
CO2 -> carbon in organism
```

It is:

```text
CO2/bicarbonate -> activated entry point -> retained metabolic intermediate -> biosynthetic routing -> embodied architecture
```

The fixed carbon has crossed an ontological threshold. It is no longer merely part of the surrounding chemical field. It has become a participant in the organism's retained construction pattern.

## Route Map 1: Calvin-Benson-Bassham Carbon Entry

The Calvin-Benson-Bassham cycle is the major global carbon-fixation route.

AMS route:

```text
CO2 + ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate -> unstable six-carbon intermediate -> 3-phosphoglycerate -> triose phosphate -> sugar route
```

In this pathway, carbon entry depends on a prepared acceptor molecule. CO2 is not simply pulled into life as isolated carbon. It is admitted by being joined to ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate, then split into stable phosphorylated products that can be routed through metabolism.

Vorton interpretation:

```text
RuBisCO forms a carboxylation gate vorton.
RuBP forms the prepared carbon-acceptor vorton.
3-phosphoglycerate forms the first stable post-entry route node.
```

The fixation event is a controlled relational reseating. Environmental carbon becomes metabolically addressable because it is joined into a molecule already under biological control.

Route profile:

```text
route class: R4
obstruction class: O4
dominance: D4
grade: G_A
```

Why not pure `R1`?

Because the Calvin route is powerful but not narrow in the same way as a single electron-wire route. It is cyclic, enzyme-dependent, energy-dependent, and exposed to oxygen competition. Its strength lies in governed recurrence rather than one-step directness.

Mechanism-facing statement:

```text
The Calvin cycle fixes carbon by using a recurring acceptor topology and phosphorylated energy grammar to turn oxidised environmental carbon into carbohydrate-route material.
```

## Route Map 2: RuBisCO Oxygenation and Photorespiratory Leakage

RuBisCO is not only a carboxylase. It is also an oxygenase.

This matters strongly for AMS because the same gate that admits CO2 can admit O2 into a less useful route. Oxygenation is not random noise. It is a competing route through the same active-site geometry.

AMS interpretation:

```text
carboxylation = productive carbon-entry route
oxygenation = competitive route misdirection
photorespiration = recovery route after misdirected entry
```

Route profile for productive carboxylation:

```text
route class: R4
obstruction class: O4
dominance: D4
grade: G_A
```

Route profile for oxygenation leakage:

```text
route class: R3
obstruction class: O2
dominance: D4
grade: G_C
```

Photorespiration is not best described as mere waste. It is a salvage route after gate competition has admitted the wrong small molecule.

Vorton interpretation:

```text
RuBisCO active-site vorton = shared gate
CO2 entry = productive closure
O2 entry = misdirected closure
photorespiration = repair/re-routing field
```

This is significant for the wider AMS model because it shows that a real route can be strong without being perfectly exclusive. A gate may be biologically central and still have leakage when two external molecules can exploit overlapping entry conditions.

## Route Map 3: Wood-Ljungdahl One-Carbon Assembly

The Wood-Ljungdahl pathway is structurally different from the Calvin cycle. It is not a sugar-first cycle. It fixes two one-carbon units into acetyl-CoA through enzyme-bound chemistry.

AMS route:

```text
CO2 -> methyl branch
CO2 -> carbonyl branch
methyl + carbonyl + CoA -> acetyl-CoA
```

The pathway is especially important for AMS because it presents carbon fixation as metal-governed one-carbon construction. It is not just organic carbon chemistry. It is carbon entry through nickel, iron-sulfur, corrinoid, protein, and redox coordination.

Vorton interpretation:

```text
methyl branch vorton = reduced one-carbon retention
carbonyl branch vorton = CO-bound metal-centre retention
CODH/ACS vorton = convergence gate
acetyl-CoA vorton = first released two-carbon route node
```

Route profile:

```text
route class: R1/R4
obstruction class: O1/O4
dominance: D1/D4
grade: G_A
```

The route is `R1` because the convergence chemistry is precise and enzyme-bound. It is `R4` because the overall pathway is strongly conditioned by anaerobic redox environment, metal-centre state, electron supply, and oxygen sensitivity.

Mechanism-facing statement:

```text
The Wood-Ljungdahl pathway fixes carbon by reducing and converging two one-carbon streams into acetyl-CoA through metal-centre controlled closure.
```

This route has strong relevance to earlier iron and sulfur maps. It depends on the kind of redox depth and embedded metal chemistry already assigned to iron and sulfur. Carbon's entry here is not independent of those elements. Carbon receives architectural entry only because redox carriers and metal centres hold the route open.

## Route Map 4: Reverse TCA and Reductive Carboxylation

The reverse TCA route uses central metabolic geometry in reductive direction.

AMS route:

```text
central metabolic intermediates + CO2 + reducing power -> larger organic intermediates -> biomass precursors
```

This pathway matters because it shows that carbon fixation can enter through metabolism's own backbone rather than through a sugar-cycle gate or acetyl-CoA convergence gate.

Vorton interpretation:

```text
reverse TCA vorton = metabolic-cycle inversion under reducing conditions
carboxylation nodes = carbon-entry gates
reductive continuity = route-retention field
```

Route profile:

```text
route class: R4
obstruction class: O4
dominance: D4
grade: G_A/G_B
```

The grade is high but slightly more context-dependent than the core Calvin and Wood-Ljungdahl descriptions. The pathway is coherent, known, and mechanistically meaningful, but its ecological distribution and operation depend strongly on organismal and environmental context.

AMS implication:

```text
carbon entry can occur by reversing a familiar oxidative metabolic route when the redox field supports it.
```

This strengthens the AMS claim that biological routes are not mere lists of molecules. Direction, energy gradient, reduction state, and environmental constraint decide whether a route builds up or breaks down.

## Route Map 5: 3HP, 4HB, and Alternative Microbial Fixation Routes

The 3-hydroxypropionate, 3-hydroxypropionate/4-hydroxybutyrate, dicarboxylate/4-hydroxybutyrate, and reductive glycine routes show that carbon fixation is plural.


These pathways often rely on CoA thioesters, ATP investment, reductive steps, carboxylation nodes, and niche-specific enzyme sequences. Their existence matters because it prevents a simplistic "the Calvin cycle is carbon fixation" model.

Vorton interpretation:

```text
alternative fixation vorton = organism-specific route closure
```

The route is real, but its confidence is distributed across multiple pathways with different ecological niches. The AMS assignment is not weakened by diversity. It is strengthened: life contains multiple carbon-entry topologies because no single carbon gate covers every environment.

## Failure and Distortion Modes

### Failure Mode 1: No Energy or Reducing Power

Without energy and reducing power, inorganic carbon remains outside the living construction route.

```text
route class: R3
obstruction class: O2/O5
dominance: D3/D4
grade: G_C
```

Carbon is present but not admitted.

### Failure Mode 2: Active-Site Competition

RuBisCO oxygenation shows that a real carbon-entry gate can suffer competing molecular entry.

```text
route class: R3
obstruction class: O2
dominance: D4
grade: G_C
```

The route is not absent. It is misdirected.

### Failure Mode 3: Oxygen Damage to Anaerobic Fixation Routes

Wood-Ljungdahl and related metal-centre routes are often oxygen-sensitive.

```text
route class: R6
obstruction class: O6
dominance: D4
grade: G_C/G_D
```

The route fails because the redox/metal-centre field cannot remain intact.

### Failure Mode 4: Carbon Fixed but Not Incorporated

Fixed carbon can enter a molecule without becoming retained organismal architecture.

```text
route class: R2/R3
obstruction class: O3/O5
dominance: D2/D3
grade: G_B/G_C
```

This is pocketing or diffusion after entry. The carbon has crossed the first gate but not the whole construction pathway.

### Failure Mode 5: Over-Reduction or Metabolic Imbalance

If reductive entry exceeds downstream routing, carbon accumulates in unstable or maladaptive states.

```text
route class: R6
obstruction class: O6
dominance: D4
grade: G_C
```

The gate is active, but the downstream route field cannot hold the incoming material coherently.

```text

```

This connects magnesium's gate-forming role to carbon's biomass-entry role.

### Carbon, Iron, and Sulfur

Iron and sulfur support low-potential electron transfer and metal-centre chemistry in anaerobic carbon fixation.

```text
iron supplies redox depth.
sulfur supplies redox closure.
carbon receives fixed architectural entry.
```

The Wood-Ljungdahl pathway is the clearest case: carbon entry depends on metal-centre and redox architectures that cannot be reduced to carbon chemistry alone.

### Carbon and Oxygen

Oxygen is double-edged. It enables high-energy aerobic life downstream, but it competes with CO2 at RuBisCO and can damage oxygen-sensitive fixation routes.

```text
oxygen expands biological power while obstructing certain carbon-entry gates.
```

This is an important route-grammar principle: an element may be constructive in one layer and obstructive in another.

The uncertainty is how far AMS should press pathway-specific vorton topology:

- Calvin-cycle route closure is clear at the cycle level, but subsite vorton assignment would require deeper enzyme-structural modelling.
- Wood-Ljungdahl metal-centre topology is highly suggestive, but exact vorton geometry should be treated as a model target for later mathematical formalisation.
- Alternative microbial pathways need more comparative scoring before each receives a separate high-confidence topology.
