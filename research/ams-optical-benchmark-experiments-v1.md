# AMS Optical Benchmark Experiments v1

## Purpose

This document defines the first explicit optical experiments for the AMS vorton modelling programme.

The purpose is not to claim a finished theory of light. It is to test whether the current model families can begin to generate coherent explanations for observed optical behaviour.

The first benchmarks are the user’s target questions:

- how does light travel through glass from a vorton perspective?
- how does light reflect from metal from a vorton perspective?
- why does propagation differ in crystal and glass?
- how do defects alter transmission, scattering, and boundary response?

## Governing Rule

These are experiments, not final answers.

Each benchmark should:

- identify the relevant model families
- define what success would look like
- define what failure would look like
- record what each family currently seems able or unable to do

## Shared Optical Questions

Every optical experiment in this document should ask:

1. What is propagating?
2. Through what sort of whole is it propagating?
3. Is the crucial behaviour bulk-driven, pathway-driven, boundary-driven, or collective-response-driven?
4. What role do defects and interfaces play?
5. Does the model explain transmission and reflection as different surface expressions of one deeper grammar, or as unrelated effects?

## Optical Benchmark 1: Transmission Through Glass

### Observed Phenomenon

Glass can be strongly transmissive despite being structurally disordered relative to a crystal.

The benchmark question is:

- how can propagation remain coherent through a disordered but real whole?

### Minimum explanatory requirements

A successful AMS model should explain at least:

- why propagation is possible through glass
- why glass is not equivalent to random scattering chaos
- why defects and impurities can still alter clarity
- why glass differs from metal and from strongly scattering media

### Most relevant model families

- `Family G: Pathway-Selective Propagation Closure`
- `Family D: Resonant Dynamic Closure`
- `Family E: Boundary-Concentrated Functional Closure`
- `Family F: Defect-Responsive Closure`

### First-pass family hypotheses

#### Family G

Best current candidate.

Hypothesis:

- light-like propagation through glass occurs because the glass whole still provides enough admissible distributed pathways for coherent propagation, even without crystal regularity

Promising because:

- it naturally explains transmission without requiring perfect lattice order
- it can likely incorporate impurity and defect sensitivity

Main risk:

- “pathways” may remain too vague unless later formalised geometrically

#### Family D

Useful but likely secondary.

Hypothesis:

- propagation depends partly on dynamic coherence across local relation-patterns rather than only on static pathway availability

Promising because:

- it may help explain why transmission remains coherent in a dynamic or non-crystal medium

Main risk:

- may blur too easily into unstable oscillation unless stabilising conditions are made explicit

#### Family E

Supportive rather than central.

Hypothesis:

- boundary entry and exit conditions matter strongly, but the bulk of glass transmission probably cannot be explained by boundary-concentrated logic alone

#### Family F

Important modifier.

Hypothesis:

- defects, strain, and impurities change transmission by altering local admissibility and scattering conditions

### Current experimental judgment

Most promising current working combination:

- `G + D + F`

Provisional confidence:

- `7/10`

## Optical Benchmark 2: Reflection From Metal

### Observed Phenomenon

Metals can reflect light strongly at their surfaces.

The benchmark question is:

- why does the surface of a metal respond so differently from a transmissive medium like glass?

### Minimum explanatory requirements

A successful AMS model should explain at least:

- why the response is strongly surface-concentrated
- why the response appears collective rather than merely local
- why metallic order matters
- why reflection differs from diffuse scattering

### Most relevant model families

- `Family H: Collective Surface-Response Closure`
- `Family E: Boundary-Concentrated Functional Closure`
- `Family F: Defect-Responsive Closure`
- `Family D: Resonant Dynamic Closure`

### First-pass family hypotheses

#### Family H

Best current candidate.

Hypothesis:

- metallic reflection occurs because the surface region responds collectively to incoming disturbance rather than only through independent local events

Promising because:

- it treats reflection as a whole-level surface phenomenon
- it matches the need for collective response

Main risk:

- “collective response” still needs sharper mathematical definition

#### Family E

Strong support family.

Hypothesis:

- boundary concentration is essential because the optical response is decisively shaped at the interface

Promising because:

- it explains why boundary conditions matter so much

Main risk:

- may not by itself explain the collective coherence of reflection

#### Family F

Important modifier.

Hypothesis:

- surface quality, defects, oxidation, and retained history alter reflectivity by changing the surface-response regime

#### Family D

Potential partial support family.

Hypothesis:

- resonance compatibility may matter, but probably as a contributor rather than the main surface explanation

### Current experimental judgment

Most promising current working combination:

- `H + E + F`

Provisional confidence:

- `7/10`

## Optical Benchmark 3: Crystal Versus Glass Propagation

### Observed Phenomenon

Both crystal and glass can transmit, but not in the same way. Crystals are more ordered. Glass is disordered but still can remain transparent.

The benchmark question is:

- what difference in model grammar accounts for this?

### Minimum explanatory requirements

A successful AMS model should explain:

- why crystal order and glass disorder do not produce the same propagation profile
- why both can still support transmission
- why defect patterns matter differently in each

### Most relevant model families

- `Family G: Pathway-Selective Propagation Closure`
- `Family C: Nested Closure`
- `Family F: Defect-Responsive Closure`
- `Family D: Resonant Dynamic Closure`

### First-pass family hypotheses

#### Crystal

Most promising model family combination:

- `C + G + F`

Hypothesis:

- crystal transmission depends on strong nested whole-order with highly structured propagation pathways

#### Glass

Most promising model family combination:

- `G + D + F`

Hypothesis:

- glass transmission depends on distributed admissible pathways without the same lattice regularity, making defect and impurity sensitivity especially important

### Current experimental judgment

The difference may not be:

- transmission versus non-transmission

but rather:

- structured pathway regularity versus distributed pathway permissibility

Provisional confidence:

- `7/10`

## Optical Benchmark 4: Defect-Sensitive Scattering

### Observed Phenomenon

Defects, impurities, rough surfaces, and internal discontinuities can alter transmission and increase scattering.

The benchmark question is:

- how do defects disrupt or redirect propagation in AMS terms?

### Minimum explanatory requirements

A successful AMS model should explain:

- why defects matter
- why some defects scatter weakly and others strongly
- why the same bulk whole can remain real while becoming optically degraded

### Most relevant model families

- `Family F: Defect-Responsive Closure`
- `Family G: Pathway-Selective Propagation Closure`
- `Family H: Collective Surface-Response Closure`

### First-pass family hypotheses

#### Family F

Best current candidate.

Hypothesis:

- defects alter propagation by locally changing admissibility, redistributing strain, or breaking coherent pathway continuity

#### Family G

Strong support family.

Hypothesis:

- scattering can often be treated as pathway disruption or forced redirection rather than as a totally separate phenomenon

#### Family H

Relevant at surfaces and near collective interface response regimes.

### Current experimental judgment

Most promising current working combination:

- `F + G`

Provisional confidence:

- `8/10`

## Benchmark 5: Metal Surface Quality And Reflection Degradation

### Observed Phenomenon

Real metallic reflection depends on:

- smoothness
- oxidation state
- surface contamination
- grain and defect profile

### Question

- what modifies a reflective surface from a vorton perspective without changing the whole into a non-metal?

### Most relevant model families

- `H`
- `E`
- `F`

### Current experimental judgment

This benchmark strongly suggests that:

- metallic reflection should not be treated as purely bulk identity
- and not as a mere local point-event either

It appears to require:

- whole-level metallic order
- plus high surface coherence
- plus defect-sensitive boundary response

Provisional confidence:

- `7/10`

## Benchmark 6: Transparent Boundary Entry And Exit

### Observed Phenomenon

Even where a material transmits, entry into and exit from the material still matter.

### Question

- what happens at the interface when propagation enters a transmissive whole?

### Most relevant model families

- `E`
- `G`
- `D`

### Current experimental judgment

This benchmark is not yet well constrained, but it matters because:

- transmissive behaviour is not only a bulk property
- interface conditions may still reshape propagation without fully blocking it

Provisional confidence:

- `5/10`

## First Comparative Results

### 1. Glass transmission and metal reflection already seem to require different family dominance

Current strongest tentative pairings:

- glass transmission: `G + D + F`
- metal reflection: `H + E + F`

That is a useful early result because it suggests the programme is already differentiating behaviours rather than forcing one model everywhere.

### 2. Defect logic is central across both transmission and reflection

This is a strong and useful sign. `Family F` is appearing as a cross-cutting support family in almost every optical benchmark.

### 3. Boundary logic matters even for transmission

The work now suggests that transmissive media still require interface modelling, even where the bulk is the main propagation region.

### 4. Optical modelling will likely require joint-family experiments

The current benchmarks do not suggest that one family alone will explain everything.

That is not a problem yet. It is a sign that:

- combined model experiments may be necessary

as long as the combinations remain disciplined.
