# AMS Route Class Benchmark Table v1

## Purpose

This document applies the current route taxonomy to the main benchmark cases in the programme.

The route taxonomy was developed in:

- `ams-completion-route-classes-v1.md`

The main question now is:

- do the route classes actually discriminate the benchmark cases cleanly?

This is the first direct side-by-side test of whether the route-level language:

- `R1` narrow viable route
- `R2` distributed weak routes
- `R3` blocked apparent route
- `R4` inward-biased route
- `R5` loop-dominated pseudo-route
- `R6` fragmenting route field

is genuinely useful across the real material pairings.

## Benchmark Cases in Scope

- `air -> glass`
- `glass -> air`
- `air -> polished metal`
- `air -> oxidised metal`
- `glass -> crystal`
- `crystal -> air`

## Reading Rule

The table records dominant or near-dominant route classes in each case.

This does not mean only one route class can ever appear in a case. It means:

- which route classes currently do the most explanatory work

## Route-Class Benchmark Table

| Benchmark case | Dominant route classes | Secondary route classes | Main outcome logic |
|---|---|---|---|
| air -> glass | `R1`, `R2` | `R3` in degraded entry | viable admission into distributed permissive order with possible weakly competing side routes |
| glass -> air | `R2`, `R3`, `R5` | `R1` in clean release, `R6` in degraded surfaces | bulk transmission is strong, but terminal release routes are often weak, blocked, or pseudo-progressive |
| air -> polished metal | `R4` | weak `R3` analogue only in non-ideal cases | strong structured boundary response with inward/return-biased route dominance |
| air -> oxidised metal | `R3`, `R6` | weak `R4` in partially preserved surfaces | apparent reflective routes degrade into blocked or fragmented surface-route behaviour |
| glass -> crystal | `R1`, `R3`, `R5` | `R2` in weaker compatibility zones | narrow viable handoff routes compete with blocked or pseudo-completing transition routes |
| crystal -> air | `R1`, `R4` | `R3` in stalled release conversion | ordered internal propagation can either find a viable narrow release route or resolve into inward-biased return |

## Case-by-Case Analysis

## 1. Air -> Glass

### Dominant Route Classes

- `R1` narrow viable route
- `R2` distributed weak routes

### Interpretation

This remains the strongest entry-side handoff benchmark.

The reason it needs both `R1` and `R2` is that glass is not best modelled as a single rigidly ordered admission channel. Instead:

- at least one genuinely viable route must exist
- but admission also benefits from a broader field of distributed permissive continuation

So:

- `R1` captures the fact that completion is real
- `R2` captures the fact that the receiving whole is permissive rather than tightly channelled

### Secondary Pressure

- `R3` appears in degraded or imperfect surfaces, where a route may look open but fail under actual continuation

### Judgment

This is a good sign for the taxonomy. The route classes do not flatten glass entry into either one clean route or one diffuse field. They allow both to coexist properly.

## 2. Glass -> Air

### Dominant Route Classes

- `R2` distributed weak routes
- `R3` blocked apparent route
- `R5` loop-dominated pseudo-route

### Interpretation

This is the strongest release-side stress case for the route taxonomy.

It fits well because the glass whole:

- carries propagation strongly in bulk
- but often struggles at the terminal layer

So the release routes often look like:

- weak but real outward options (`R2`)
- apparently open but actually non-completing routes (`R3`)
- active local release attempts that do not truly complete (`R5`)

### Secondary Pressure

- `R1` appears only in the cleaner release cases
- `R6` appears when surface degradation becomes strong enough to break route coherence entirely

### Judgment

This is one of the best results in the whole table. The route taxonomy maps onto glass release unusually well.

## 3. Air -> Polished Metal

### Dominant Route Classes

- `R4` inward-biased route

### Interpretation

This is the cleanest route-class benchmark in the whole programme.

Reflection from polished metal is now best understood not as:

- no route

but as:

- a strongly organised route structure biased away from outward handoff and toward structured return

That is exactly what `R4` captures.

### Secondary Pressure

In less-than-ideal surfaces, a weak `R3` analogue may appear where route viability looks present but is not actually completed outwardly.

### Judgment

This is a major success for the route taxonomy. `R4` makes the reflection case much cleaner than before.

## 4. Air -> Oxidised Metal

### Dominant Route Classes

- `R3` blocked apparent route
- `R6` fragmenting route field

### Interpretation

This case is one of the strongest diagnostics in the table.

It shows how a surface can still suggest some reflective or structured response while no longer preserving a truly viable coherent route.

That gives:

- `R3` where a route looks available but breaks under progression
- `R6` where the surface degrades enough that route coherence fragments

### Secondary Pressure

- weak `R4` can survive where oxidation has not yet destroyed too much structured return bias

### Judgment

This pairing strongly supports the route taxonomy because it shows a clean shift from:

- polished-metal `R4`

toward:

- blocked and fragmenting route fields

## 5. Glass -> Crystal

### Dominant Route Classes

- `R1` narrow viable route
- `R3` blocked apparent route
- `R5` loop-dominated pseudo-route

### Interpretation

This is the strongest compatibility-side route test in the table.

It fits especially well because:

- true successful handoff needs at least one narrow viable route into crystal-style admissibility (`R1`)
- many partially promising transition routes prove blocked under real progression (`R3`)
- some active local transition behaviour is not genuine completion but pseudo-progressive looping (`R5`)

### Secondary Pressure

- `R2` appears in weaker compatibility zones where many weak local continuations exist without dominant structured admission

### Judgment

This is one of the clearest wins for the route taxonomy outside the release side.

## 6. Crystal -> Air

### Dominant Route Classes

- `R1` narrow viable route
- `R4` inward-biased route

### Interpretation

This pairing looks cleaner now than it did before the route-class work.

That is because crystal exit is best understood as a competition between:

- a narrow viable release route
- a strongly organised inward-biased alternative

That is exactly the `R1` vs `R4` contrast.

### Secondary Pressure

- `R3` appears in cases where ordered release begins but fails to complete

### Judgment

The route taxonomy gives crystal exit a clearer structure than the earlier exit-only language did.

## Cross-Benchmark Findings

## 1. The Route Taxonomy Survives Contact with the Main Cases

This is the main result.

The route classes do not collapse when applied to the benchmark cases. They actually sharpen them.

## 2. `R1`, `R3`, `R4`, and `R5` Are the Most Important Current Classes

Current priority by explanatory weight:

- `R1` narrow viable route
- `R3` blocked apparent route
- `R4` inward-biased route
- `R5` loop-dominated pseudo-route

These are doing the most real work across the major pairings.

## 3. `R2` Is Important but More Contextual

`R2` matters especially in:

- glass entry
- glass release

But it is less universally decisive than `R1`, `R3`, `R4`, and `R5`.

## 4. `R6` Is Best Understood as a Breakdown Edge

`R6` matters most where route coherence is already failing.

It is important, but it is less often the dominant explanatory class in the strongest benchmark cases.

## 5. Release and Compatibility Now Share a Route Logic

This is another major gain.

The same route classes now help explain both:

- release problems
- compatibility problems

without collapsing the cases into one undifferentiated story.

## Strongest Current Case-to-Class Mappings

Most confident mappings:

- `air -> polished metal` -> `R4`
- `air -> oxidised metal` -> `R3` + `R6`
- `glass -> air` -> `R2` + `R3` + `R5`
- `glass -> crystal` -> `R1` + `R3` + `R5`
- `crystal -> air` -> `R1` + `R4`

These should now be treated as stable benchmark associations unless later modelling overturns them.

## Strategic Consequences

## 1. The Programme Now Has a Route-Level Vocabulary That Actually Bites

That is the main achievement of this pass.

The model can now say:

- what sort of route dominates a case

not just:

- whether a case is transmissive or reflective

## 2. `R3` and `R5` Are the Key Frontier Classes

These are the strongest classes for the next deepening stage because they sit near the real unresolved failures:

- blocked apparent progress
- pseudo-progressive looping

Those are exactly the cases where a weak ontology would start hand-waving.

## 3. The Reflection Side Is Now Very Clean

`R4` gives the polished-metal case real sharpness.

That is one of the strongest signs that the route-level work is not decorative.

## Confidence

- confidence that the route taxonomy survives the benchmark cases: `9/10`
- confidence that `R4` cleanly captures polished-metal reflection: `9/10`
- confidence that `R3` and `R5` are key frontier classes: `8/10`
- confidence that `R6` is better treated as a breakdown-edge class than a central benchmark class: `8/10`

## Recommendation

Keep the route taxonomy.

Do not add new route classes yet.

The next gains should come from deepening the frontier classes rather than multiplying categories.
