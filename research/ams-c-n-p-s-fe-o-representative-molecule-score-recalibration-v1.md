# AMS C N P S Fe O Representative Molecule Score Recalibration v1

## Purpose

This note recalibrates the current six-family grammar against representative molecule and complex classes.

The locked family identities are:

```text
Carbon carries molecular architecture.
Nitrogen carries biological availability and recognition.
Phosphorus carries transferable biological order.
Sulfur carries controllable chemical reactivity.
Iron carries redox depth.
Oxygen carries polarity and oxidative power.
```

The question here is:

```text
how well do these family identities actually explain real molecule classes?
```

The bridge examples show three main results.

The highest-confidence molecule classes are:

- amino acids;
- peptide backbones;
- nucleotides;

This indicates that the current family grammar is especially mature for:


### Result 2: Sulfur and Iron Fit Best in Governed Complexes

Sulfur and iron are slightly less "clean" in free-molecule abstraction because their strongest identities appear most clearly under governance:

- thiol repair systems;
- Fe-S nodes;
- heme chambers;
- storage and transport machinery;
- toxicity and ferroptotic thresholds.

This is not a weakness of the identities. It is part of the identities.

### Result 3: Oxygen Is the Main Cross-Cutting Family

Oxygen appears in nearly every representative case, but not as a vague universal filler.

It does different precise work:

- water field;
- carbonyl polarity;
- phosphate perimeter;
- terminal respiration;
- ROS boundary.

This confirms that oxygen's breadth is real but still compressible.

## Pressure Test

The key pressure question is:

```text
do the six family identities start to blur when applied together?
```

Current answer:

```text
No, not substantially.
```

Where they remain sharp:

- `C` versus `O` in membranes and amino acids;
- `N` versus `P` in nucleotides;
- `S` versus `O` in glutathione and oxidative repair;
- `Fe` versus `O` in heme respiration;
- `Fe` versus `S` in Fe-S systems.

Where some blur remains:

- oxygen/phosphorus perimeter division in phosphate-rich systems;
- oxygen/carbon co-determination in heavily oxygenated organic molecules;
- iron/sulfur partitioning in Fe-S complexes at very fine mechanism level.

These are acceptable residual overlaps because they occur in genuinely coupled systems rather than because the family identities are weak.
