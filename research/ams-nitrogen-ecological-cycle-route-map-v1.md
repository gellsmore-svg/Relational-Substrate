## Source Anchors

The source base supports these facts:

- Nitrogen is used in many fundamental biomolecules and participates in environmental redox chemistry.
- Biogeochemical processes control the amount and form of nitrogen available to organisms as fixed nitrogen.
- Nitrogen is often the proximate limiting nutrient in surface environments.
- The global nitrogen cycle includes processes that create fixed nitrogen and processes that destroy or remove fixed nitrogen.
- Biologically driven nitrogen transformations include nitrogen fixation, assimilation, mineralisation/ammonification, nitrification, DNRA, denitrification, and anammox.
- Nitrogen fixation and assimilation are anabolic processes; mineralisation, nitrification, DNRA, denitrification, and anammox are catabolic or consequence routes.
- Denitrification and anammox are major microbial routes of nitrogen loss, with denitrification globally dominant and anammox especially important in aquatic systems.
- Anthropogenic nitrogen inputs have strongly altered the global nitrogen cycle through Haber-Bosch fertiliser, nitrogen-fixing crops, fossil-fuel combustion, livestock systems, sewage, and land-use change.
- Excess reactive nitrogen contributes to nitrate leaching, eutrophication, air pollution, acidification, biodiversity shifts, nitrous oxide emissions, and human/ecosystem health impacts.
- Nitrogen-cycle perturbations are often more local and immediate than the carbon dioxide problem, though they include global greenhouse consequences through nitrous oxide.

### AMS Model Strength

The AMS mapping is strong because:

- ecological nitrogen cycling directly extends N4 and N5;

### Remaining Uncertainty

The uncertainty lies in quantification:

- ecosystems differ sharply by soil, ocean, sediment, climate, land use, oxygen state, microbial community, and hydrology;
- nitrogen budgets require local and global flux data;
- anammox and denitrification proportions vary by environment;
- management models require quantitative thresholds rather than qualitative route grammar.
