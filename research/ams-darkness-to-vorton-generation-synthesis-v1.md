## Modelling Guardrail: Cells, Edges, and Complexes

Some of the supporting research notes describe the substrate through cells, edges, faces, or an oriented complex such as:

```text
K = (V, E, F, C)
```

This should be read as modelling language, not ontology.

In that usage:

- `cell` means a local modelling neighbourhood or discretised region
- `edge` means an adjacency relation between neighbouring regions
- `face` or `loop` means a local closure test across related adjacencies
- `complex` means a mathematical scaffold for simulation or formal reasoning

The term must not be read as inserting a new material layer between substrate and vortons. AMS should not imply:

```text
substrate -> cells -> vortons
```

The cleaner relation is:

```text
substrate, modelled locally when needed -> stable closure-patterns/vortons
```

Cells are therefore like coordinates, mesh elements, or finite-element regions. They help a model ask local questions about update, adjacency, closure, and propagation. They are not tiny pieces of substrate-stuff, not proto-particles, and not a more material substrate underneath vortons.

Book-facing prose should usually avoid `cell` for this layer unless it is explicitly explained as a formal discretisation. Prefer:

- local substrate region
- modelling neighbourhood
- adjacency region
- route region
- closure neighbourhood

This keeps the substrate continuous in the ontological sense while still allowing discrete mathematics for simulation and route analysis.

## Core Guardrail

AMS should not say:

```text
darkness creates vortons
```

That wording makes darkness sound like a substance, agent, or occult source. The cleaner AMS claim is:

```text
ordered darkness is the non-expressive ground state of the substrate
within which the capacities required for vorton formation can operate
```

The difference matters. Darkness is not nothingness, not independent matter, not a field, and not a hidden actor. It names substrate order before luminous propagation, electrical slip, magnetic alignment, or stable primary topology becomes expressed.

## Ontological Ladder

The proposed ladder is:

```text
D0 ordered non-expressive substrate ground
-> primitive substrate capacities
-> local perturbation or admissibility mismatch
-> transient torsion/tension packet
-> candidate closure-pattern
-> failure mode or proto-vorton
-> stable vorton
-> secondary material regime
```

This ladder keeps the categories distinct.

`D0` is the ordered ground. It is not itself a vorton.

Primitive substrate capacities are the conditions AMS already names as torsion, tension, alignment, and closure.

A transient torsion/tension packet is a temporary expression in the substrate. It may be light-like, stress-like, alignment-like, or mixed, depending on route and medium.

A candidate closure-pattern is a local attempt at self-returning order. Most such candidates fail.

A proto-vorton is a candidate closure-pattern that has crossed some closure thresholds but has not yet demonstrated durable invariant identity.

A stable vorton is a primary topological identity: a bounded, distributed, returnable closure-pattern that persists through perturbation while preserving its discriminating profile.

Secondary material regimes arise only when stable vorton identities aggregate, couple, reseat, and interact in persistent networks.

## Ordered Darkness as `D0`

Let:

```text
D0
```

denote ordered darkness.

In first-pass semi-formal terms, `D0` is characterised by low expressed propagation and high latent order:

```text
Psi_expr(D0) ~= 0
S_slip(D0) ~= 0
C_expressed(D0) ~= 0
C_latent(D0) > C_min
A_adm(D0) > A_min
Q_coh(D0) > Q_min
```

where:

- `Psi_expr` = expressed transient propagation, such as luminous activity
- `S_slip` = net route slip or occupancy transfer
- `C_expressed` = expressed stable closure-pattern identity
- `C_latent` = latent coherence or order
- `A_adm` = local admissibility
- `Q_coh` = coherence of local state transitions

This says that darkness is quiet, not empty. It is ordered enough to permit later expression without already being that expression.

## Primitive Capacities

The darkness-to-vorton account depends on four AMS substrate capacities.

### Torsion

Torsion is the substrate capacity for twist-bearing organisation.

Without torsion, AMS cannot account for:

- local rotational order
- knot-like identity
- winding signatures
- stable twist-bearing persistence

### Tension

Tension is the substrate capacity to hold strained configuration.

Without tension, a perturbation would flatten immediately into undifferentiated redistribution. There would be no retained cost, no stored mismatch, and no threshold behaviour.

### Alignment

Alignment is coordinated orientation or relational ordering.

Without alignment, neighbouring regions could not hand on admissible orientation, phase, or closure constraint. Magnetic propagation, optical coherence, and stable vorton formation would all lose their shared grammar.

### Closure

Closure is the capacity for self-returning organisation.

Without closure, a perturbation can travel or dissipate, but it cannot become a persistent primary topological identity.

## Perturbation and Candidate Formation

A local perturbation may be represented as a mismatch bundle:

```text
M_i = [tau_i, theta_i, sigma_i, rho_i, psi_i]
```

where:

- `tau_i` = torsional load
- `theta_i` = orientation or phase displacement
- `sigma_i` = stored strain or polarisation-like mismatch
- `rho_i` = occupancy or slip pressure
- `psi_i` = transient coherent amplitude

The supporting topology-first research notes use a related edge-state bundle:

```text
Xi = {U_e, rho_e, psi_e, sigma_e}
```

This is compatible with AMS if `Xi` is read as a local state-description rather than as a new ontology. The important point is that propagation is local admissibility updating. A mismatch does not need to be treated as a field-object spreading through a container. It can be treated as unresolved reconfiguration handed on through connected substrate and matter routes.

## Threshold Sequence

The basic sequence is:

```text
D0 ordered ground
-> local perturbation
-> transient torsion/tension packet
-> alignment loop
-> candidate closure-pattern
-> failure or proto-vorton
-> stable vorton
```

A candidate closure-pattern becomes interesting only when it begins to satisfy the existing AMS closure triad:

```text
B_in(V) > B_min
D_spread(V) > D_min
R_return(V) > R_min
```

where:

- `B_in(V)` = inward boundedness or retention
- `D_spread(V)` = distributedness of retained constraint
- `R_return(V)` = strength of closed or quasi-closed return paths

This triad prevents a common error. Vorton generation is not mere intensity. A strong local disturbance is not enough. The candidate must be retained, distributed, and returnable.

For stable vorton identity, add an invariant condition:

```text
I_profile(V, t0) ~= I_profile(V, t0 + N_min cycles)
```

This means a stable vorton must preserve a discriminating invariant profile across a minimum persistence window.

The profile may include:

- invariant topological signature
- winding or return-count structure
- stability envelope
- coupling behaviour
- permitted reconfiguration modes
- relation to secondary material regimes

This aligns with the technical companion's Appendix A vorton discrimination requirement.

## Proto-Vorton and Stable Vorton

A proto-vorton is:

```text
a candidate closure-pattern that has crossed partial closure thresholds
but has not yet demonstrated stable invariant identity
```

A stable vorton is:

```text
a bounded, distributed, returnable, coherent closure-pattern
whose invariant profile persists across perturbation
```

The distinction is essential. Without it, AMS would overgenerate stable entities and treat every near-loop as matter. That would make the model less coherent, not more.

## Failure Modes

Most candidate closure-patterns should fail. The non-persistent vorton atlas already gives the right negative-space grammar.

Important failure classes include:

- diffuse topology: too weakly bounded, so organisation leaks away
- collapse-prone topology: bounded but too concentrated, so return paths overload
- overconstrained topology: too rigid to reseat or adapt
- underreturned topology: local strain forms but no coherent return path closes
- unstable oscillatory topology: near-locking cycles continue without stable closure
- incompatible adjacency topology: local viability fails because neighbourhood coupling is inadmissible
- threshold-fragile topology: viable only across a narrow condition band

This negative taxonomy is not optional. It is what keeps vorton theory from becoming a catch-all explanation. A coherent AMS account must explain both why some topology persists and why most candidate topology does not.

## Darkness and Light

Light and vorton generation must remain distinct.

Light is:

```text
transient coherent propagation
```

A vorton is:

```text
stable primary closure
```

The difference can be stated through route outcome:

```text
light-like event:
coherence preserved through handoff, closure remains subcritical

vorton-forming event:
transient disturbance becomes bounded, distributed, returnable, and invariant-preserving
```

Ordered darkness underlies both, but it is neither light nor vorton. It is the ordered substrate condition that permits both transient expression and stable closure.

## Darkness and Electricity

Electricity, in the topology-first AMS grammar, is ordered slip, occupancy reseating, or stored mismatch along admissible routes.

The darkness-to-vorton bridge helps here because electrical behaviour often depends on whether routes remain transiently transmissive or become locally locked.

In AMS terms:

```text
conductive path:
route slip remains admissible and percolating

dielectric response:
slip is blocked, mismatch stores as polarisation-like lock-up

breakdown or defect formation:
stored mismatch crosses threshold and forces new route or local topology
```

Vorton generation would not be ordinary current. It would be a threshold case where local route stress produces a persistent closure-pattern rather than only transmission, storage, or dissipation.

## Darkness and Magnetism

Magnetism, in the topology-first AMS grammar, is alignment-closure mismatch and its propagation through matter and substrate routes.

The darkness-to-vorton bridge matters because magnetic effects are naturally close to closure language:

```text
magnetic propagation:
alignment mismatch handed onward

magnetic domain behaviour:
stable or metastable alignment regimes

vorton involvement:
primary closures bias, anchor, or resist local alignment changes
```

This does not mean every magnetic effect is a new vorton. It means vorton-bearing matter can participate in alignment response through its existing closure-patterns.

## Generation Model

First-pass decision sequence:

```text
1. Is the substrate region ordered enough to support coherent update?
   If no, no stable candidate forms.

2. Does perturbation create torsion/tension rather than mere diffusion?
   If no, the event dissipates.

3. Does an alignment loop or return path form?
   If no, the event remains transient.

4. Is boundedness above threshold?
   If no, the candidate diffuses.

5. Is distributedness above threshold?
   If no, the candidate collapses or overconcentrates.

6. Is returnability above threshold?
   If no, the candidate is underreturned.

7. Are coherence and junction quality sufficient?
   If no, the candidate fragments or fails at adjacency.

8. Is leakage low enough across the persistence window?
   If no, the proto-vorton decays.

9. Is invariant identity preserved?
   If yes, stable vorton identity may be assigned.
```

Compact form:

```text
D0
-> M_i
-> torsion/tension packet
-> return loop
-> candidate closure-pattern
-> proto-vorton
-> stable vorton
```

## Toy Lifetime Law

A cautious toy lifetime law can be retained from the supporting research:

```text
tau_v ~= tau_0 * exp((chi - chi_c) / Lambda_loss)
```

where:

- `tau_v` = vorton lifetime
- `tau_0` = base substrate-cycle timescale
- `chi` = closure surplus above threshold
- `chi_c` = critical closure threshold
- `Lambda_loss` = effective leakage or loss scale

This should remain explicitly labelled as toy formalism. It is useful because it says the right qualitative thing: persistence should rise sharply once closure surplus exceeds leakage, but the actual constitutive terms are not yet known.

## Coherence Tests

The darkness-to-vorton account should pass these tests before being promoted into book prose.

1. Does it distinguish ordered darkness from nothingness, field, matter, and agency?
2. Does it distinguish transient propagation from stable primary topology?
3. Does it explain why most candidate closures fail?
4. Does it connect vorton generation to torsion, tension, alignment, and closure without adding a new occult primitive?
5. Does it preserve conservation language by treating formation as costly reconfiguration?
6. Does it connect to route variables already defined in the AMS research corpus?
7. Does it leave room for conventional physics comparison rather than pretending AMS has direct proof?
