# AMS Book Repetition Pass Notes v1

## Purpose

This file records the repetition pass across the current drafted chapter sequence.

The drafted prose chapters currently are:

```text
ams-book-chapter-1-draft-v1.md
ams-book-chapter-2-draft-v1.md
ams-book-chapter-3-draft-v1.md

ams-book-chapter-6-draft-v1.md
ams-book-chapter-7-draft-v1.md
ams-book-chapter-8-draft-v1.md
```

The purpose of the repetition pass is not to remove all recurrence.

The draft needs deliberate recurrence because the whole book is built on a cumulative continuity grammar.

The actual goal is:

```text
preserve strategic recurrence of the core claims
while trimming repeated scaffolding and repeated restart language
```

## Central Claim

The central claim is:

> The current draft repeats the right things more often than it repeats the wrong things. The main repetition issue is not conceptual redundancy at the level of the argument spine, but repeated explanatory scaffolding at chapter openings, repeated contrast formulas, and slightly over-frequent restatement of the same continuity logic in nearly the same sentence shape. The next edit should therefore compress repeated support language while preserving the book's deliberate central refrains.

More briefly:

```text
The draft repeats its backbone well, but repeats its braces too often.
```

## What Should Be Repeated

The following should remain strategically repeated across the draft:

## 1. Created Order

The claim that reality is:

```text
created bounded continuity
```

should remain a recurring controlling line.

## 2. Material Grammar

The claim that created order is materially articulate through stable family roles should continue to echo where needed.

## 3. Life as Recoverability

The claim that life is more than visible activity and has to do with owned recoverability should remain recurrent.

## 4. Same Subject Continuity

The claim that the same subject remains the bearer of damage and healing is essential in the later arc and must remain visible.

## 5. Restoration vs Replacement

This contrast is central enough that some repetition is not only acceptable but necessary.

These are the draft's proper refrains.

## What Is Being Repeated Too Much

The repetition pass suggests five main over-repeated structures.

## 1. Opening Re-grounding of Earlier Chapters

Several chapters open by restating the previous chapter's conclusion at slightly fuller length than now necessary.

The main candidates are:

- Chapter 2 opening after Chapter 1
- Chapter 3 opening after Chapter 2
- Chapter 4 opening after Chapter 3
- Chapter 5 opening after Chapter 4

This is the most obvious repetition class.

## 2. “Not X, Not Y, but Z” Formulas

This rhetorical pattern is strong and often useful.

But it now appears often enough that it risks becoming too mechanically recognisable.

It is especially common around:

- life definitions
- personhood definitions
- corruption
- redemption
- resurrection

The logic should stay. The sentence pattern should vary more.

## 3. Continuity-and-Rupture Pairing

The later chapters rightly repeat:

- continuity is real
- rupture is real
- replacement is inadequate

But some of the phrasing is currently close enough that it could be tightened.

The strongest overlap zones are:

- Chapter 6
- Chapter 7
- Chapter 8

## 4. “Visible Activity Is Not Enough” Language

This idea is central and must remain.

But it appears in closely related form in:

- Chapter 4
- Chapter 5
- Chapter 6
- Chapter 8

The idea should stay. The exact framing can diversify.

## 5. “Same Damaged Continuity” Language

This is important, but it currently appears near its full intensity in multiple arc chapters.

It should remain strongest in:

- Chapter 7
- Chapter 8

and be slightly lighter elsewhere once the manuscript is assembled.

## The Strongest Repetition Zones

The repetition pass suggests three especially important zones.

## Zone 1: Chapters 3-5

Pattern:

- machine order not yet life
- life not yet personhood
- recoverability and continuity restated

Assessment:

the progression is correct, but the opening scaffolding could be compressed by about 10-15% once assembled.

## Zone 2: Chapters 6-8

Pattern:

- same subject
- same wounded continuity
- restoration versus replacement

Assessment:

these are necessary repetitions, but the sentence forms are slightly too similar across adjacent chapters.

Variation and compression would help.

## Zone 3: Chapter Openings Generally

Pattern:

many chapters begin by summarising the prior chapter's gain before advancing.

Assessment:

this is good for isolated files, but less necessary in a continuous manuscript.

This is probably the single biggest repetition opportunity.

## What Should Be Trimmed First

The highest-value trims are:

1. duplicated opening restatement after transitions already earned
2. repeated “not X, not Y, but Z” sentence forms
3. repeated full-intensity continuity formulas across Chapters 6-8

These will likely yield the biggest readability gain with minimal conceptual loss.

## What Should Be Preserved Even If Repetitive

The following should remain even if repeated:

1. created order as first reality
2. life as future-bearing recoverability
3. personhood as the continuing embodied subject
4. corruption as fragmentation within continuity
5. redemption as restoration rather than replacement
6. resurrection as the hardest continuity test

These are not disposable repetitions. They are the argument spine.
