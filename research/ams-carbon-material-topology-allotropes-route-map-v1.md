## Source Anchors

The source base supports these facts:

- Carbon can form a wide variety of allotropes across multiple dimensionalities and hybridisation states.
- Diamond is built from sp3 tetrahedral carbon atoms bonded to four neighbours; it is hard, dense, rigid, chemically resistant, optically transmissive, thermally conductive, and electrically insulating.
- Graphite is built from sp2 carbon layers with delocalised electrons and weak interlayer bonding; it is conductive and mechanically anisotropic.
- Graphene is an atomically thin two-dimensional carbon lattice with strong in-plane bonding and exceptional electronic, mechanical, thermal, and surface properties.
- Carbon nanotubes can be understood as rolled graphene-like structures with one-dimensional topology and strong anisotropic properties.
- Fullerenes are closed or partly closed carbon meshes, often built from curved sp2 carbon networks.
- Amorphous carbon and diamond-like carbon contain mixed sp2/sp3 bonding without long-range crystalline order; their properties depend on sp3 ratio, sp2 content, hydrogen content, and local network structure.
- Carbon fibres contain graphitic or turbostratic carbon structures whose layer orientation strongly affects strength, modulus, toughness, and anisotropy.
- Activated and porous carbons derive function from high surface area, pore topology, and surface chemistry, making them useful for adsorption, separation, storage, and filtration.
- Raman spectroscopy and other structural probes are used to distinguish carbon allotropes and bonding states.

## Route Map 1: Diamond as 3D Tetrahedral Closure

Diamond is a three-dimensional sp3 carbon network.

AMS route:

```text
carbon atom + four tetrahedral neighbours -> extended covalent lattice -> hardness, rigidity, insulation, optical transparency
```

Diamond's identity comes from full local covalent saturation in a retained 3D network. Electrons are tied into sigma bonding rather than delocalised across a conductive field.

Vorton interpretation:

```text
diamond vorton = 3D tetrahedral constraint lattice
```

This is not a loop-dominant electronic vorton like benzene. It is a distributed rigidity vorton: each carbon is locally locked into a repeating tetrahedral constraint field.

Route profile:

```text
route class: R1/R2
obstruction class: O1
dominance: D1
grade: G_A
```

Material implication:

```text
diamond closes strongly in geometry and weakly in electronic mobility.
```

That is why diamond can be hard and insulating at the same time.

## Route Map 2: Graphite as Layered Delocalised Anisotropy

Graphite is built from stacked sp2 carbon layers.

AMS route:

```text
sp2 carbon sheet + delocalised pi field + weak interlayer bonding -> conductive anisotropic layered material
```

Graphite's identity is not simply "carbon with weak bonds". It is strong within layers and weak between layers. This makes it conductive, black, lubricating, shearable, and directionally unequal.

Vorton interpretation:

```text
graphite layer vorton = in-plane delocalised sheet field
graphite stack vorton = weakly coupled layered route field
```

Route profile:

```text
route class: R2/R5
obstruction class: O1/O5
dominance: D1/D4
grade: G_A
```

AMS boundary notation:

```text
B_in layer >> B_out between layers
J_trans in-plane >> J_trans across layers
```

Material implication:

```text
graphite conducts and shears because its route strength is directional.
```

## Route Map 3: Graphene as Exposed Two-Dimensional Carbon Field

AMS route:

```text
single carbon sheet -> honeycomb lattice -> extended pi field -> high mobility, strength, thermal conduction, surface exposure
```

Graphene exposes the graphite layer without the full stacked graphite context. Its sheet topology allows extreme surface-to-volume behaviour and strong sensitivity to defects, substrate, doping, strain, and adsorbed molecules.

Vorton interpretation:

```text
graphene vorton = exposed 2D covalent-electronic route field
```

Route profile:

```text
route class: R2/R5
obstruction class: O1/O5

grade: G_A
```

Graphene is a high-confidence case for the AMS distinction between route and object. It is not merely a thin object. It is an extended route plane.

Material implication:

```text
graphene converts carbon topology into a two-dimensional electronic/mechanical surface.
```

## Route Map 6: Amorphous Carbon and Diamond-Like Carbon as Mixed Local Order

Amorphous carbon lacks long-range crystalline order but can contain different local bonding mixtures.

AMS route:

```text
mixed sp2/sp3/sp1 local fields + no long-range lattice -> property spectrum between graphitic, diamond-like, polymer-like, and disordered forms
```

Diamond-like carbon is especially important because it may combine high hardness, wear resistance, low friction, and varied electrical behaviour depending on sp3 ratio, hydrogen content, and local structure.

Vorton interpretation:

```text
amorphous carbon vorton = local-order field without global lattice closure
DLC vorton = sp3-rich protective constraint field embedded in disorder
```

Route profile:

```text
route class: R2/R6
obstruction class: O5/O6
dominance: D3/D4
grade: G_B/G_A
```

This is not a low score because amorphous carbon is useless. It is lower than diamond/graphene because its route field is distributed and locally heterogeneous rather than globally crystalline.

AMS implication:

```text
long-range order is not required for material function, but it changes confidence and route predictability.
```

Activated carbon does not primarily function as a perfect crystal or a delocalised conductor. It functions as a high-surface-area route field where molecules can enter, bind, be separated, or be stored.

## Cross-Element Implications

### Carbon and Silicon

Silicon builds extended inorganic networks, especially in silicates and silica. Carbon builds more diverse topology across molecular, biological, and material scales.

```text
silicon closes extended oxide networks.
carbon carries topology across skeletons, sheets, cages, fibres, pores, and life.
```

### Carbon and Iron

Iron supplies redox depth and magnetism. Carbon supplies topology and electronic field geometry. Their interaction appears in steels, carbides, catalysts, electrodes, and redox materials, but carbon's pure-material range is not iron-like.

```text
iron changes state deeply.
carbon changes form extensively.
```

### Carbon and Calcium

Calcium bridges and mineralises. Carbon supplies either organic architecture or carbonate/inorganic carbon forms. In materials, calcium tends toward ionic/coordination roles; carbon tends toward network topology.

```text
calcium bridges.
carbon frameworks.
```

### Carbon and Sulfur

Sulfur tunes, locks, passivates, or disrupts carbon structures through functional groups, redox chemistry, vulcanisation-like crosslinking, and surface chemistry.

```text
sulfur modifies carbon routes at reactive boundaries.
```

### Carbon and Boron/Nitrogen/Oxygen/Hydrogen

Doping and surface chemistry can shift carbon material properties dramatically.

```text
doping and termination tune carbon topology without replacing the carbon route-field.
```

Hydrogen changes diamond-like carbon and amorphous carbon behaviour. Boron can make diamond electrically useful. Nitrogen and oxygen alter defects, surfaces, wettability, and electronic properties.
