# AMS Calcium Material Family Entry v1

## Purpose

This entry opens the calcium workstream in the AMS material-family corpus.

Calcium is a high-value element family because it connects:

- silicate glasses and minerals
- carbonate polymorphs and biomineralization
- phosphate minerals, hydroxyapatite, bone, and teeth
- biological signalling and membrane-adjacent regulation
- material interfaces, dissolution, and structural reinforcement

For AMS modelling, calcium is not just another divalent cation. It repeatedly acts as a bridge-former, stabiliser, modifier, and biological mineral-order carrier.

## Core AMS Claim

Calcium introduces bivalent relational constraint without the same high local field intensity as magnesium.

AMS reading:

```text
Ca2+
=> bridge-capable constraint
=> moderate-high coordination flexibility
=> stabilisation of extended mineral order
=> compatibility with biological templating
```

Compared with Mg:

```text
Mg2+
=> stronger local field, smaller radius, stronger gate pressure

Ca2+
=> broader bridge capacity, larger coordination accommodation, stronger scaffold compatibility
```

This makes calcium especially important in systems where order must be strong enough to persist but permissive enough to assemble biologically.

## Primary Material Roles

### 1. Calcium in Silicate Glasses

In silicate glasses, calcium commonly behaves as a network modifier or stabilising modifier.

AMS mapping:

```text
CaO in silicate glass
=> M3-like modifier constraint, but less sharply gated than Mg
=> R1/R2 balance
=> O1/O2 with reduced O5 relative to Na-only systems
```

Calcium does not simply loosen the network like sodium. It can compensate charge, reduce excessive mobility, and stabilise the structure while still modifying the silicate network.

Route profile:

```text
primary_route: R1/R2
secondary_route: R3 under high constraint or incompatible local composition
primary_obstruction: O1
secondary_obstruction: O2
dominance: D1/D4 depending on modifier mixture
```

In soda-lime glass, calcium is central because it prevents the sodium-modified silicate network from becoming too soluble or weak.

AMS reading:

```text
Na2O
=> M2 diffusion / weak route pressure

CaO
=> bridge-stabilising modifier pressure
=> reduces unrestricted M2 dominance
=> converts broad O5 weakness into more gated O1/O2 structure
```

### 2. Calcium Carbonate

Calcium carbonate is one of the major mineral forms in geological and biological systems.

Important forms:

```text
calcite
aragonite
vaterite
amorphous calcium carbonate
```

Source anchors report that calcium carbonate biominerals can occur as calcite, aragonite, vaterite, and amorphous phases, with biological systems using organic matter and templates to influence polymorph selection and morphology.

AMS mapping:

```text
CaCO3
=> calcium-carbonate closure field
=> polymorph-sensitive route grammar
=> biological templates steer closure pathway
```

Route profile:

```text
calcite
=> stable closure
=> R1/D1

aragonite
=> alternative ordered closure
=> R1 with different geometry

vaterite
=> metastable closure
=> R3/R5 risk depending on transition path

amorphous calcium carbonate
=> precursor / storage / transition field
=> D4 or D2 before crystalline lock-in
```

The important AMS point is that calcium carbonate is not one structure. The same chemical composition can settle into different topological and geometric closure states.

This makes CaCO3 a useful stress test for vorton closure:

```text
same bulk chemistry
different stable/metastable closure patterns
different route histories
different biological controls
```

### 3. Calcium Phosphate and Hydroxyapatite

Hydroxyapatite is a calcium phosphate mineral central to bone and teeth.

Source anchors report hydroxyapatite as a calcium hydroxide phosphate mineral and describe bone mineralization as deposition of calcium and phosphate, primarily as hydroxyapatite crystals, into a collagen matrix.

AMS mapping:

```text
calcium phosphate / hydroxyapatite
=> mineral closure compatible with biological matrix order
=> T1B mineral structure integrated into T1C tissue architecture
```

Route profile:

```text
primary_route: R1
secondary_route: R3 during defective or incomplete mineralization
primary_obstruction: O1
secondary_obstruction: O2/O3 under poor nucleation or pathological deposition
dominance: D1 when properly templated, D4 during formation/remodelling
```

Bone is not merely mineral plus collagen as a mixture. It is a higher-order composite where mineral crystals, collagen orientation, vesicle-mediated nucleation, and biological regulation form a structured T1C material.

AMS reading:

```text
collagen matrix
=> biological template / scaffold

calcium phosphate
=> mineral closure

osteoblast-mediated vesicle and phosphate regulation
=> controlled route admission

hydroxyapatite propagation
=> T1B mineral order entering T1C tissue function
```

This makes calcium phosphate one of the strongest bridges between material ontology and organism-level formation.

## Calcium as a Bridge-Constraint Carrier

Calcium's distinctive AMS profile:

```text
charge: +2
coordination: flexible enough for multiple mineral contexts
field strength: lower than Mg, higher constraint than monovalent alkalis
biological compatibility: high
mineral closure diversity: high
```

Calcium often creates order by bridging rather than by extreme local locking.

This gives it a characteristic role:

```text
stabilise without over-closing
bridge without fully freezing
permit templated assembly
support persistent composite order
```

## Comparison with Sodium and Magnesium

| Ion | AMS modifier tendency | Route tendency | Obstruction tendency | Typical role |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Na+ | loosening / mobile modifier | `R2` | `O5` | distributed weak route |
| Mg2+ | selective high-field constraint | `R1/R3` | `O1/O2` | local gate / strong interruption |
| Ca2+ | bridge-forming stabiliser | `R1/R2` | `O1/O2` | scaffold-compatible constraint |

Calcium sits between sodium and magnesium:

```text
less diffusive than Na
less sharply gated than Mg
more scaffold-compatible than either in many biological minerals
```

## Vorton Interpretation

In vorton terms, calcium-bearing systems often favour closure that is:

```text
bounded enough to persist
distributed enough to assemble into extended structures
compatible enough to be biologically templated
```

Calcium does not merely create a local knot. It often enables repeated closure across a scaffold.

Candidate vorton behavior:
