## Source Anchors

The source base supports these facts:

- Glycolysis occurs in the cytosol and converts glucose into pyruvate while producing ATP and NADH.
- Under aerobic conditions, pyruvate can enter mitochondria and be converted into acetyl-CoA.
- Under anaerobic or hypoxic conditions, pyruvate can be converted into lactate by lactate dehydrogenase, regenerating NAD+.
- The pyruvate dehydrogenase complex converts pyruvate into acetyl-CoA and CO2 and depends on multiple cofactors.
- Acetyl-CoA is a major carbon input into the citric acid cycle and can be derived from glucose and fatty acids.
- The citric acid cycle is a metabolic hub for oxidative catabolism of carbohydrates, amino acids, and fatty acids.
- Citric acid cycle oxidative steps reduce NAD+ and FAD to NADH and FADH2.
- NADH and FADH2 feed the electron transport chain, where redox reactions support proton-gradient formation and ATP synthesis.
- The citric acid cycle is also amphibolic: intermediates can leave the cycle for biosynthesis and can be replenished by anaplerotic routes.
- Complex I and Complex II of the electron transport chain receive electrons from NADH and FADH2 respectively; both involve metal and redox carriers, including iron-sulfur clusters.

## AMS Metabolic Principle

The metabolic principle:

```text
carbon skeletons carry route memory through transformation.
```

In metabolism, carbon molecules are not simply consumed. Their skeletons are redirected through a controlled sequence of changes. A six-carbon sugar becomes two three-carbon molecules. Pyruvate becomes acetyl-CoA. Acetyl-CoA enters a cyclic route. Carbon is released as CO2, electrons are transferred to NADH and FADH2, and remaining intermediates can be rebuilt, diverted, or replenished.

AMS interpretation:

```text
metabolism = governed carbon reconfiguration coupled to redox transfer
```

This distinguishes metabolism from undirected combustion. Combustion collapses carbon order into heat and simple oxidised products. Metabolism extracts energy by staged route conversion while preserving addressable intermediates.

## Route Map 1: Glycolysis as Carbon Splitting and Phosphate-Trapped Routing

Glycolysis begins with glucose and ends with pyruvate.

AMS route:

```text
glucose -> glucose-6-phosphate -> fructose-1,6-bisphosphate -> two triose phosphates -> pyruvate
```

The important point is not merely that glucose yields ATP. The route first traps and activates the sugar through phosphorylation. The carbon skeleton is then rearranged and split into two three-carbon routes. Oxidation of glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate reduces NAD+ to NADH, while later phosphate-transfer steps generate ATP.

Vorton interpretation:

```text
glucose-phosphate vorton = trapped intracellular carbon route
bisphosphate vorton = prepared split-state
triose vorton = duplicated three-carbon route
pyruvate vorton = mobile branch-point node
```

Route profile:

```text
route class: R1/R4
obstruction class: O1/O4
dominance: D1/D4
grade: G_A
```

Glycolysis is `R1` because its sequence is sharply conserved and route-specific. It is also `R4` because it acts as a branch-field: pyruvate can enter lactate fermentation, acetyl-CoA formation, alanine synthesis, oxaloacetate formation, or other context-specific routes.

Mechanism-facing statement:

```text
Glycolysis converts a six-carbon storage/uptake skeleton into two addressable three-carbon branch nodes while coupling carbon oxidation to NADH and ATP formation.
```

## Route Map 3: Pyruvate Dehydrogenase and Acetyl-CoA Entry

The pyruvate dehydrogenase complex links glycolysis to the citric acid cycle.

AMS route:


This route converts a three-carbon pyruvate into a two-carbon acetyl carrier. It releases one carbon as CO2 and captures reducing power as NADH. It also uses cofactor choreography: thiamine pyrophosphate, lipoic acid, CoA, FAD, and NAD+.

## Route Map 4: Citric Acid Cycle as Carbon Oxidation and Route Hub

The citric acid cycle oxidises acetyl carbon while generating reduced electron carriers and maintaining biosynthetic intermediates.

AMS route:


Carbon enters as acetyl-CoA, joins oxaloacetate, passes through rearrangement and oxidation, releases CO2, and reduces NAD+ and FAD. The cycle is not merely circular repetition. It is a controlled hub where carbon oxidation, electron extraction, and biosynthetic branching coexist.

Vorton interpretation:

```text
The citric acid cycle is a carbon-route turntable: it oxidises, redirects, restores, and exports carbon skeletons under redox control.
```

Vorton interpretation:

## Route Map 6: Fermentation as Redox Recovery Without Full Oxidation

Fermentation is often treated as inferior respiration. AMS should be more precise.

Fermentation is a redox-balancing route when full respiratory closure is unavailable.

AMS route:

```text

```

The point is not maximum ATP. The point is NAD+ regeneration so glycolysis can continue. Fermentation preserves route continuity under oxygen limitation or mitochondrial absence.

Vorton interpretation:

The route is not globally maximal, but it is locally coherent. In erythrocytes, hypoxic tissue, and intense muscular demand, this route has real functional value.

## Cross-Element Implications

### Carbon and Phosphorus

Phosphorus traps, activates, and transfers metabolic order.

```text
carbon skeleton + phosphate activation = addressable metabolic intermediate
```

Glycolysis makes this obvious. Glucose is not simply degraded. It is phosphorylated, retained, rearranged, split, and harvested.

### Carbon and Iron

Iron carries redox depth in heme, Fe-S systems, and respiratory complexes.

```text
carbon provides reducing equivalents.
iron routes redox depth.
```

Without iron-governed redox routing, carbon oxidation cannot be efficiently coupled into respiratory ATP production.

### Carbon and Sulfur

Sulfur contributes CoA chemistry, lipoamide chemistry, cysteine-dependent enzyme structures, Fe-S clusters, and redox buffering.

```text
sulfur makes carbon metabolism both transferable and repairable.
```

CoA thioesters, lipoic-acid relay chemistry, glutathione, thioredoxin, and Fe-S clusters all place sulfur near carbon's metabolic routes.

### Carbon and Oxygen

Oxygen closes high-yield aerobic respiration by accepting electrons.

```text
oxygen allows deeper carbon energy extraction but increases oxidative-damage risk.
```

Oxygen therefore plays a dual role: route closer and damage amplifier.

### Carbon and Nitrogen

Nitrogen joins carbon skeletons in amino acids, nucleotides, cofactors, and regulatory chemistry.

```text
carbon skeletons become biological meaning-bearing molecules when nitrogen is routed into them.
```

This prepares the later nitrogen family analysis.
