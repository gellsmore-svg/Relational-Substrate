# AMS Sulfate Extracellular Matrix Charge Field Map v1

## Purpose

This note maps sulfur's `S6` role:

```text
S6: extracellular sulfate charge-field contributor
```

The previous sulfur maps established sulfur as:

- disulfide fold-lock chemistry;
- iron-sulfur electron-route chemistry;
- thiol redox-buffer and signalling chemistry.

This note moves sulfur outside the cell into the extracellular matrix, glycocalyx, basement membrane, cartilage, developmental fields, and tissue signalling spaces.

The central claim is:

> Sulfated extracellular chains turn tissue space into a charged route field: they bind water, resist compression, retain ligands, tune morphogen gradients, present growth factors, and govern movement through the extracellular environment.

## Source Anchors

The source base is strong:

- Proteoglycans consist of core proteins with covalently attached glycosaminoglycan chains.
- Major sulfated glycosaminoglycans include heparan sulfate, chondroitin sulfate, dermatan sulfate, keratan sulfate, and heparin.
- Proteoglycans are found in extracellular matrix, cell surfaces, basement membranes, secretory granules, cartilage, and the glycocalyx.
- Sulfated GAG chains carry high negative charge density.
- Interstitial proteoglycans containing chondroitin sulfate and dermatan sulfate bind water and form hydrated matrices.
- Cartilage aggrecan bears many chondroitin sulfate and keratan sulfate chains and forms large aggregates with hyaluronan and link protein.
- Aggrecan's negative charge density attracts counter-ions, generates osmotic swelling pressure, and supports cartilage hydration and load-bearing capacity.
- Heparan sulfate proteoglycans bind many protein ligands and can serve as reservoirs, co-receptors, endocytic receptors, and regulators of morphogen/growth-factor distribution.
- HSPGs help regulate morphogen movement, signalling, diffusion, trafficking, and gradient formation.
- Fgf8 in zebrafish embryos diffuses through extracellular space and interacts reversibly with heparan sulfate proteoglycans, tuning gradient formation and receptor binding.
- HSPGs can either confine ligand range or support short-range ligand mobility depending on context.

## AMS Role Assignment

Sulfur role:

```text
S6: extracellular sulfate charge-field contributor
```

Associated sulfur roles:

- `S5`: redox-state control, because sulfate is an oxidised sulfur endpoint integrated into biological structure;
- `S8`: boundary chemistry, because sulfated matrices regulate what may cross, bind, diffuse, or signal;
- `S7`: mineral/extracellular bridge, because sulfate chemistry also links biological matrices with mineral and ionic environments.

Core distinction:

```text
thiols govern internal chemical state
sulfates govern extracellular charged space
```

Sulfated GAGs do not mainly act by rapid redox switching. They act by persistent charged patterning.

## Mechanism 1: Hydrated Compression Field

Primary example:

```text
aggrecan + chondroitin sulfate + keratan sulfate + hyaluronan + collagen matrix
```

Cartilage proteoglycans form large hydrated assemblies. Sulfated GAG chains create fixed negative charge. That charge attracts counter-ions and water. The collagen network restrains the swelling pressure, converting charge and water retention into load-bearing tissue mechanics.

AMS route:

```text
sulfated GAG charge
-> counter-ion/water attraction
-> osmotic swelling pressure
-> collagen-contained hydrated matrix
-> compressive load resistance
```

Classification:

```text
R1/O1/D1/G_A
```

Why:

This is a strongly real route. Sulfation produces a predictable tissue-scale physical effect: hydration and compressive resistance.

AMS variables:

```text
B_in    = mechanical load / hydration demand
Q_coh   = matrix coherence: GAG density, aggregate integrity, collagen containment
J_bound = water and ion retention inside matrix
J_trans = load redistribution through hydrated matrix
B_out   = tissue deformation/recovery route
I_comp  = degradation, inflammation, enzymatic cleavage, dehydration
D       = D1 when coherent
```

Failure route:

```text
aggrecan cleavage / GAG loss / matrix degradation
-> charge-density reduction
-> water-retention loss
-> weakened compression route
-> cartilage erosion or mechanical failure
```

Failure classification:

```text
R3/R6/O2/O6/D4/G_C
```

## Mechanism 2: Ligand Reservoir and Presentation Field

Primary example:

```text
heparan sulfate proteoglycans + growth factors / cytokines / chemokines / morphogens
```

HSPGs bind many protein ligands through sulfated chain patterns. These interactions can protect ligands, hold them near cells, present them to receptors, restrict their range, or tune signalling duration.

AMS route:

```text
secreted ligand
-> reversible binding to sulfated chain
-> local reservoir / co-receptor presentation
-> receptor engagement or controlled release
```

Classification:

```text
R4/O4/D4/G_A
```

Why:

This is a conditional route. The ligand is neither fully free nor permanently trapped. It is held in a governed extracellular state.

AMS variables:

```text
B_in    = ligand arrival
Q_coh   = sulfation-pattern coherence and receptor-context coherence
J_bound = ligand retained on HSPG/ECM chain
J_trans = ligand transfer to receptor or neighbouring surface
B_out   = signalling output / further diffusion
I_comp  = proteolysis, nonspecific binding, heparanase cleavage, wrong sulfation pattern
D       = D4
```

This makes sulfated extracellular chains route editors rather than passive matrix filler.

## Mechanism 3: Morphogen Gradient Tuning

Primary example:

```text
Fgf8 + heparan sulfate proteoglycans in embryonic extracellular matrix
```

Morphogens must move through tissue while retaining enough spatial order to specify cell fate. Pure free diffusion can be too uncontrolled. Permanent binding would prevent range. HSPGs provide reversible interaction points that tune distribution and receptor engagement.

AMS route:

```text
morphogen source
-> extracellular diffusion
-> reversible HSPG binding
-> tuned local concentration field
-> receptor encounter
-> positional cell response
```

Classification:

```text
R2/R4/O4/D4/G_A
```

Why:

The route is distributed, but not random. HSPG interaction supplies weak route texture across tissue space.

Gradient failure modes:

| Failure | AMS classification | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Too little binding | `R2/O5/D3/G_C` | ligand spreads too freely; gradient loses local control |
| Too much binding | `R5/O3/D2/G_C` | ligand becomes trapped; range collapses |
| Wrong sulfation pattern | `R3/O2/D4/G_C` | apparent route exists but signalling specificity fails |
| Excess degradation | `R6/O6/D4/G_C` | route field fragments |

This supports a precise AMS claim:

> Sulfated extracellular chains convert diffusion into guided tissue-scale signalling.

## Mechanism 4: Boundary and Permeability Field

Proteoglycans organise basement membranes, glycocalyx layers, and extracellular spaces. They regulate permeability, cell adhesion, migration, and interaction with collagen, laminin, fibronectin, integrins, and growth-factor receptors.

AMS route:

```text
cell/tissue boundary
-> sulfated polymer field
-> selective retention, exclusion, adhesion, or migration
-> tissue-level boundary behaviour
```

Classification:

```text
R1/R4/O1/O4/D1/D4/G_B/G_A
```

Why:

The role is not a single route but a boundary field. It may stabilise tissue architecture or regulate transient passage.

## Vorton Interpretation

Sulfated extracellular matrices produce extended charge-field vortons.

These differ from:

- disulfide vortons, which are covalent topology locks;
- Fe-S vortons, which are compact mineral redox nodes;
- thiol-redox vortons, which are transient state-control closures.

Sulfated ECM vortons are distributed fields:

```text
fixed sulfate charges
-> polymer extension
-> counter-ion/water shell
-> ligand-binding topology
-> tissue-scale route field
```

They are not point-like. They form persistent charged surfaces and hydrated regions that shape how matter, water, ions, proteins, and cells move through extracellular space.

Viability conditions:

1. Sulfate pattern must be dense enough to bind water/ions or ligands.
2. Polymer chains must be extended enough to create an extracellular field.
3. Core proteins must localise chains correctly.
4. The matrix must restrain swelling where load-bearing is required.
5. Binding must remain reversible where signalling gradients are required.
6. Degradation/remodelling must be governed rather than fragmenting the field.

## Testable Expectations

If the AMS sulfate-ECM model is coherent, the following should hold:

1. Loss of sulfated GAG charge should reduce water retention, osmotic swelling pressure, and compressive resistance in relevant tissues.
2. Cartilage mechanical failure should correlate with aggrecan/GAG depletion and collagen containment breakdown.
3. HSPG composition and sulfation pattern should tune growth-factor and morphogen range.
4. Reversible ligand binding should support stronger route control than either pure diffusion or irreversible trapping.
5. Basement membrane and glycocalyx defects should alter permeability, adhesion, migration, or signalling thresholds.
6. Excess degradation of sulfated chains should fragment extracellular route fields.
7. Oversulfation, undersulfation, or wrong sulfation pattern should misroute ligand interactions even when proteoglycan mass remains present.

These expectations match the source literature strongly.
