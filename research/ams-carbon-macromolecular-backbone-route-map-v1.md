## Source Anchors

The source base supports these facts:

- The four major classes of biological macromolecules are carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids.
- Biological macromolecules are large molecules necessary for life and are built from smaller organic molecules.
- Biological macromolecules contain carbon, usually along with hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, phosphorus, sulfur, and other minor elements.
- Carbohydrates include monosaccharides, disaccharides, and polysaccharides; they provide energy and structural support.
- Cellulose is a major plant structural polysaccharide.
- Lipids include fats, oils, waxes, phospholipids, and steroids; many are hydrophobic because of nonpolar C-C and C-H bonds.
- Phospholipids are major membrane components.
- Proteins are composed of amino-acid monomers linked into polypeptides; protein shape and function are closely linked.
- Nucleic acids are polymers of nucleotides and carry genetic information.
- Nucleotides contain a pentose sugar, phosphate group, and nitrogenous base.
- Carbohydrates, proteins, and nucleic acids are polymers built by covalently linking monomers; lipids are not generally polymers but still depend on carbon-rich architecture.

## Mechanism 1: Protein Carbon Backbone

Proteins are polymers of amino acids. Carbon supplies the central amino-acid skeleton, the carbonyl carbon in peptide bonds, and much of the side-chain structural variation.

AMS route:

```text
amino-acid carbon skeleton
-> peptide linkage
-> polypeptide chain
-> fold geometry
-> functional protein
```

Classification:

```text
R1/R4/O1/O4/D1/D4/G_A
```

Why:

The route is strongly real and sequence-dependent. Carbon skeleton continuity permits chain formation; folding turns chain into higher-order function.

AMS variables:

```text
B_in    = amino-acid monomer availability and sequence order
Q_coh   = backbone integrity, stereochemistry, peptide linkage, folding conditions
J_bound = monomers retained in covalent chain
J_trans = folding and functional route formation
B_out   = protein structure/function
I_comp  = misfolding, wrong sequence, racemisation, cleavage, denaturation
D       = D1/D4
```

AMS reading:

```text
carbon frames the protein route; sequence and fold make it functional.
```

Failure:

```text
wrong sequence / wrong stereochemistry / misfolding -> apparent chain but wrong function
```

Classification:

```text
R3/R5/O2/O3/D4/D2/G_C
```

## Mechanism 2: Nucleic-Acid Carbon Backbone Contribution

Nucleic acids depend on carbon in two places:

- pentose sugar rings;
- nitrogenous base skeletons.

Phosphorus supplies the phosphate backbone link, but carbon supplies the sugar frame and base architecture.

AMS route:

```text
pentose sugar + phosphate + base
-> nucleotide
-> polymer
-> sequence-bearing nucleic acid
```

Classification:

```text
R1/R4/O1/O4/D1/D4/G_A
```

Why:

Carbon frames the nucleotide's geometry. Phosphorus supplies repeatable charged linkage. Nitrogenous bases carry symbolic difference through carbon-nitrogen ring architectures.

AMS reading:

```text
carbon gives nucleic acids their sugar/base body;
phosphorus gives them repeatable charged linkage.
```

This is one of the key cross-element integrations:

```text
carbon architecture + phosphorus transfer order + nitrogen base difference = genetic polymer route
```

Failure:

```text
wrong sugar, wrong base, damaged carbon ring, backbone cleavage, base oxidation
```

Classification:

```text
R3/R6/O2/O6/D4/G_C
```

## Mechanism 3: Carbohydrate and Polysaccharide Backbones

Carbohydrates use carbon ring and chain skeletons patterned with oxygen/hydrogen groups.

AMS route:

```text
monosaccharide carbon skeleton
-> glycosidic linkage
-> disaccharide / polysaccharide
-> storage, recognition, or structural function
```

Classification:

```text
R1/R4/O1/O4/D1/D4/G_A
```

Why:

Sugar monomers are not interchangeable blobs. Their carbon ring geometry and hydroxyl patterning determine polymer behaviour.

Examples:

```text
starch/glycogen = storage route
cellulose = plant structural route
chitin = structural route with nitrogen-bearing modification
```

AMS reading:

```text
carbon sugar frames can become energy stores, structural fibres, or recognition fields.
```

Failure:

```text
wrong linkage or stereochemistry -> different polymer properties
```

Classification:

```text
R3/O2/D4/G_C
```

This is a strong demonstration that connectivity and stereochemistry matter at polymer scale.

## Mechanism 4: Lipid Carbon Architecture

Lipids are not usually monomer-repetition polymers like proteins or nucleic acids, but carbon is still central. Fatty acids, triglycerides, phospholipids, waxes, and steroids depend on carbon-rich hydrophobic frames.

AMS route:

```text
carbon-rich hydrophobic skeleton
-> lipid assembly
-> energy storage / membrane body / signalling scaffold
```

Classification:

```text
R1/R4/O1/O4/D1/D4/G_A
```

Why:

Carbon-hydrogen and carbon-carbon bonding gives lipids their nonpolar character. This makes them boundary-forming, energy-dense, and water-excluding.

AMS reading:

```text
carbon supplies the hydrophobic body of lipid architecture.
```

Membrane implication:

```text
carbon chains create nonpolar interior;
phosphorus headgroups create charged interface;
water drives assembly.
```

Failure:

```text
carbon-chain oxidation / lipid peroxidation -> membrane route collapse
```

Classification:

```text
R6/O6/D4/G_D
```

This links directly to iron `I6` ferroptosis.

## Cross-Element Comparison

### Carbon and Phosphorus

Nucleic acids and phospholipids show the contrast:

```text
carbon supplies the body;
phosphorus supplies repeatable charged linkage or headgroup order.
```

In DNA/RNA:

```text
carbon sugar/base architecture + phosphate backbone = genetic polymer.
```

### Carbon and Sulfur

Proteins show the contrast:

```text
carbon frames the amino-acid chain;
sulfur adds fold locks, thiol switches, and redox gates.
```

### Carbon and Iron

Ferroptosis shows the contrast:

```text
carbon lipid chains form membrane boundary;
iron-driven redox leakage destroys them.
```

Heme shows cooperation:

```text
carbon porphyrin architecture embeds iron redox depth.
```

### Carbon and Calcium

Structural tissues show the contrast:

```text
carbon polymers form organic matrix;
calcium minerals add hard bridge-forming constraint.
```

### Carbon and Silicon

Carbon polymers are flexible, variable, and sequence-bearing.

Silicon networks are stable, extended, and less biologically reconfigurable.

```text
carbon = variable molecular architecture
silicon = extended inorganic network closure
```

## Testable Expectations

If the AMS `C3` model is coherent, the following should hold:

1. Carbon-containing backbone continuity should be essential to protein, nucleic-acid, carbohydrate, and lipid identity.
2. Macromolecular function should depend on linkage order, not monomer inventory alone.
3. Wrong stereochemistry should disrupt biological polymer fit.
4. Protein function should depend on carbon-framed sequence folding.
5. Nucleic-acid continuity should require carbon sugar/base architecture plus phosphate linkage.
6. Polysaccharide properties should change with linkage geometry and branching.
7. Lipid boundary function should depend on carbon-chain hydrophobicity and oxidation state.
8. Carbon skeleton damage should propagate into higher-order biological dysfunction.

These expectations align strongly with the source literature.
